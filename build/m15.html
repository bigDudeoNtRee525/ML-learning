Module 15

Practical Workflow, Deployment, Ethics & Next Steps

You can train models now. Linear regression, trees, neural nets, transformers — all of it. So here is the uncomfortable truth: the math was the easy 10%. The other 90% — the part that decides whether you ship something useful or quietly ship garbage — nobody taught you yet. This module is that 90%. It is also your map out of tutorial hell, for good.

01 · the problem, before the lesson

Your model is 90% accurate. Now what?

You trained a cat-vs-dog classifier. 200 validation images, 180 right, 20 wrong. Your instinct is screaming one of these at you. Pick the one you'd actually do tomorrow morning — before you read another word.

90% accuracy, 20 mistakes. What do you do to get better?

A, B, and C are all guesses dressed up as work. You can burn a week on any of them and have no idea if it helped — because you never found out why the model fails. D costs ten minutes and tells you which of A/B/C is even worth doing. The people who get better at ML are the ones who read their errors. The people who spin in circles tweak knobs blind. This module is mostly about being the first kind.

A true story to keep you honest: in 2018 a large company killed a résumé-screening model it had spent years building. Trained on a decade of hires that were mostly men, it taught itself that the word "women's" (as in "women's chess club captain") was a negative signal. Nobody coded that. The accuracy looked fine. The failure was in everything around the math — framing, data, fairness, monitoring — and nobody looked.

02 · the shape of a real project

It's a loop, not a recipe

Every module so far taught one technique. A real project is not one technique — it's a loop you walk many times, and almost all the value is in walking it well, not in any one fancy step.

1. Frame  →  2. Get & clean data  →  3. Baseline  →  4. Model
        ↑___________________________________________ ↓
    6. Iterate  ←——————————  5. Evaluate + error analysis

1–4 the obvious part everyone does. 5–6 (evaluate + iterate) where beginners lose the most ground — they jump straight back to step 4 and tweak knobs blindly instead of reading the mistakes from step 5.

Walk the six the way a pro thinks about them:

1. Frame. Before any code: what decision does this inform, and how will we know it's good? Pick one number — your primary metric — and write down the bar for "good enough to ship" before you start, so you can't fool yourself later. A spam filter that's 99% accurate sounds great until you notice 99% of email is spam, so "flag everything" also scores 99%. (You met that trap in Module 5.)

2. Get & clean data. Usually the biggest chunk of real work. "Garbage in, garbage out" isn't a slogan, it's the #1 reason projects fail. This is the Module 2 and Module 7 skill set: missing values, wrong labels, leakage, knowing how the data was collected.

3. Baseline. Build the dumbest thing that produces a prediction, and measure it. Regression → predict the mean. Classification → predict the majority class. A baseline tells you (a) whether the problem is even learnable, and (b) a number every fancy model must beat.

The plain-pasta rule. Building a model with no baseline is like inventing an elaborate sauce before tasting plain pasta. Maybe the pasta was already great and your sauce makes it worse — but you'd never know, because you never tasted the simple version. The baseline is the plain pasta. Every fancy model must beat it or it hasn't earned its complexity. Pros taste the pasta first, every time.

4. Model. Now — and only now — reach for the strong stuff: Module 8 algorithms, the neural nets of Modules 10–13. Start simple; add complexity only when the data justifies it.

5. Evaluate + error analysis. Compute your metric on held-out data (Module 6) — then don't stop at the number. Pull the wrong examples and read them. This one habit separates people who improve their models from people who spin.

6. Iterate. Error analysis points to the next fix. Change one thing, walk the loop again, compare to your previous best. Repeat.

03 · earn the diagnosis

More data, or a bigger model? Stop guessing.

Back to the cold-open card. A and B were both wrong because they're opposite cures for opposite diseases — and you can't pick the cure until you've named the disease. Here's the diagnostic, and it's two subtractions.

Compare three error rates: what a human (or known ceiling) achieves, your error on the training set, and your error on the validation set. Two gaps tell you everything:

\[ \text{avoidable bias} = E_{\text{train}} - E_{\text{human}}, \qquad \text{variance} = E_{\text{val}} - E_{\text{train}} \]

E_human error a human / known ceiling reaches. E_train error on data the model already saw. E_val error on fresh held-out data. Each E is a fraction wrong, so smaller is better.

In plain English: avoidable bias is how far you are from achievable even on data you've already seen — big means the model is too weak (underfitting). Variance is how much worse you do on new data than on training data — big means the model memorized the training set (overfitting).

Make the call before I tell you the rule:

Human error 1%, training error 2%, validation error 13%. What's broken, and what fixes it?

Avoidable bias \(= 2\% - 1\% = 1\%\) (tiny — the model fits what it's seen). Variance \(= 13\% - 2\% = 11\%\) (huge — it falls apart on new data). Textbook overfitting. The reliable cure is more data, plus regularization or a simpler model. Making it bigger (answer A) would overfit even harder — the exact wrong move, which is why "bigger model" in the cold open was a coin flip.

The two gaps turn "more data or bigger model?" from taste into arithmetic:

  • High avoidable bias (training error itself too high) → make the model stronger: bigger network, more features, less regularization, train longer. More data will not help — the model can't even fit what it has.
  • High variance (big train→val gap) → more data (the most reliable cure for overfitting), more regularization, or a simpler model. A bigger model makes this worse.

Collecting 100,000 images when your problem is bias is wasted months. A giant model when your problem is variance just overfits faster.

04 · the highest-leverage ten minutes in ML

Error analysis is just structured counting

Take the examples your model got wrong, assign each a cause, count them, and rank by how much fixing that cause could buy you. The key number:

\[ \text{potential gain} = \frac{\text{errors in that category}}{\text{total examples evaluated}} \]

numerator how many mistakes that one cause is responsible for. denominator the size of the sample you scored. The result caps the most accuracy you could ever recover by fixing that one bucket.

If 50% of your errors are blurry images but blurry images are only 5% of your data, fixing blur lifts overall accuracy by at most a few points. The fraction caps your upside before you waste a week on it.

LAB read the tally, make the calls

You trained a cat/dog classifier: 200 validation images, 20 wrong (90% accuracy). You opened all 20 and tagged each with its most likely cause. Here's the sheet. Don't open the verdict — do the three missions yourself first.

Show the worked reasoning

Step 1 — current state. Error \(= 20/200 = 0.10\), so accuracy 90%. Ceiling is 100%, so there are 10 points on the table, total.

Step 2 — the mislabeled bucket is a free lesson. 4 of the 20 "errors" aren't errors — the dataset's label is wrong and the model was right. So true accuracy is already \((180+4)/200 = 92\%\), and at least \(4/200 = 2\%\) of your labels are noisy. Cleaning labels raises both your measured score and the ceiling everything else is judged against. Always check mislabeling first.

Step 3 — rank the real fixes. Blurry: \(9/200 = 4.5\%\) max gain. Occluded: \(5/200 = 2.5\%\). Unusual breed: \(2/200 = 1.0\%\). Even eliminating every blurry miss buys at most 4.5 points.

Step 4 — decide. (a) Fix the 4 mislabels and spot-check the rest — bad labels poison training and evaluation. (b) Then attack blur (largest gain, 4.5 pts) via blur augmentation, see Module 12. (c) Ignore "unusual breed": a week there buys at most 1 point. All of that from a tally of 20 images in ten minutes, no retraining. That's the leverage.

05 · automate the tally

From scratch: the error-analysis routine

The by-hand process above, in dependency-free Python. You give it the true labels, the predictions, and a function that names a cause for each example; it returns the mistakes and a tally sorted biggest-bucket-first.

from collections import Counter def error_analysis(X, y_true, y_pred, cause_fn): """Collect misclassified examples and tally them by cause. X : list of inputs (e.g. dicts describing each image) y_true : list of correct labels y_pred : list of model predictions cause_fn : function(x) -> str, your guess at why x is hard """ mistakes = [] causes = Counter() for x, yt, yp in zip(X, y_true, y_pred): if yt != yp: # only look at the errors cause = cause_fn(x) mistakes.append((x, yt, yp, cause)) causes[cause] += 1 total = len(y_true) print(f"{len(mistakes)} errors out of {total} " f"({len(mistakes)/total:.1%} error rate)\n") print("Cause count share of all data") for cause, n in causes.most_common(): # biggest bucket first print(f"{cause:<22}{n:>5} {n/total:>10.1%}") return mistakes, causes # --- a small synthetic example you can run as-is --- # Each image is a dict of attributes; the "model" got some wrong. X = [{"id": i, "blurry": i % 5 == 0, "occluded": i % 7 == 0} for i in range(200)] y_true = ["cat"] * 200 # Pretend the model misses exactly the blurry-or-occluded ones. y_pred = ["dog" if (x["blurry"] or x["occluded"]) else "cat" for x in X] def cause_fn(x): if x["blurry"]: return "blurry" if x["occluded"]: return "occluded" return "other" mistakes, causes = error_analysis(X, y_true, y_pred, cause_fn)

Predict the print-out before you run it. Every 5th image is blurry (40 of them). Every 7th is occluded (29), but 6 of those are also blurry (every 35th), so cause_fn's if/elif counts them as blurry — leaving 23 "occluded". So you'll see 63 errors out of 200 (31.5% error rate), blurry largest at 40 (= 20.0% of all data) and occluded second at 23 (= 11.5%). The ordering and the "share of all data" column hand you the same verdict as the by-hand sheet — now reproducible on thousands of examples.

06 · find the bug

This evaluation looks fine. It's lying to you.

A beginner wrote this to "check if the model is good." It runs clean, prints a great number, and the model is worthless. The bug isn't a typo or a crash — it's a thinking error. Find it before you open the answer.

from sklearn.tree import DecisionTreeClassifier # fraud detection: 0.8% of transactions are fraud (label 1) model = DecisionTreeClassifier() model.fit(X_train, y_train) preds = model.predict(X_test) accuracy = (preds == y_test).mean() # <-- the wrong question entirely print(f"Accuracy: {accuracy:.1%}") # prints 99.2% -> "ship it!" # (no baseline computed, no precision, no recall)
Show the bug & why

Two linked sins, and neither is a syntax error. (1) Wrong metric. Fraud is 0.8% of transactions, so a model that predicts "never fraud" for everything scores \(1 - 0.008 = 99.2\%\) accuracy — identical to this one. The 99.2% measures nothing. (2) No baseline. Nobody computed "always predict the majority class," so there's no way to see that the fancy tree didn't beat the dumbest possible guess. The fix: report precision and recall (or PR-AUC) against an explicit majority-class baseline, because the real costs are missing fraud (false negative) and annoying honest customers (false positive) — and accuracy is blind to both on imbalanced data. Pick the metric that matches the cost of each error before you train.

07 · ship it

Save the model, then put it behind a link

Once a model is good enough you need two things: save & reload it (so you don't retrain every time) and let other people use it. For scikit-learn, joblib saves; for PyTorch, torch.save stores the weights. Then Gradio turns any Python function into a web page in a few lines.

import joblib from sklearn.datasets import load_iris from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestClassifier # 1) Train a quick model (you'd use your real, evaluated model here). data = load_iris() clf = RandomForestClassifier(n_estimators=100, random_state=0) clf.fit(data.data, data.target) # 2) SAVE it to disk so you never have to retrain to use it. joblib.dump(clf, "iris_model.joblib") # 3) LOAD it back (e.g. in a separate app file or after a restart). clf = joblib.load("iris_model.joblib") print(clf.predict([[5.1, 3.5, 1.4, 0.2]])) # -> array([0]) (setosa)

Trains an iris classifier, writes it to iris_model.joblib, loads it back, predicts on one flower — printing [0], the "setosa" class. This split — save in one program, load in another — is exactly what lets your training script and your serving app be two separate things.

Now wrap the loaded model in a web UI. gr.Interface takes a function, a list of input widgets, and an output widget, and builds the whole page — boxes, a Submit button, a result area — automatically.

import gradio as gr import joblib clf = joblib.load("iris_model.joblib") names = ["setosa", "versicolor", "virginica"] def classify(sepal_len, sepal_wid, petal_len, petal_wid): features = [[sepal_len, sepal_wid, petal_len, petal_wid]] probs = clf.predict_proba(features)[0] # one prob per class return {names[i]: float(probs[i]) for i in range(3)} demo = gr.Interface( fn=classify, inputs=[ gr.Number(label="sepal length (cm)", value=5.1), gr.Number(label="sepal width (cm)", value=3.5), gr.Number(label="petal length (cm)", value=1.4), gr.Number(label="petal width (cm)", value=0.2), ], outputs=gr.Label(num_top_classes=3), title="Iris classifier", description="Enter four measurements; see the predicted species.", ) if __name__ == "__main__": demo.launch() # local web page; share=True gives a public link

Run with python app.py and Gradio prints a local URL (like http://127.0.0.1:7860). Open it: four number boxes, a Submit button, a bar chart of class probabilities — built from a function and a list. Pass share=True to launch() for a temporary public link you can text to a friend. This is the fastest path from "a model on my laptop" to "something a human can actually try."

LAB from notebook to public link

No widget here — your laptop is the lab. Take any model you trained in an earlier module and ship it. Tick these as you go.

  • Save a trained model to disk, restart Python, load it back, and predict joblib.dump / joblib.load, or torch.save / load_state_dict
  • Wrap it in a Gradio Interface and open the local URL match the input widgets to your real features
  • Get a public link and send it to one person who isn't you launch(share=True)
Gradio for humans, FastAPI for software. Gradio is perfect for demos and for letting non-programmers poke at your model. But when another program needs predictions — a mobile app, a website backend, a scheduled job — you want plain JSON, not a web UI. That's FastAPI: a function decorated with @app.post("/predict") that takes JSON in and returns JSON out, serving many requests efficiently. Rule of thumb: Gradio for humans to click, FastAPI for software to call. Plenty of teams ship both.
08 · the part that gets people hurt

Average accuracy is a liar

A model that's accurate on average can be systematically wrong for one group of people — and that's how ML causes real harm. A loan model with 92% overall accuracy might be 95% for one group and 80% for another. The average hides the gap. The fix is a discipline, not a button: check your metrics separately for each subgroup, not just overall.

Three concrete fairness questions you can ask of almost any classifier, given a sensitive attribute (gender, race, age group) you must not discriminate on:

  • Demographic parity: does the model approve each group at similar rates? Compare \( P(\hat{y}=1 \mid \text{group A}) \) with \( P(\hat{y}=1 \mid \text{group B}) \), where \( \hat{y}=1 \) means "approved." Big gaps deserve scrutiny.
  • Equal opportunity: among people who actually deserve approval, does the model approve each group at similar rates? Compares the true positive rate across groups — often the fairer question: of the qualified people, are we treating groups equally?
  • Calibration: when the model says "70% likely," is that true for every group? If "70%" means 70% for one group but 50% for another, the scores mean different things for different people.

These can conflict — you usually can't satisfy all three at once, which is itself the point: fairness is explicit trade-offs and value choices, not a single button. Here's a minimal subgroup audit you can run on any predictions.

import numpy as np def subgroup_audit(y_true, y_pred, group): """Print accuracy and positive-prediction rate per group.""" y_true, y_pred, group = map(np.array, (y_true, y_pred, group)) print(f"{'group':<10}{'n':>6}{'accuracy':>11}{'approval rate':>16}") for g in np.unique(group): m = group == g acc = (y_pred[m] == y_true[m]).mean() approval = (y_pred[m] == 1).mean() # rate of positive class print(f"{str(g):<10}{m.sum():>6}{acc:>11.1%}{approval:>16.1%}") # Toy data: group B is approved far less often than group A. y_true = [1,1,0,1, 1,0,0,0] y_pred = [1,1,0,1, 0,0,0,0] group = ["A","A","A","A", "B","B","B","B"] subgroup_audit(y_true, y_pred, group)

Group A is approved 75% of the time, group B 0% — a glaring disparity you'd never see from overall accuracy alone (which is 87.5%). Running this breakdown before you ship is the single most practical fairness habit. Real audits use libraries like fairlearn, but the idea is exactly this loop: slice by group, compare the metrics, investigate gaps.

Harm is concrete, not abstract. Documented ML failures: résumé screeners that downranked women; healthcare risk scores that gave Black patients lower priority for the same illness (the model used past spending as a proxy for need, and less had historically been spent on them); facial recognition with far higher error rates on darker-skinned faces; recidivism scores that flagged Black defendants high-risk more often. The common thread every time: the data encoded historical inequity, no one audited subgroups, and the system shipped anyway. Auditing isn't optional polish — it's part of building the thing correctly.

10 · the boss fight

Stop following. Start building.

Tutorial hell is the comfortable trap of always following someone else's steps. You feel productive, but you're building their project, not the skill of starting from a blank file. The cure is uncomfortable on purpose: pick a question you care about, find or scrape your own messy data, and walk this module's loop with no tutorial holding your hand. Getting stuck and unsticking yourself is the learning — and it's exactly where a Claude Code companion shines as a pair-programmer instead of a lecturer.

🤖 Boss fight — with your Claude companion

This is the final boss of the whole course: an independent project, mentored not narrated. Don't paste a tutorial. Paste this, then drive.

Be my ML project mentor, not a tutorial. I want to ship one real project end-to-end and I do the work. Step 1: ask me what question I actually care about, then help me pick a concrete prediction target and ONE primary metric, and make me write down the "good enough to ship" bar before any code. If you want a target, in rough order of difficulty:

  • Predict something local: house prices in your city, or whether a tweet on a topic is positive — find data, build a baseline, beat it, deploy a Gradio demo.
  • Reproduce then twist: take a Kaggle competition, get a baseline submission in, then change one thing and measure whether it helped.
  • Fine-tune a small model: a pretrained model from Hugging Face on your own small labeled dataset (ties back to Module 14).
  • Ship it end-to-end: data → model → saved artifact → Gradio app with a public link → a README with your error analysis and fairness check. This is the one to put in front of an employer.

Train on these before you go

  1. A fraud detector achieves 99.2% accuracy; fraud is 0.8% of transactions. Is it good? What metric should you report, and what baseline also scores ~99%?
    Show solution

    Almost certainly useless. "Never fraud" scores \(1 - 0.008 = 99.2\%\) too — that's the baseline this model hasn't beaten. Accuracy is the wrong metric for such imbalance. Report precision and recall (or PR-AUC), because the real costs are missing fraud (false negative) and annoying honest customers (false positive). You'd want high recall on fraud while keeping false positives tolerable. See Modules 5 and 6.

  2. Human error 1%, training error 2%, validation error 13%. Compute avoidable bias and variance, say which dominates, name two fixes and one that would not help.
    Show solution

    Avoidable bias \(= 2\% - 1\% = 1\%\) (small). Variance \(= 13\% - 2\% = 11\%\) (large). Variance dominates — overfitting. Fixes: more data (most reliable), regularization or a simpler model, or data augmentation for images. What would not help: a bigger model — more capacity overfits even more. Bias is already nearly handled at 1%.

  3. Error analysis on 100 examples, 25 errors: 12 "cluttered background," 8 "object too small," 5 "actually mislabeled." Compute max gain per bucket. What first, and why?
    Show solution

    Potential gain = errors ÷ total. Cluttered \(12/100 = 12\%\). Small object \(8/100 = 8\%\). Mislabeled \(5/100 = 5\%\). Do mislabeled first despite being smallest — those 5 aren't model errors, so true accuracy is already \((75+5)/100 = 80\%\), and 5% noisy labels corrupt both training and evaluation. Then attack cluttered background (largest gain, 12 pts) via augmentation or more cluttered training images. Small object is third.

  4. A loan model has 91% overall accuracy. Audit: under-25 approved 30% of the time at 78% accuracy; over-25 approved 65% at 94% accuracy. Name two possible causes and two concrete next steps before shipping.
    Show solution

    Causes: (1) Historical bias / imbalance — if the bank rarely approved young applicants, the model learned and perpetuates that, regardless of true creditworthiness. (2) Fewer/noisier examples for under-25, so the model is genuinely worse for them (note 78% vs 94%). (3) A feature acting as an age proxy (e.g. "years of credit history"). Next steps: (a) measure a fairness criterion explicitly — e.g. equal opportunity, comparing true-positive rate across age groups among applicants who actually repaid; (b) collect/up-weight more young-applicant data and audit which features drive the gap, removing age proxies; (c) involve domain/legal stakeholders — lending fairness is regulated and the "right" trade-off is a value decision. Do not ship until the gaps are understood and justified.
