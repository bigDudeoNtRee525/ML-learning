Module 5

Logistic Regression & Classification

01 · the moment linear regression breaks

An email lands. Spam, or not?

You spent Module 3 building a model that predicts a number: \( z = wx + b \), a straight line. It is good at "how much will this house sell for." Now I hand you a new job: is this email spam — yes or no?

So you do the obvious thing. You label spam as 1 and not-spam as 0, you fit a line through it, and you say "if the line is above 0.5, call it spam." Reasonable. Watch it fail.

Here is the count of suspicious links in an email (\( x \)) versus its true label (\( y \), 0 or 1). Fit a straight line through those dots in your head. Now answer:

You fit a line to 0/1 labels. One email shows up with 40 suspicious links — wildly spammy. What number does your straight line predict for it?

A straight line does not know that probabilities live between 0 and 1. Feed it a big \( x \) and it cheerfully returns 4.7, or −2.3 for a clean email. "This email is 470% spam" is gibberish. Worse: one extreme outlier tilts the whole line, dragging the 0.5 crossing point and misclassifying emails that were fine before. Linear regression is the wrong shape for yes/no. We need an output that can't escape the 0-to-1 box.

02 · the fix is a dimmer switch

Don't kill the line. Squash it.

The line itself is fine as a measure of evidence. Big positive \( z = wx+b \) means "tons of evidence for spam." Big negative means "tons of evidence for clean." Near zero means "on the fence." Keep that. The problem is only the final number escaping the box.

So bolt on a second step: take the unbounded evidence \( z \) and run it through an S-shaped squashing function that physically cannot leave 0 and 1. Think of a dimmer switch, not a light switch. A light switch is brutal — fully on or fully off. The dimmer slides smoothly: far-left is off (probability 0), the middle is half-bright (0.5), far-right is full (probability 1). Crank the dial to the extreme and the light barely changes; near the middle a tiny turn swings it a lot.

That dimmer is the sigmoid. Before I show you the formula, predict its behaviour:

The sigmoid takes any \( z \) from −∞ to +∞ and returns a probability. What does it return when the evidence is exactly zero, \( z = 0 \)?

Zero evidence is the definition of "I have no idea" — a coin flip, 0.5. That is the pivot point of the whole curve. Push \( z \) positive and it slides up toward 1; push it negative and it slides down toward 0, but it never reaches either. There is always a sliver of doubt. That smoothness is not decoration — it is exactly what lets gradient descent feel which way to nudge the dial.

03 · now the notation you earned

You just invented the sigmoid.

Two steps, written down. First the same old linear score from Module 3. Then squash it.

\[ z = wx + b \qquad\qquad \sigma(z) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-z}} \]

x the input feature (e.g. link-count). w the weight — how strongly \( x \) pushes toward "yes." b the bias — slides the curve left/right. z the raw evidence, any real number. σ (sigma) the sigmoid, the dimmer switch. e ≈ 2.718, Euler's number, a fixed constant.

Read the formula like a sentence. When \( z \) is large positive, \( -z \) is large negative, so \( e^{-z} \) is tiny, the denominator is ≈ 1, and \( \sigma(z) \approx 1 \). When \( z \) is large negative, \( e^{-z} \) is huge, the fraction is tiny, and \( \sigma(z) \approx 0 \). When \( z = 0 \), \( e^{0}=1 \), so \( \sigma(0) = \frac{1}{1+1} = 0.5 \) — the coin flip you predicted. One formula, the whole S.

The model's predicted probability that the example is class 1 is:

\[ \hat{y} = \sigma(wx + b) \]

ŷ ("y-hat") our estimate of \( P(\text{class}=1) \). Predicted class is 1 if \( \hat{y} \ge 0.5 \), else 0. Since \( \sigma(z) \ge 0.5 \) exactly when \( z \ge 0 \), the decision boundary sits wherever \( wx + b = 0 \).

That boundary is the dividing line between "predict yes" and "predict no." With one feature it's a single point on the number line; with two features it's a straight line on a plane. And the 0.5 threshold? Just a default. You can move it — more on that when it bites you in Module 6.

Free reuse. Notice nothing here is new except the squash. The z = wx + b is Module 3, unchanged. In Beat 07 the optimiser will be Module 4's gradient descent, unchanged. You're adding one twist, not starting over.
04 · do it by hand before the machine does

Squash three scores yourself.

Grab \( e \approx 2.718 \). Cover the answers and compute \( \sigma(z) \) for \( z = 0 \), \( z = 2 \), and \( z = -1 \) before you peek. You only understand the dimmer once your own hand has turned it.

Check my sigmoid arithmetic

\( z = 0 \): \( e^{-0} = 1 \), so \( \sigma(0) = \frac{1}{1+1} = 0.5 \). Zero evidence, a perfect coin flip.

\( z = 2 \): \( e^{-2} = \frac{1}{2.718^2} = \frac{1}{7.389} \approx 0.135 \). Then \( \sigma(2) = \frac{1}{1 + 0.135} = \frac{1}{1.135} \approx 0.881 \). Strong positive evidence, about 88% sure of "yes."

\( z = -1 \): \( e^{-(-1)} = e^{1} = 2.718 \). Then \( \sigma(-1) = \frac{1}{1 + 2.718} = \frac{1}{3.718} \approx 0.269 \). Negative evidence, about 27% — i.e. ~73% sure of "no."

Sanity check: as \( z \) went −1 → 0 → 2, the probability climbed 0.27 → 0.50 → 0.88. More evidence, more confidence, smoothly. Good.

05 · the lab — drive the dimmer

Bend the curve. Move the boundary. Flip a point.

This is the dimmer switch, live. Slide \( w \) to make the S steep or gentle, slide \( b \) to drag it left or right. The orange line is the decision boundary (where \( wx + b = 0 \)); watch each point flip its predicted class as it crosses \( p = 0.5 \). Don't just wiggle it — run the missions.

LAB sigmoid & the decision boundary

Each mission is something you make happen in the widget, then verify with your eyes (or your boss companion).

  • Park the decision boundary at a target x Slide b until the 0.5 crossing sits at a specific x you pick (say x = 3). The boundary lives at x = −b/w.
  • Make the curve STEEP, then GENTLE Crank w large → a near-vertical cliff (decisive, almost a hard switch). Shrink w toward 0 → a lazy ramp (everything hovers near 0.5).
  • Flip a point's predicted class Pick a point near the boundary and move w or b until it crosses 0.5 — its class label should switch.
  • Break it on purpose Set w negative. What happens to "more evidence = more yes"? Explain why the curve now runs backward.
What "steep w" actually means

\( w \) is the gain on the dimmer. Big \( |w| \) = the curve goes from ~0 to ~1 over a tiny range of \( x \): the model is decisive, almost a hard switch, confident everywhere except a razor-thin no-man's-land. Small \( w \) = a gentle ramp: wishy-washy, most points sit near 0.5. Negative \( w \) flips the orientation — now bigger \( x \) means lower probability. The boundary location is set by \( b \) (specifically \( x = -b/w \)); the boundary sharpness is set by \( w \).

06 · how wrong is a probability?

Confident and wrong should hurt. A lot.

To train this thing we need to measure error. In Module 3 we used squared distance \( (\hat{y}-y)^2 \). For probabilities that is a weak punishment — being 99% sure of the wrong answer barely registers. We want a loss that is near zero when you're confidently right, and that explodes toward infinity when you're confidently wrong. That is how a model learns humility.

Before the formula, predict the shape of the punishment:

The true label is 1. Your model said \( \hat{y} = 0.01 \) — just 1% on the thing that actually happened. Log loss penalises you by \( -\ln(\hat{y}) \). Roughly how big is that penalty?

\( -\ln(0.01) \approx 4.6 \). Push your wrong confidence to \( \hat{y} = 0.001 \) and the penalty jumps to \( \approx 6.9 \); at \( 10^{-6} \) it's \( \approx 13.8 \). The logarithm has a vertical wall at 0 — as your wrong-confidence approaches certainty, the loss approaches infinity. Meanwhile if you'd said 0.99 and been right, \( -\ln(0.99) \approx 0.01 \), almost free. That brutal asymmetry is the entire point of log loss.

The formula for a single example with true label \( y \in \{0,1\} \) and predicted probability \( \hat{y} \):

\[ L(\hat{y}, y) = -\big[\, y \ln(\hat{y}) + (1 - y)\ln(1 - \hat{y}) \,\big] \]

y the true label, 0 or 1. ŷ your predicted probability of class 1. ln natural log (base \( e \)). The \( y \) and \( (1-y) \) are an on/off switch: only one term ever survives.

It looks like two terms but only one is ever live. If \( y = 1 \), then \( (1-y) = 0 \) deletes the second term and the loss is \( -\ln(\hat{y}) \) — "how little probability did you give to yes?" If \( y = 0 \), the first term vanishes and the loss is \( -\ln(1-\hat{y}) \) — "how little did you give to no?" Average \( L \) over all examples to get the total cost gradient descent minimises.

Do it by hand. Your model predicts \( \hat{y} = 0.9 \). Compute the loss in both worlds before you peek.

Check my log-loss arithmetic

True label \( y = 1 \) (confident and right): loss \( = -\ln(0.9) = -(-0.105) \approx 0.105 \). Small — gently nudged, because you were confident and correct but not perfectly certain.

True label \( y = 0 \) (confident and wrong): loss \( = -\ln(1 - 0.9) = -\ln(0.1) = -(-2.302) \approx 2.302 \). More than twenty times bigger for the exact same prediction. The model is hammered for being sure of the wrong thing.

0.105 versus 2.302 — that gap is log loss teaching the model to be confident only when it has earned it.

07 · the optimiser is free

No new optimiser. Module 4, again.

Here is the delightful part. To train, we minimise the average log loss with gradient descent — the exact procedure from Module 4. We just need the gradients (the slopes telling us how to nudge \( w \) and \( b \)). After the calculus settles, they come out identical in shape to linear regression's:

\[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial w} = (\hat{y} - y)\,x, \qquad \frac{\partial L}{\partial b} = (\hat{y} - y) \]

(ŷ − y) the prediction error. Weight gradient = error × input. Bias gradient = error alone (its "input" is the constant 1). The only change from Module 3 is that \( \hat{y} \) is now \( \sigma(wx+b) \), not raw \( wx+b \).

So the update rule is the one you already know cold:

\[ w \leftarrow w - \alpha\,\frac{\partial L}{\partial w}, \qquad b \leftarrow b - \alpha\,\frac{\partial L}{\partial b} \]

α (alpha) the learning rate, a small positive step size. ← means "becomes." Same loop, same minus sign, same robot-in-fog from Module 4.

Logistic regression is linear regression wearing a sigmoid hat and a log-loss coat. Now find the bug a beginner planted in the loop.

This trainer runs without errors, but the loss climbs every step instead of falling. Read it and spot the bug before you open the fix.

import numpy as np def sigmoid(z): return 1.0 / (1.0 + np.exp(-z)) X = np.array([[1.,1.],[1.5,.5],[.5,1.5],[1.,2.], [4.,4.],[4.5,3.5],[3.5,4.5],[4.,5.]]) y = np.array([0,0,0,0,1,1,1,1], dtype=float) w, b, alpha, n = np.zeros(2), 0.0, 0.1, len(y) for step in range(2001): y_hat = sigmoid(X @ w + b) error = y_hat - y grad_w = (X.T @ error) / n grad_b = error.mean() w = w + alpha * grad_w # <-- something is wrong on this line b = b + alpha * grad_b # <-- and on this one
Show the bug & why

The signs are flipped. w = w + alpha * grad_w walks uphill — it climbs the loss. The gradient points toward higher cost, so to go down you must subtract it: w = w - alpha * grad_w and b = b - alpha * grad_b. This is the identical sign trap from Module 4: one flipped minus is the whole difference between a model that learns and one that screams off to infinity.

08 · the whole thing, from scratch

Predict the output, then run it.

The corrected, complete trainer on a tiny 2D dataset: blue points (class 0) low-left, green points (class 1) high-right. Sigmoid, log loss, plain gradient descent. Before you run it — predict: what loss does it start at when every weight is zero, before any learning?

My guess for the starting loss

With w = 0, b = 0, every \( z = 0 \), so every \( \hat{y} = \sigma(0) = 0.5 \). The model guesses 0.5 for everything, and log loss of a constant 0.5 is \( -\ln(0.5) \approx 0.693 \). That is the "I know nothing" baseline — if your loss ever sits above 0.693, your model is worse than a coin flip.

import numpy as np # --- tiny 2D dataset: 4 points class 0 (lower-left), 4 points class 1 (upper-right) X = np.array([ [1.0, 1.0], [1.5, 0.5], [0.5, 1.5], [1.0, 2.0], # class 0 [4.0, 4.0], [4.5, 3.5], [3.5, 4.5], [4.0, 5.0], # class 1 ]) y = np.array([0, 0, 0, 0, 1, 1, 1, 1], dtype=float) def sigmoid(z): return 1.0 / (1.0 + np.exp(-z)) def log_loss(y_true, y_hat): eps = 1e-12 # clip so we never take log(0) y_hat = np.clip(y_hat, eps, 1 - eps) return -np.mean(y_true * np.log(y_hat) + (1 - y_true) * np.log(1 - y_hat)) # --- parameters: one weight per feature, plus a bias w = np.zeros(2) # [w1, w2] b = 0.0 alpha = 0.1 # learning rate n = len(y) for step in range(2001): z = X @ w + b # linear score for every example y_hat = sigmoid(z) # squash to probabilities error = y_hat - y # (y_hat - y), the shared gradient piece grad_w = (X.T @ error) / n # average gradient for the weights grad_b = error.mean() # average gradient for the bias w -= alpha * grad_w # gradient descent step (note the MINUS) b -= alpha * grad_b if step % 500 == 0: print(f"step {step:4d} loss {log_loss(y, y_hat):.4f} w={np.round(w,3)} b={b:.3f}") # --- evaluate probs = sigmoid(X @ w + b) preds = (probs >= 0.5).astype(int) print("predicted classes:", preds) print("accuracy:", (preds == y).mean())

The loss starts near 0.693 (your predicted \( -\ln 0.5 \) baseline) and falls steadily toward roughly 0.05 as the weights grow. The last line reports accuracy: 1.0 — all eight points correct. The boundary is wherever X @ w + b == 0; both learned weights are positive, so it's the diagonal splitting lower-left from upper-right. And notice: no new optimiser was written. The error = y_hat - y trick is the same gradient shape from Module 4.

09 · the three-line version & going multiclass

scikit-learn, and beyond yes/no.

Once you understand the mechanics, the library does all of it — and hands you predict_proba so you can read calibrated probabilities, not just hard labels. Binary Iris: is the flower setosa, or not? Predict the first row of predict_proba before running — setosa is famously easy to separate, so expect near-certainty.

from sklearn.datasets import load_iris from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression from sklearn.model_selection import train_test_split iris = load_iris() X = iris.data # 4 features per flower y = (iris.target == 0).astype(int) # 1 if species 'setosa', else 0 X_train, X_test, y_train, y_test = train_test_split( X, y, test_size=0.3, random_state=42) model = LogisticRegression() model.fit(X_train, y_train) # gradient-based optimisation, under the hood print("test accuracy:", model.score(X_test, y_test)) # probabilities for the first 3 test flowers: P(class 0), P(class 1) print(model.predict_proba(X_test[:3]).round(3)) print("hard predictions:", model.predict(X_test[:3]))

Prints test accuracy: 1.0, then a small table where each row is two numbers summing to 1 — e.g. [0.998 0.002] meaning "99.8% not-setosa, 0.2% setosa." .predict just applies the 0.5 threshold to those probabilities for you. Same idea as your from-scratch loop, fewer keystrokes, plus battle-tested numerical care.

Three or more classes: softmax

What about ten digits, or all three Iris species? Generalise the sigmoid to softmax. Instead of one score \( z \), compute one score \( z_k \) per class \( k \), then turn the whole bundle into probabilities that are all positive and sum to 1:

\[ P(\text{class}=k) = \frac{e^{z_k}}{\sum_{j} e^{z_j}} \]

zk the score for class \( k \). The denominator adds \( e^{z_j} \) over every class \( j \), normalising so the probabilities sum to 1. Exponentiate every score (forces positive), then divide each by the total.

The biggest score gets the biggest probability; the chosen class is the highest one. The matching loss is categorical cross-entropy — the same "negative log of the probability you gave the true class" idea, extended to many classes. scikit-learn does this automatically: call LogisticRegression on a target with three labels and it runs the multiclass version. You'll meet softmax again as the last layer of classification neural networks in Module 10.

Lock this in. The sigmoid you bent in the lab is the 2-class special case of softmax. You will see softmax again in Module 10 as the final layer of every classification neural net — same exponentiate-then-normalise, just wider. The trap to dodge: sigmoid is for binary (one probability); softmax is for multiclass (a probability per class). A sigmoid bolted onto ten classes is a bug, not a shortcut.

Why can't a plain straight line predict yes/no probabilities?
tap to flip
A line climbs forever — it returns nonsense like 4.7 or −2.3, outside [0,1], and outliers tilt the whole boundary. The sigmoid squashes evidence into a value that can't escape 0 to 1.
What is \( \sigma(0) \), and why?
tap to flip
0.5. At \( z=0 \), \( e^{0}=1 \), so \( \sigma = \frac{1}{1+1} \). Zero evidence = coin flip. That's also where the decision boundary sits.
Why log loss instead of squared error?
tap to flip
Log loss explodes toward infinity for confident-and-wrong predictions (the log has a wall at 0) and is near-zero for confident-and-right. Squared error barely punishes overconfidence.
What's new in the training loop vs. Module 4?
tap to flip
Nothing in the optimiser. Same \( w \leftarrow w - \alpha\cdot\text{grad} \), same error = y_hat - y gradient shape. The only change: \( \hat{y} = \sigma(wx+b) \) instead of raw \( wx+b \).
Sigmoid vs. softmax — when each?
tap to flip
Sigmoid for binary (one probability). Softmax for 3+ classes (a probability per class, summing to 1). Softmax with 2 classes reduces to the sigmoid.

12 · spar a few more

Extra reps, answers hidden.

  1. Compute \( \sigma(z) \) by hand for \( z = 1 \) and \( z = -2 \) (use \( e \approx 2.718 \)). Which prediction is more confident, and in which direction?
    Show solution

    \( z = 1 \): \( e^{-1} = 1/2.718 \approx 0.368 \), so \( \sigma(1) = \frac{1}{1.368} \approx 0.731 \) — about 73% for class 1.

    \( z = -2 \): \( e^{2} \approx 7.389 \), so \( \sigma(-2) = \frac{1}{8.389} \approx 0.119 \) — about 12% for class 1, i.e. ~88% for class 0.

    \( z = -2 \) is more confident (0.119 is further from 0.5 than 0.731 is) and points toward class 0.

  2. A model predicts \( \hat{y} = 0.6 \). Compute the log loss if \( y = 1 \), and again if \( y = 0 \). Which is larger, and why?
    Show solution

    \( y = 1 \): loss \( = -\ln(0.6) \approx 0.511 \).

    \( y = 0 \): loss \( = -\ln(1 - 0.6) = -\ln(0.4) \approx 0.916 \).

    The \( y=0 \) case is larger: the model leaned the wrong way (only 0.4 on the correct class 0), so it's penalised more than when it leaned right.

  3. With \( w = 2 \), \( b = -4 \) for a single feature \( x \): where is the decision boundary? Predict the class for \( x = 1 \) and \( x = 3 \).
    Show solution

    Boundary: \( 2x - 4 = 0 \Rightarrow x = 2 \). For \( x \gt 2 \), \( z \gt 0 \) so class 1; for \( x \lt 2 \), class 0.

    \( x = 1 \): \( z = 2(1)-4 = -2 \), \( \sigma(-2) \approx 0.12 \) → class 0. \( x = 3 \): \( z = 2(3)-4 = 2 \), \( \sigma(2) \approx 0.88 \) → class 1.

  4. The imbalance trap. 5% of transactions are fraud. A model always predicts "not fraud." What's its accuracy, why is it terrible, and what would you do instead?
    Show solution

    Accuracy = 95% (right on every legit transaction, which are 95% of the data) yet it catches zero fraud — useless for the actual goal. Fix: lower the threshold well below 0.5 so suspicious transactions get flagged (more false alarms, more fraud caught), and judge with recall and precision (or area under the precision–recall curve), not raw accuracy. You'll formalise these in Module 6.
