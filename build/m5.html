Module 5

Logistic Regression and Classification

Learning objectives

By the end of this module you'll be able to:

  • Explain the difference between predicting a number (regression) and predicting a category (classification), and say why we predict a probability in between.
  • Use the sigmoid function \( \sigma(z) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-z}} \) to squash any score into a probability between 0 and 1, by hand and in code.
  • Describe what a decision boundary is, and explain why the 0.5 threshold is a convenient default rather than a law of nature.
  • Explain cross-entropy / log loss in plain words first, then compute it for a single prediction by hand.
  • Implement logistic regression from scratch in NumPy — sigmoid, log loss, and a gradient descent loop — and watch it learn a boundary.
  • Train the same model in three lines with scikit-learn, read predict_proba, and extend the idea to many classes with softmax.

Why should I care?

An email arrives. Is it spam, or not? A patient's blood test comes back. Does this point to a disease, or not? A user hovers over a "Buy" button. Will they click, or not? None of these questions wants a number like "spam-ness = 73.4". They want a decision — but a good system should also tell you how sure it is. "This email is 98% likely to be spam" lets you auto-delete it; "this email is 55% likely to be spam" tells you to keep it in the inbox just in case. Logistic regression is the simplest, most-used tool for exactly this: turning evidence into a calibrated probability and then into a yes/no call. It is the workhorse behind credit scoring, medical risk models, and click prediction — and, beautifully, it reuses everything you already learned in Module 3 — Linear Regression and Module 4 — Gradient Descent. You are not starting over; you are adding one small twist.

Intuition first

In Module 3 you built a model that produces a number: \( z = w x + b \), a straight line. That number can be anything — minus a million, zero, plus a thousand. For "will the user click?" a raw number is awkward: what would "click-ness = 42" even mean? We want an answer between "definitely no" and "definitely yes", and the natural language for that is probability: a value from 0 (no way) to 1 (certainly).

So logistic regression does two steps. First it computes the same old linear score \( z = w x + b \) — call this the evidence. A big positive \( z \) means "lots of evidence for yes"; a big negative \( z \) means "lots of evidence for no"; \( z \) near zero means "on the fence". Second, it squashes that evidence into a probability using a special S-shaped curve called the sigmoid. Huge positive evidence maps to a probability near 1, huge negative evidence to near 0, and zero evidence to exactly 0.5 — a coin flip.

Analogy — the dimmer switch. A regular light switch is brutal: fully on or fully off, nothing in between. That is what we'd get if we tried to output 0 or 1 directly. The sigmoid is a dimmer switch. As you turn the dial (increase the evidence \( z \)) the light brightens smoothly from off (probability 0) through half-bright (0.5) up to fully on (probability 1). It never quite hits pure 0 or pure 1 — there's always a sliver of doubt — and around the middle a small turn of the dial makes a big difference, while at the extremes you can crank the dial hard and barely change the brightness. That smoothness is not just pretty: it is exactly what lets gradient descent feel which way to nudge the dial.

Here is the sigmoid curve. Notice the gentle S: flat at the far left near 0, steep through the middle, flat at the far right near 1.

The sigmoid maps any real score \( z \) (horizontal) to a probability between 0 and 1 (vertical). At \( z=0 \) it passes through exactly 0.5.

Play with it: drag w to stretch or squash the S-curve and b to slide it left or right. Watch the orange decision boundary (where w·x + b = 0) move, and see each point flip between predicted class 0 and class 1 as it crosses p = 0.5.

Once we have a probability, making a decision is easy: pick a threshold. The default is 0.5 — if the predicted probability is at least 0.5, say "yes" (class 1); otherwise "no" (class 0). The set of inputs where the probability is exactly 0.5 is the decision boundary: the dividing line between the two predicted classes. With one input feature that boundary is a single point on the number line; with two features it is a straight line on a 2D plane.

Making it precise

Let \( x \) be an input feature (a number describing the example, e.g. an email's link-count). Let \( w \) be the weight (a number we adjust during training that says how strongly \( x \) pushes toward "yes") and \( b \) the bias (a number we adjust that shifts the whole curve left or right). The linear score, exactly as in Module 3, is

\[ z = w x + b \]

where \( z \) is the raw evidence — any real number. We turn \( z \) into a probability with the sigmoid function \( \sigma \) (the Greek letter "sigma"):

\[ \sigma(z) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-z}} \]

Here \( e \approx 2.718 \) is Euler's number (a fixed constant), and \( e^{-z} \) means \( e \) raised to the power \( -z \). Read the formula in words: when \( z \) is large and positive, \( -z \) is large and negative, so \( e^{-z} \) is tiny (close to 0), so the denominator is about 1 and \( \sigma(z) \approx 1 \). When \( z \) is large and negative, \( e^{-z} \) is huge, so the fraction is tiny and \( \sigma(z) \approx 0 \). When \( z = 0 \), \( e^{0} = 1 \), so \( \sigma(0) = \frac{1}{1+1} = 0.5 \). That single formula produces the whole S-curve.

We write the model's predicted probability that the example is class 1 as

\[ \hat{y} = \sigma(w x + b) \]

where \( \hat{y} \) (read "y-hat") is our estimate of \( P(\text{class} = 1) \). The predicted class is then class 1 if \( \hat{y} \ge 0.5 \), else class 0. Because \( \sigma(z) \ge 0.5 \) happens exactly when \( z \ge 0 \), the decision boundary is simply the place where \( w x + b = 0 \).

How wrong is a probability? Cross-entropy, in words first

In Module 3 we measured error with squared distance, \( (\hat{y} - y)^2 \). For probabilities that is a poor fit. We want a loss that does two things: it should be near zero when we confidently predict the correct class, and it should grow very large when we confidently predict the wrong class. Confident-and-wrong should hurt a lot — that is how the model learns humility.

The loss that does this is log loss (also called cross-entropy). The intuition: it asks "what probability did you assign to the answer that actually happened?" and then penalises you by the negative logarithm of that probability. If you said the right answer was 99% likely and it happened, \( -\ln(0.99) \) is almost 0 — barely any penalty. If you said it was 1% likely and it still happened, \( -\ln(0.01) \approx 4.6 \) — a big penalty. The logarithm is what makes overconfident mistakes explode toward infinity.

For a single example with true label \( y \in \{0, 1\} \) (0 or 1) and predicted probability \( \hat{y} \), the formula is

\[ L(\hat{y}, y) = -\big[\, y \ln(\hat{y}) + (1 - y)\ln(1 - \hat{y}) \,\big] \]

where \( \ln \) is the natural logarithm (log base \( e \)). This looks like two terms but only one is ever "switched on": if the true label \( y = 1 \), then \( (1-y) = 0 \) kills the second term and the loss is \( -\ln(\hat{y}) \) — penalise by how little probability you gave to "yes". If \( y = 0 \), the first term vanishes and the loss is \( -\ln(1 - \hat{y}) \) — penalise by how little probability you gave to "no". The \( y \) and \( (1-y) \) are just a tidy on/off switch for picking the right penalty. Averaging \( L \) over all training examples gives the total cost we minimise.

Worked example — by hand

Part 1 — squashing scores with the sigmoid. Let's compute \( \sigma(z) \) for three scores, using \( e \approx 2.718 \).

  • \( z = 0 \): \( e^{-0} = 1 \), so \( \sigma(0) = \frac{1}{1+1} = \frac{1}{2} = 0.5 \). Zero evidence, a perfect coin flip.
  • \( z = 2 \): \( e^{-2} = \frac{1}{e^{2}} = \frac{1}{2.718^2} = \frac{1}{7.389} \approx 0.135 \). Then \( \sigma(2) = \frac{1}{1 + 0.135} = \frac{1}{1.135} \approx 0.881 \). Strong positive evidence, about 88% confident in "yes".
  • \( z = -1 \): \( e^{-(-1)} = e^{1} = 2.718 \). Then \( \sigma(-1) = \frac{1}{1 + 2.718} = \frac{1}{3.718} \approx 0.269 \). Negative evidence, about 27% — i.e. roughly 73% confident in "no".

Sanity check: as \( z \) went from \( -1 \) to \( 0 \) to \( 2 \), the probability climbed from 0.27 to 0.50 to 0.88. More evidence, more confidence, smoothly. Good.

Part 2 — the cost of a single prediction. Suppose the model predicts \( \hat{y} = 0.9 \) (90% sure it's class 1). Compare two worlds.

  • If the true label is \( y = 1 \) (we were right): loss \( = -\ln(0.9) = -(-0.105) \approx 0.105 \). Small — we are gently nudged because we were confident and correct, but not perfectly certain.
  • If the true label is \( y = 0 \) (we were confidently wrong): loss \( = -\ln(1 - 0.9) = -\ln(0.1) = -(-2.302) \approx 2.302 \). More than twenty times larger. The model is strongly punished for being sure of the wrong thing.

That asymmetry — 0.105 versus 2.302 for the very same prediction — is the whole point of log loss. It teaches the model to be confident only when it has earned it.

Reusing gradient descent — no new optimizer

Here is the delightful part. To train the model we minimise the average log loss by gradient descent, exactly the procedure from Module 4 — Gradient Descent. We just need the gradients (the slopes telling us which way to nudge \( w \) and \( b \)). Remarkably, after the calculus settles, they look identical in shape to linear regression's:

\[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial w} = (\hat{y} - y)\,x, \qquad \frac{\partial L}{\partial b} = (\hat{y} - y) \]

In words: the gradient is the prediction error \( (\hat{y} - y) \) times the input \( x \) (for the weight) or just the error (for the bias). The only change from Module 3 is that \( \hat{y} \) is now \( \sigma(wx+b) \) instead of the raw \( wx+b \). So the update rule is unchanged:

\[ w \leftarrow w - \alpha\,\frac{\partial L}{\partial w}, \qquad b \leftarrow b - \alpha\,\frac{\partial L}{\partial b} \]

where \( \alpha \) (alpha) is the learning rate, a small positive number controlling step size, and \( \leftarrow \) means "becomes". You already know how to run this loop. Logistic regression is linear regression wearing a sigmoid hat and a log-loss coat.

With two features, the decision boundary is a straight line. Points on one side get probability above 0.5 (predicted blue), points on the other side below 0.5 (predicted green).

In code: from scratch

Let's build the whole thing in NumPy on a tiny 2D dataset: blue points (class 0) clustered low-left, green points (class 1) high-right. We implement the sigmoid, the log loss, and a plain gradient descent loop, then watch the loss fall and read off the learned boundary.

import numpy as np # --- tiny 2D dataset: 4 points class 0 (lower-left), 4 points class 1 (upper-right) X = np.array([ [1.0, 1.0], [1.5, 0.5], [0.5, 1.5], [1.0, 2.0], # class 0 [4.0, 4.0], [4.5, 3.5], [3.5, 4.5], [4.0, 5.0], # class 1 ]) y = np.array([0, 0, 0, 0, 1, 1, 1, 1], dtype=float) def sigmoid(z): return 1.0 / (1.0 + np.exp(-z)) def log_loss(y_true, y_hat): eps = 1e-12 # clip so we never take log(0) y_hat = np.clip(y_hat, eps, 1 - eps) return -np.mean(y_true * np.log(y_hat) + (1 - y_true) * np.log(1 - y_hat)) # --- parameters: one weight per feature, plus a bias w = np.zeros(2) # [w1, w2] b = 0.0 alpha = 0.1 # learning rate n = len(y) for step in range(2001): z = X @ w + b # linear score for every example y_hat = sigmoid(z) # squash to probabilities error = y_hat - y # (y_hat - y), the shared gradient piece grad_w = (X.T @ error) / n # average gradient for the weights grad_b = error.mean() # average gradient for the bias w -= alpha * grad_w # gradient descent step b -= alpha * grad_b if step % 500 == 0: print(f"step {step:4d} loss {log_loss(y, y_hat):.4f} w={np.round(w,3)} b={b:.3f}") # --- evaluate probs = sigmoid(X @ w + b) preds = (probs >= 0.5).astype(int) print("predicted classes:", preds) print("accuracy:", (preds == y).mean())

Running this prints a loss that starts near 0.693 (that's \( -\ln 0.5 \), the loss of a model guessing 0.5 for everything) and steadily falls toward roughly 0.05 as the weights grow. The final line reports accuracy: 1.0 — all eight points classified correctly. The decision boundary is wherever X @ w + b == 0; because both learned weights are positive, that boundary is the diagonal line separating lower-left from upper-right, exactly matching the picture above. Notice we wrote no new optimizer: the error = y_hat - y trick is the same gradient shape you used in Module 4.

In code: the library way

Once you understand the mechanics, scikit-learn does all of it in a few lines — and adds predict_proba so you can read the calibrated probabilities, not just the hard labels. Here we use the classic Iris dataset, reduced to a binary problem: is the flower the species setosa, or not?

from sklearn.datasets import load_iris from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression from sklearn.model_selection import train_test_split iris = load_iris() X = iris.data # 4 features per flower y = (iris.target == 0).astype(int) # 1 if species 'setosa', else 0 X_train, X_test, y_train, y_test = train_test_split( X, y, test_size=0.3, random_state=42) model = LogisticRegression() model.fit(X_train, y_train) # gradient-based optimisation, under the hood print("test accuracy:", model.score(X_test, y_test)) # probabilities for the first 3 test flowers: P(class 0), P(class 1) print(model.predict_proba(X_test[:3]).round(3)) print("hard predictions:", model.predict(X_test[:3]))

This prints test accuracy: 1.0 (setosa is famously easy to separate), then a small table where each row is two numbers summing to 1 — for example [0.998 0.002] meaning "99.8% not-setosa, 0.2% setosa". The .predict method just applies the 0.5 threshold to those probabilities for you. Same idea as your from-scratch loop, fewer keystrokes, plus battle-tested numerical care.

More than two classes: softmax

What if you have three or more categories — say all three Iris species, or the ten handwritten digits 0–9? We generalise the sigmoid to the softmax function. Instead of one score \( z \), we compute one score \( z_k \) per class \( k \), then convert the whole bundle of scores into a set of probabilities that are all positive and sum to 1:

\[ P(\text{class}=k) = \frac{e^{z_k}}{\sum_{j} e^{z_j}} \]

where \( z_k \) is the score for class \( k \), and the denominator \( \sum_j e^{z_j} \) adds up \( e^{z_j} \) over every class \( j \) so the probabilities are normalised. In words: exponentiate every score (making them all positive), then divide each by the total. The biggest score gets the biggest probability; the chosen class is the one with the highest probability. The matching loss is categorical cross-entropy, the same "negative log of the probability you gave the true class" idea extended to many classes. Scikit-learn handles this automatically — call LogisticRegression on a target with three labels and it does the multiclass version for you. You will meet softmax again as the last layer of classification neural networks in Module 10 — Neural Network Fundamentals.

Common pitfalls & misconceptions

  • "Logistic regression is a kind of regression, so it predicts numbers." The name is a historical accident. It is a classification algorithm. The only number it predicts is a probability, which you immediately turn into a class label.
  • Judging an imbalanced problem by accuracy. If 99% of emails are legitimate, a lazy model that always says "not spam" scores 99% accuracy while catching zero spam. On imbalanced data, accuracy lies — use precision, recall, or AUC instead (you'll meet these in Module 6 — Model Evaluation).
  • Treating the 0.5 threshold as sacred. 0.5 is just the default. For cancer screening you might flag anyone above 0.1 (better to over-refer than miss a case); for an expensive automated action you might require 0.9. Move the threshold to trade off false alarms against misses.
  • Reading probabilities as certainties. A predicted 0.7 does not mean the model is "70% right"; it means that, among inputs like this one, roughly 70% are class 1. Probabilities describe frequencies, not guarantees.
  • Forgetting to scale features. Like linear regression, logistic regression converges faster and behaves better when features are on similar scales. If one feature is in thousands and another in fractions, standardise them first (Module 7 — Feature Engineering).
  • Confusing sigmoid with softmax. Sigmoid is for binary (one probability); softmax is for multiclass (a probability per class that sum to 1). Using two sigmoids for two classes is fine; using a sigmoid for ten classes is not.

Exercises

  1. Compute \( \sigma(z) \) by hand for \( z = 1 \) and \( z = -2 \). Use \( e \approx 2.718 \). Which prediction is more confident, and in which direction?
    Show solution

    For \( z = 1 \): \( e^{-1} = 1/2.718 \approx 0.368 \), so \( \sigma(1) = \frac{1}{1+0.368} = \frac{1}{1.368} \approx 0.731 \) — about 73% confident in class 1.

    For \( z = -2 \): \( e^{2} \approx 7.389 \), so \( \sigma(-2) = \frac{1}{1+7.389} = \frac{1}{8.389} \approx 0.119 \) — about 12% for class 1, i.e. ~88% confident in class 0.

    \( z = -2 \) is the more confident prediction (0.119 is further from the uncertain 0.5 than 0.731 is), and it points toward class 0.

  2. A model predicts \( \hat{y} = 0.6 \). Compute the log loss if the true label is \( y = 1 \), and again if \( y = 0 \). Which is larger and why?
    Show solution

    If \( y = 1 \): loss \( = -\ln(0.6) \approx -(-0.511) = 0.511 \).

    If \( y = 0 \): loss \( = -\ln(1 - 0.6) = -\ln(0.4) \approx -(-0.916) = 0.916 \).

    The \( y = 0 \) case is larger because the model leaned the wrong way (it gave only 0.4 probability to the correct class 0), so it is penalised more than when it leaned the right way.

  3. The decision boundary is where \( w x + b = 0 \). If \( w = 2 \) and \( b = -4 \) for a single feature \( x \), at what value of \( x \) does the prediction flip from class 0 to class 1? Predict the class for \( x = 1 \) and \( x = 3 \).
    Show solution

    Boundary: \( 2x - 4 = 0 \Rightarrow x = 2 \). For \( x \gt 2 \), \( z \gt 0 \) so \( \hat{y} \gt 0.5 \) (class 1); for \( x \lt 2 \), class 0.

    \( x = 1 \): \( z = 2(1) - 4 = -2 \), \( \sigma(-2) \approx 0.12 \) → class 0. \( x = 3 \): \( z = 2(3) - 4 = 2 \), \( \sigma(2) \approx 0.88 \) → class 1.

  4. In the from-scratch code, the gradient line is error = y_hat - y. Explain why the bias gradient is just the mean of error while the weight gradient multiplies by X. (Hint: revisit the formulas under "Reusing gradient descent".)
    Show solution

    The per-example gradients are \( \partial L/\partial w = (\hat{y}-y)\,x \) and \( \partial L/\partial b = (\hat{y}-y) \). The bias has no input attached (its "feature" is the constant 1), so its gradient is just the error itself; averaging over examples gives error.mean(). Each weight \( w_i \) is tied to feature \( x_i \), so its gradient scales the error by that feature value — in matrix form X.T @ error / n computes the average of \( (\hat{y}-y)\,x_i \) for every feature at once.

  5. Challenge. Suppose 5% of transactions are fraud. A model always predicts "not fraud". What is its accuracy, and why is that a terrible model? Suggest a better threshold strategy and a better metric.
    Show solution

    Accuracy = 95% (it is right on every legitimate transaction, which are 95% of the data), yet it catches zero fraud — useless for the actual goal. This is the imbalanced-data trap. Better: lower the decision threshold well below 0.5 so borderline-suspicious transactions get flagged (accepting more false alarms to catch more fraud), and evaluate with recall (fraction of true fraud caught) and precision (fraction of flags that are real), or the area under the precision–recall curve, instead of raw accuracy. You'll formalise these in Module 6.

Projects — build something real

Reading and hand-calculation get you understanding; shipping a small project gets you fluency. Pick one (or work through several) and do it end to end. Each lists a concrete goal, a dataset you can grab today, milestones to pace yourself, a success bar, and a stretch for when you want more.

Project 1 — Spam vs. ham classifier

  • Goal: Train a logistic regression model that labels a short text message as spam (1) or not-spam (0) and reports a calibrated probability for each message.
  • Dataset: The SMS Spam Collection (~5,500 labelled texts), available from the UCI Machine Learning Repository or as a Kaggle download.
  • Milestones: (1) Load the data and split into train/test. (2) Turn each message into numeric features with CountVectorizer or TfidfVectorizer. (3) Fit LogisticRegression and print test accuracy. (4) Inspect predict_proba on five messages and eyeball whether the confidence makes sense.
  • Success: Test accuracy above 0.95, and you can explain one false positive and one false negative by reading the message.
  • Stretch: Plot precision and recall as you sweep the threshold from 0.1 to 0.9, and pick the threshold you would actually deploy if missing spam costs more than a false alarm.

Project 2 — From-scratch trainer, no scikit-learn

  • Goal: Reproduce the from-scratch NumPy model above on a real (small) dataset and match scikit-learn's accuracy within a couple of percent.
  • Dataset: The Breast Cancer Wisconsin dataset, built into scikit-learn via load_breast_cancer() (569 examples, 30 features, binary target).
  • Milestones: (1) Standardise the features (subtract mean, divide by standard deviation) so gradient descent behaves. (2) Run your own sigmoid + log-loss + gradient-descent loop. (3) Track the loss every 100 steps and confirm it decreases. (4) Compare your accuracy to a fitted LogisticRegression.
  • Success: Your hand-rolled model lands within 2 percentage points of scikit-learn on a held-out test set, and the loss curve is monotonically (mostly) decreasing.
  • Stretch: Add L2 regularisation (subtract \( \lambda w \) from each weight gradient) and observe how it shrinks the weights and affects test accuracy.

Project 3 — Threshold tuning for an imbalanced problem

  • Goal: Take an imbalanced dataset and show concretely why accuracy misleads, then choose a threshold that optimises a metric you actually care about.
  • Dataset: The Credit Card Fraud Detection dataset on Kaggle (284,807 transactions, only ~0.17% fraud) — heavily imbalanced by design.
  • Milestones: (1) Fit logistic regression and note the deceptively high accuracy. (2) Compute precision, recall, and the confusion matrix at threshold 0.5. (3) Sweep the threshold and plot the precision-recall curve. (4) Report the threshold that gives recall above 0.80.
  • Success: You can state, in one sentence, why the 99.8% accuracy model is useless and what threshold you would ship instead.
  • Stretch: Re-train with class_weight="balanced" and compare the precision-recall curve to the unweighted model.

Project 4 — Multiclass with softmax

  • Goal: Move beyond binary: classify handwritten digits into ten classes (0 through 9) using softmax logistic regression.
  • Dataset: The digits dataset built into scikit-learn via load_digits() (1,797 8x8 grayscale images, ten classes).
  • Milestones: (1) Flatten each 8x8 image into a 64-length feature vector. (2) Fit LogisticRegression (it uses softmax for more than two classes automatically). (3) Print the test accuracy and the per-class confusion matrix. (4) Find the two digits most often confused with each other.
  • Success: Test accuracy above 0.93, and you can name which digit pair the model confuses most.
  • Stretch: Visualise the learned weight vector for one class as an 8x8 image and describe what pattern it has learned to detect.

Project 5 — Probability calibration check

  • Goal: Test whether your model's stated probabilities are honest — when it says "70%", does the event really happen about 70% of the time?
  • Dataset: Any binary dataset from the projects above; the Titanic survival dataset (Kaggle) works well because the signal is moderate, not perfect.
  • Milestones: (1) Fit logistic regression and get test-set probabilities. (2) Bin predictions into ten buckets (0.0-0.1, 0.1-0.2, ...). (3) For each bucket, compare the average predicted probability to the actual fraction of positives. (4) Plot the two against each other — this is a reliability diagram.
  • Success: You produce a reliability diagram and can say whether the model is over-confident, under-confident, or well-calibrated.
  • Stretch: Apply CalibratedClassifierCV and show whether it pulls the reliability curve closer to the diagonal.
