Module 13

Sequences, NLP, and the Road to Attention

Every model you've built so far ate a tidy rectangle of numbers. Language is not a rectangle. It's a stream, it has order, and a word's meaning can hinge on a word ten words back. This module is the story of how the field learned to read — first by remembering one word at a time (and forgetting the start by the end), then by letting every word look at every other word at once. That second idea is attention, and it runs every chatbot you've ever used. You're going to compute one attention step with your own hands before you leave.

01 · the problem, before the lesson

One pronoun, two suspects

Read this slowly:

"The trophy didn't fit in the suitcase because it was too big."

What does "it" refer to — the trophy, or the suitcase? Don't scroll. Answer out loud, and then answer the harder question: how did you know?

You knew it's the trophy instantly. A suitcase being too big wouldn't stop something fitting inside it. But notice what your brain just did: to resolve "it," you reached back seven words to "trophy," skipped right over the equally-close "suitcase," and used world-knowledge about what fits in what. Now imagine a model that reads strictly left to right and is only allowed to remember a single running summary. By the time it hits "it," "trophy" is seven rewrites old. Good luck.

That reach-back — connecting a word to a faraway word that matters, while ignoring a nearby word that doesn't — is the entire problem of this module. Hold onto the trophy sentence. It comes back.

02 · why your old models choke

Two sentences, same words, opposite meaning

Here's the thing that should bother you. Take "dog bites man" and "man bites dog." Identical words. One is a Tuesday; the other is front-page news. Lock in a prediction before the reveal:

You feed both sentences to a model that treats its input as an unordered bag of features — "count how many times each word appears" — like a souped-up version of the tabular models from Modules 3–5. What does it see?

Both sentences have the same word counts, so a bag-of-features model receives byte-for-byte identical input and must give an identical answer. Order is gone. This is why text breaks every model so far: a house always had exactly 4 features and "feature 3" was always bedrooms (Module 3); an image was always 28×28 pixels (Module 12). Text refuses both rules — length varies (one review is 5 words, the next 500) and order carries meaning. You need machinery built for ordered, variable-length streams.

03 · the first good idea

Read with a sticky note

The first machinery that worked was recurrence: read the sequence one item at a time, left to right, keeping a running summary in your head that you rewrite at each step. Picture it literally.

Reading aloud with one sticky note. You read a sentence one word at a time. After each word you scribble a fresh summary of "the story so far" on a single sticky note — crossing out the old one and writing a new one. You never look back at earlier words, only at your latest note. By the end, the note is your understanding of the whole sentence. That sticky note has a name: the hidden state. Rewriting it at every word is exactly what a recurrent network (RNN) does.

Here's the recurrent loop as a picture. The same little box is applied again and again; each copy takes the next word and the previous note, and produces an updated note.

The same RNN cell, "unrolled" across three words. The green hidden state \( h \) (the sticky note) is the only path information can travel along — everything word 1 contributes must survive being rewritten at every later step.

The flaw is already staring at you. A single small note can only hold so much. Predict what breaks:

You read a 200-word paragraph with the sticky-note method. By the last word, how well do you remember the gist of word 1?

Word 1's contribution has to survive being squeezed and rewritten at every one of the 200 steps. Mathematically the influence tends to shrink multiplicatively at each step — that's the vanishing gradient problem from Module 11 — so long-range links fade to noise. This is the long-range memory problem, and it's the whole reason attention was invented. (The LSTM is a fancier RNN with "gates" that protect the note; it softens the fade but doesn't cure it.)

And there's a second, sneakier problem you can't see in the picture: it's inherently sequential. To compute the note at word 200 you need word 199's, which needs 198's, all the way down. You cannot do them in parallel — the loop forbids it. On modern GPUs that love doing thousands of things at once, that's a brutal bottleneck. Both of these flaws have the same root cause: everything is forced through one note, one step at a time.

04 · but first, a more basic problem

"cat" is not a number

Before any of this runs, we hit a wall: a neural network only eats numbers, and "cat" is a string of letters. We need two things — a way to chop text into pieces (tokenization) and a way to turn each piece into a vector (embeddings). Embeddings first, because they hold the prettiest idea in NLP.

The dumb way to turn a word into numbers is one-hot encoding: give every word in your vocabulary its own slot, and write a word as a vector that's 1 in its slot and 0 everywhere else. "cat" in a 50,000-word vocabulary becomes 50,000 numbers, all zero except one. Before you read on, spot the fatal flaw:

Under one-hot encoding, how far apart are "cat" and "dog" compared to "cat" and "democracy"?

Every word's 1 sits in a different slot, so "cat" is exactly as unrelated to "dog" as it is to "democracy." The vectors are gigantic and meaningless. We want the opposite: a representation where nearby means similar in meaning.

An embedding delivers exactly that. Instead of one slot per word, represent each word as a short, dense vector — say 50 to 300 numbers, none necessarily zero — and learn those numbers during training with gradient descent (Module 4). The training pressure pushes words that appear in similar contexts toward similar vectors. The result: words land in a space where nearby means similar in meaning. "cat" and "dog" end up close; "cat" and "democracy" end up far. Even directions become meaningful — the famous king − man + woman ≈ queen roughly works, because "royalty" and "gender" got encoded along consistent directions.

A map of meaning. Picture a giant map where every word is a pin. Nobody places the pins by hand; training slides them around until words used in similar ways cluster — a "kitchen" neighborhood (spoon, plate, oven), a "weather" neighborhood (rain, storm, cloudy). To ask "how related are two words?" you measure how close their pins are. The twist versus a real map: it has dozens or hundreds of dimensions, not two, so one pin can be near "fruit" along one axis and near "tech company" along another. That's how a single vector for "apple" sits between both worlds.

How do we measure "closeness"? Not raw distance — we care about direction, not length. The tool is cosine similarity: the cosine of the angle between two vectors. We'll make it precise and compute it by hand in a moment.

05 · what counts as a "word"?

Tokenization, and why "tokenization" isn't one token

Before a word becomes a vector, we have to decide what a "word" even is. That's tokenization: splitting raw text into the units a model processes, called tokens. Two obvious choices both fail. Predict which way each one breaks:

You tokenize by splitting on whole words. A user types the typo "teh", an emoji, and the word "antidisestablishmentarianism". What goes wrong?

Whole-word tokenizing means a giant vocabulary and an "unknown" token for everything novel — the model learns nothing from "teh" or that new product name. And "run"/"running"/"runs" share no structure. (Option C is the failure mode of the opposite extreme — single characters — where vocabulary is tiny and nothing is ever unknown, but every word becomes many tokens, sequences get long and expensive, and the model must rediscover that "c-a-t" is a meaningful chunk.)

Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) is the compromise nearly every modern model uses: tokens are sub-word chunks, learned from data. The recipe is almost insultingly simple:

  1. Break all text into individual characters.
  2. Find the most frequent adjacent pair of symbols. Merge it into one new symbol. Record the merge.
  3. Repeat a fixed number of times (e.g. 30,000 merges).

Frequent pieces like "th", "ing", and common whole words like "the" get merged early and become single tokens; rare words get left as several smaller pieces. The payoff: common words are one token (efficient), rare or novel words gracefully fall back to known sub-word pieces (no "unknown" token ever needed), and related words share structure — "running" might be run + ning, sharing run with "runs."

The one thing to burn in now: tokens are usually not whole words. "tokenization" itself splits into more than one token (you'll see it land as two pieces below), and a space is often glued onto the front of the next token. This trips up almost every beginner — and you'll see it live in the library code below.

06 · the breakthrough

What if every word could just look at every other word?

Back to the wall. RNNs (and LSTMs) carried the field for years but never escaped their two flaws: forgetful over long ranges, and stuck being sequential. Both come from forcing everything through one note, one step at a time. So ask the heretical question:

Why force information through a single bottleneck note at all?

What if, when processing the word "it," the model could look directly at every other word in the sentence at the same time and decide, for each one, "how much should this word inform my understanding of 'it' right now?" No relay race. No fading. Every word reachable in one hop, and all the looking happening in parallel. That direct, all-to-all, learnable looking-up is self-attention — and it's about to dissolve both RNN problems at once.

Lock this in. The two RNN flaws — fading memory and forced-sequential — are the exact two things attention fixes. When you meet the full Transformer in Module 14, every component is there to make this one looking-up operation work at scale. Attention is the engine; Module 14 is the rest of the car.

Mission 4. Every row sums to 1.00, always. Softmax guarantees it. A query distributes a fixed budget of attention — it can't attend "a lot" to everything; spending more on one token means less for the rest.

You just watched every query's row sum to 1. Why is that not a coincidence — what forces it?

Softmax of a row \([s_1, s_2, \dots]\) is \(\big[\frac{e^{s_1}}{\sum_j e^{s_j}}, \frac{e^{s_2}}{\sum_j e^{s_j}}, \dots\big]\). Every term is positive (it's \(e\) to a power) and they share the same denominator — the sum of all of them — so they add to exactly 1. That's what makes attention weights a genuine distribution: a fixed budget of attention split across tokens. You saw the same softmax do classification in Module 5; here it splits attention instead of class probability.

08 · now (and only now) the math

You felt it. Here's the notation.

Two formulas earn their place now: how we measure word similarity, and the attention operation you just drove.

Cosine similarity

For two vectors \( \mathbf{a} \) and \( \mathbf{b} \) (bold = "a list of numbers"), cosine similarity is the cosine of the angle between them:

\[ \cos(\theta) = \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{\lVert \mathbf{a} \rVert \, \lVert \mathbf{b} \rVert} \]

a·b the dot product — multiply element by element, add up the results (Module 1). ‖a‖ ("the norm of a") the vector's length, \( \sqrt{a_1^2 + a_2^2 + \dots} \). Dividing the dot product by both lengths cancels how long the vectors are, leaving only how aligned their directions are.

Value near \(1\) means "pointing the same way" (similar); near \(0\) means "perpendicular" (unrelated); near \(-1\) means "opposite." It ignores length on purpose — a word that appears a lot might get a longer vector, but we care about which way it points, not how big it is.

Self-attention

Self-attention starts from a sequence of token vectors — \( n \) tokens, each a vector. From each one we produce three new vectors by multiplying it with three learned weight matrices:

  • a Query \( \mathbf{q} \) — "what am I, this token, looking for?"
  • a Key \( \mathbf{k} \) — "what do I, this token, advertise to others who might look at me?"
  • a Value \( \mathbf{v} \) — "if someone attends to me, here's the actual information I hand over."

It's a database metaphor: a query is matched against keys, and you retrieve the matching values — except the match is soft (every key matches a little) and the weights are learned. Stack the per-token vectors as rows to get matrices \( Q \), \( K \), \( V \). The whole operation is one compact line:

\[ \text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\!\left( \frac{Q K^{\top}}{\sqrt{d_k}} \right) V \]

QKᵀ every query dotted with every key → an \(n\times n\) score matrix. ÷√dₖ shrink the scores so softmax isn't too spiky (\(d_k\) = key length, often 64). softmax turn each row into weights that sum to 1. ×V mix the values by those weights.

Read it left to right, because every symbol earns its keep:

  • \( Q K^{\top} \) — queries times the transpose (flip, rows↔columns) of the keys. Entry \((i,j)\) is the dot product of token \(i\)'s query with token \(j\)'s key: a raw score for "how much should token \(i\) attend to token \(j\)?" High when their directions align. For \(n\) tokens this is an \(n \times n\) score matrix — every token scored against every token.
  • \( \div \sqrt{d_k} \) — divide by the square root of the key dimension. In high dimensions, dot products get large, which would make softmax too "spiky" (all weight on one token). Dividing keeps the numbers tame.
  • \( \text{softmax}(\cdot) \) — applied to each row: turn raw scores into positive weights that sum to 1 (Module 5). Now row \(i\) reads "I, token \(i\), put 70% of my attention on token A, 30% on token B." These are the attention weights you drove in the lab.
  • \( \times V \) — each token's output is a weighted average of all the value vectors, weighted by how much it chose to attend to each. Token "it" pulls mostly the value of "trophy" and walks away with a representation infused with "trophy."

That's the entire mechanism — and both RNN problems vanish at once. Every token reaches every other token in one step (no fading relay), and the whole \(n \times n\) computation is one big matrix multiply a GPU does in parallel (no sequential loop). The price — flag it now, pay it in Module 14 — is that an \(n \times n\) score matrix means cost grows with the square of the sequence length. Double the text, quadruple the work. That quadratic cost is the central tension in every long-context model.

The trophy sentence, solved. Self-attention from "it" learns to point mostly at "trophy" (0.62), some at "suitcase" (0.28), almost none at "the." These are the softmaxed scores — and yes, they sum to 1.

09 · do it by hand

One full attention pass, no computer

Before you trust the code, grind the numbers yourself. Two parts. Work each line before you read the next.

Part A — cosine similarity

Compare \( \mathbf{a} = [1, 0] \) and \( \mathbf{b} = [0.7, 0.7] \).

  • Dot product: \( (1)(0.7) + (0)(0.7) = 0.7 \).
  • Lengths: \( \lVert \mathbf{a} \rVert = \sqrt{1^2 + 0^2} = 1 \); \( \lVert \mathbf{b} \rVert = \sqrt{0.7^2 + 0.7^2} = \sqrt{0.98} \approx 0.99 \).
  • Cosine \( = \dfrac{0.7}{1 \times 0.99} \approx 0.707 \).

That's exactly \( \cos 45^\circ \) — right, because \( [0.7, 0.7] \) points up-and-right at 45° while \( [1,0] \) points straight right. Somewhat similar; pointing partly the same way.

Part B — self-attention for 2 tokens

Two tokens with tiny 2-D Q, K, V vectors. To keep the arithmetic clean we'll skip the \( \sqrt{d_k} \) scaling here (the code below puts it back, and you'll see exactly what it changes).

  • Queries: \( \mathbf{q}_1 = [1, 0] \), \( \mathbf{q}_2 = [0.5, 0.5] \)
  • Keys: \( \mathbf{k}_1 = [1, 1] \), \( \mathbf{k}_2 = [0, 2] \)
  • Values: \( \mathbf{v}_1 = [2, 0] \), \( \mathbf{v}_2 = [0, 4] \)

Step 1 — score every query against every key (the \( Q K^{\top} \) matrix). Each entry is a dot product:

  • \( \mathbf{q}_1 \cdot \mathbf{k}_1 = (1)(1) + (0)(1) = 1 \)    \( \mathbf{q}_1 \cdot \mathbf{k}_2 = (1)(0) + (0)(2) = 0 \)
  • \( \mathbf{q}_2 \cdot \mathbf{k}_1 = (0.5)(1) + (0.5)(1) = 1 \)    \( \mathbf{q}_2 \cdot \mathbf{k}_2 = (0.5)(0) + (0.5)(2) = 1 \)

Score matrix \( \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \). Row 1 is token 1's scores against tokens 1 and 2; row 2 is token 2's.

Step 2 — softmax each row into attention weights. Row 1, \( [1, 0] \): \( e^{1} = 2.718 \), \( e^{0} = 1 \), sum \( = 3.718 \). Weights \( = [2.718/3.718,\; 1/3.718] = [0.731,\; 0.269] \). Token 1 attends 73% to itself, 27% to token 2 — sensible, since its query \([1,0]\) aligns more with key \([1,1]\) than \([0,2]\). Row 2, \( [1, 1] \): scores equal, so weights split evenly \( [0.5,\; 0.5] \).

Step 3 — mix the values with those weights (the \( \times V \) step). Each output is a weighted average of \( \mathbf{v}_1 = [2,0] \) and \( \mathbf{v}_2 = [0,4] \):

  • Token 1 \( = 0.731 \cdot [2, 0] + 0.269 \cdot [0, 4] = [1.462, 0] + [0, 1.076] = [1.462,\; 1.076] \).
  • Token 2 \( = 0.5 \cdot [2, 0] + 0.5 \cdot [0, 4] = [1, 0] + [0, 2] = [1,\; 2] \).

That's a complete self-attention pass. Each token left with a new vector blending information from every token, weighted by learned relevance. Every number here is reproduced by the code below.

10 · debug the loop

This attention code runs. It's wrong.

A beginner wrote this to reproduce the by-hand example. It throws no error — and the weights come out backwards (the token that should dominate gets the least attention). Find the bug before opening the answer. Read every line.

import numpy as np def softmax(x): e = np.exp(x - x.max(axis=-1, keepdims=True)) return e / e.sum(axis=-1, keepdims=True) Q = np.array([[1.0, 0.0], [0.5, 0.5]]) K = np.array([[1.0, 1.0], [0.0, 2.0]]) V = np.array([[2.0, 0.0], [0.0, 4.0]]) scores = Q @ K # <-- something is wrong on this line weights = softmax(scores) output = weights @ V print(weights)
Show the bug & why

Q @ K multiplies the queries by the keys without transposing K. The formula is \( Q K^{\top} \) — you need Q @ K.T. Entry \((i,j)\) must be query \(i\) dotted with key \(j\); without the transpose you dot query \(i\) with the wrong column and get a different matrix that still has the right shape (2×2), so NumPy happily runs and prints garbage. This is the most common attention bug there is: a shape that's correct but a meaning that's wrong. Fix: scores = Q @ K.T. (And in a real model you'd also divide by np.sqrt(d_k) — see the next section.)

11 · from scratch, correctly

Predict the output, then run it

First, cosine similarity on a toy "embedding" where two of three words are meant to be related. We hand-pick the vectors (real ones are learned — see pitfalls).

import numpy as np def cosine(a, b): return np.dot(a, b) / (np.linalg.norm(a) * np.linalg.norm(b)) # pretend these tiny 2D vectors came out of a trained embedding cat = np.array([0.9, 0.1]) dog = np.array([0.8, 0.2]) car = np.array([0.1, 0.95]) print(f"cat vs dog: {cosine(cat, dog):.3f}") # animals -> high? print(f"cat vs car: {cosine(cat, car):.3f}") # unrelated -> low?

Predict first: will "cat vs dog" be near 1 or near 0? What about "cat vs car"? Now check: it prints cat vs dog: 0.991 and cat vs car: 0.214. The two animals score near 1 (their pins sit close on the map of meaning); "cat" and "car" — similar spelling, unrelated meaning — score low. That gap is the entire point of embeddings: similarity reflects meaning, not letters.

Now self-attention, same three-step recipe, this time with the \( \sqrt{d_k} \) scaling real models use. Same Q, K, V as the worked example, so you can match the (scaled) numbers.

import numpy as np def softmax(x): # subtract the row max for numerical stability, then normalize each row e = np.exp(x - x.max(axis=-1, keepdims=True)) return e / e.sum(axis=-1, keepdims=True) # two tokens, each with a 2D query, key, and value (stacked as rows) Q = np.array([[1.0, 0.0], [0.5, 0.5]]) K = np.array([[1.0, 1.0], [0.0, 2.0]]) V = np.array([[2.0, 0.0], [0.0, 4.0]]) d_k = Q.shape[1] # key/query dimension = 2 scores = Q @ K.T / np.sqrt(d_k) # 2x2 raw scores, scaled weights = softmax(scores) # each row sums to 1 output = weights @ V # weighted mix of the value vectors print("scaled scores:\n", np.round(scores, 3)) print("attention weights:\n", np.round(weights, 3)) print("output:\n", np.round(output, 3))

Output:

scaled scores: [[0.707 0. ] [0.707 0.707]] attention weights: [[0.67 0.33 ] [0.5 0.5 ]] output: [[1.34 1.321] [1. 2. ]]

Compare to your by-hand result. The weights are \( [0.67, 0.33] \) here instead of \( [0.731, 0.269] \), and token 1's output is \( [1.34, 1.321] \) instead of \( [1.462, 1.076] \). Shapes match exactly; the small differences come entirely from the \( \div \sqrt{2} \) scaling we skipped by hand — dividing shrinks the gap between scores, so softmax produces gentler, less peaky weights. Token 2's scores were already equal, so scaling leaves its 50/50 split and its \( [1, 2] \) output untouched. This three-line core — scores → softmax → mix values — is, no exaggeration, the heart of every transformer in Module 14.

12 · the library way

Real tokens, real embedding lookup

You'll almost never hand-roll tokenization; you load a tokenizer that ships with a pretrained model. Hugging Face's AutoTokenizer downloads the right one and turns a sentence into tokens and integer IDs. (Install with pip install transformers; the first run downloads a small tokenizer file.)

from transformers import AutoTokenizer tok = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained("bert-base-uncased") sentence = "Tokenization isn't magic." tokens = tok.tokenize(sentence) # human-readable sub-word pieces ids = tok.encode(sentence) # integer ids the model actually eats print(tokens) print(ids) print(tok.convert_ids_to_tokens(ids)) # ids back to tokens (with specials)

Predict before you read on: how many tokens does the single word "Tokenization" become? Now look:

['token', '##ization', 'isn', "'", 't', 'magic', '.'] [101, 19204, 3989, 3475, 1005, 1056, 3894, 1012, 102] ['[CLS]', 'token', '##ization', 'isn', "'", 't', 'magic', '.', '[SEP]']

Everything from the BPE section comes alive. "Tokenization" becomes two tokens, token + ##ization — the ## marks "this continues the previous word," exactly the sub-word behavior we wanted. "isn't" breaks into isn + ' + t. And the model's own bookends [CLS] and [SEP] get added automatically (that's why ids has 9 entries but tokens has 7). Every token maps to an integer ID — and those IDs are what get looked up in the embedding table next.

That lookup is one line in PyTorch. torch.nn.Embedding is literally a learnable table with one row per vocabulary entry; index it with token IDs, get back embedding vectors that flow into the network and get trained by gradient descent.

import torch import torch.nn as nn vocab_size, dim = 30522, 8 # bert vocab size, tiny 8D embeddings embedding = nn.Embedding(vocab_size, dim) ids = torch.tensor([101, 19204, 3989]) # a few token ids from above vectors = embedding(ids) # shape: (3 tokens, 8 numbers each) print(vectors.shape) # torch.Size([3, 8])

Prints torch.Size([3, 8]): three token IDs in, three 8-dimensional vectors out. The values start random and become meaningful only after training — which is the whole point of the first pitfall below: embeddings are learned, never hand-written.

13 · traps everyone falls in

Common pitfalls

  • "Embeddings are hand-designed dictionaries." No — nobody decides "cat" should be \([0.9, 0.1]\). Embeddings start random and are learned by gradient descent alongside the rest of the model; the geometry (cat near dog) emerges from training on lots of text. The toy vectors here were hand-picked only to illustrate cosine similarity.
  • Confusing tokens with words. A token is usually a sub-word. "tokenization" is several tokens; a leading space is often part of a token. "A 4,000-token limit" is not 4,000 words — often closer to 3,000 English words. Always check the actual tokenizer output.
  • Thinking BPE understands grammar. BPE is pure frequency-counting on character sequences — it merges common adjacent pairs and knows nothing about syntax or meaning. The meaning all arrives later, from embeddings and attention operating on the resulting tokens.
  • "Attention is just a fancy RNN." Opposites in spirit. An RNN passes information step by step through one hidden state (sequential, forgetful). Attention lets every token read every other token directly and simultaneously (parallel, no fading). That difference is exactly why transformers replaced RNNs.
  • Forgetting attention is quadratic. The score matrix is \(n \times n\), so doubling the input roughly quadruples compute and memory. This is why context windows are a big deal and "efficient attention" is an active research area — back in Module 14.
  • Mixing up Q, K, and V. Query asks ("what am I looking for?"), key advertises ("what do I offer?"), value delivers ("here's my content"). The score uses query·key; the output is built from values. Swap them and you get wrong results that still run without error — the worst kind of bug.

Softmax: exponentiate each score, divide by the total. Positive numbers sharing one denominator add to exactly 1 — a fixed attention budget split across tokens.
Why is "tokenization" not one token?
tap to flip
BPE uses sub-word tokens learned by merging frequent character pairs. Rare/long words fall back to several pieces (e.g. token + ##ization), so no "unknown" token is ever needed.
What's the hidden cost of attention, and where does it come from?
tap to flip
Quadratic in sequence length: the score matrix is \(n\times n\), so doubling the text quadruples compute and memory. That's why context windows matter.
Cosine similarity near 1, near 0, near −1 — what does each mean?
tap to flip
1 = same direction (similar). 0 = perpendicular (unrelated). −1 = opposite. It measures direction, ignoring vector length.

16 · stretch yourself

Exercises

  1. Compute the cosine similarity of \( \mathbf{a} = [3, 4] \) and \( \mathbf{b} = [4, 3] \) by hand. More or less similar than \( [1, 0] \) and \( [0.7, 0.7] \) from the worked example?
    Show solution

    Dot product: \( (3)(4) + (4)(3) = 24 \). Lengths: \( \lVert \mathbf{a} \rVert = \sqrt{9 + 16} = 5 \); \( \lVert \mathbf{b} \rVert = \sqrt{16 + 9} = 5 \). Cosine \( = 24 / (5 \times 5) = 24/25 = 0.96 \). Much closer to 1 than the worked example's \(0.707\), so these are more similar — a small angle (about 16°) versus 45°.

  2. Run one BPE merge by hand on the single word "banana" (character sequence b a n a n a). What's the most frequent adjacent pair, and what does one merge produce?
    Show solution

    Adjacent pairs: (b,a), (a,n), (n,a), (a,n), (n,a). Counts: (a,n) twice, (n,a) twice, (b,a) once — a tie between "an" and "na" at 2 each. BPE breaks ties by a fixed rule (e.g. first encountered), so say we merge "an". The sequence becomes b · an · an · a — four symbols instead of six. Repeated structure ("an") gets compressed into one reusable token; that's exactly why BPE makes frequent patterns cheap.

  3. The raw score matrix (before softmax) for two tokens is \( \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \). Softmax each row. Which token is more "decisive" about where it looks, and why?
    Show solution

    Row 1, \([2, 0]\): \( e^2 = 7.389 \), \( e^0 = 1 \), sum \(= 8.389\). Weights \(= [0.881,\; 0.119]\). Row 2, \([0, 0]\): both \(e^0 = 1\), sum \(= 2\), weights \(= [0.5,\; 0.5]\). Token 1 is far more decisive — 88% on token 1, only 12% on token 2 — because its scores differ a lot. Token 2's scores are equal, so it spreads attention evenly (maximum uncertainty). Larger score gaps → sharper attention.

  4. Continuing exercise 3, with value vectors \( \mathbf{v}_1 = [10, 0] \) and \( \mathbf{v}_2 = [0, 10] \), compute each token's output vector.
    Show solution

    Token 1, weights \([0.881, 0.119]\): \( 0.881 \cdot [10, 0] + 0.119 \cdot [0, 10] = [8.81,\; 1.19] \). Token 2, weights \([0.5, 0.5]\): \( [5,\; 5] \). Token 1, attending mostly to \( \mathbf{v}_1 \), comes out near \([10, 0]\); token 2, attending equally, lands exactly between. The output is always a weighted average of the values.

  5. Explain in 3–4 sentences why a plain RNN struggles with "The keys that I left on the kitchen table this morning before the long meeting are gone," linking "keys" to "are." Then say how self-attention handles the same link.
    Show solution

    For the RNN to make "are" (plural) agree with "keys," information about "keys" must travel through every word in between — ~12 of them — surviving the hidden state being rewritten at each step. Over that distance "keys" tends to fade (long-range memory / vanishing-gradient), so the agreement is fragile. Self-attention removes the relay: processing "are," the model computes a direct score between "are" and "keys" and places high weight on it in one hop, regardless of the 12 words between. Distance no longer dilutes the connection.
