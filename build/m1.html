Module 1

Just-Enough Math Intuition

You have been told ML needs years of math. That is a lie people repeat to feel smart. The truth: three little ideas — dot products, derivatives, distributions — carry you most of the way, and you can literally see all three. This module is not a gate you must scale. It is a reference you will keep coming back to. Today you build the pictures so the formulas later have somewhere to live.

How to use this. Do not memorize this page. Skim it once, beat the boss, then come back when a later module says "remember the dot product" or "this is the chain rule." Each idea here gets re-summoned the exact moment a real problem needs it — which is when it finally makes sense.
01 · before any theory

A song, two lists of numbers, one score

A music app wants to guess if you'll like a track. It stores the song as [loudness, tempo, energy] = [0.8, 0.3, 0.9]. It stores your taste as [0.2, 0.9, 0.7] — you mildly like loud, love fast, like energetic.

It needs one number: a match score. No theory yet. Just answer the human question — how would you turn two lists like that into a single "how well do these match" number? Commit to a method in your head before you tap.

Two lists, [0.8, 0.3, 0.9] and [0.2, 0.9, 0.7]. Which recipe gives the most sensible single "match" score?

C. Multiplying matching slots rewards agreement: when both lists are big in the same slot, that slot contributes a lot; when one is near zero, that slot barely counts. Adding everything (A) ignores which list is which. Subtracting (B) measures distance, not match — a real tool, but a different one. The recipe in C has a name you'll use for the rest of your life in ML: the dot product. You just invented it.

02 · now the notation

Vectors are arrows, dot products are agreement

That list of numbers has a name: a vector. That's the whole definition — an ordered list. We write it in brackets and give it a bold name: \( \mathbf{v} = [3, 2] \). The numbers inside are its components; how many there are is its dimension.

Here's the part that makes it click: a 2D vector is also an arrow. Start at the origin, walk 3 right and 2 up — the tip lands at \((3,2)\). A vector is two things at once: a list you compute with, and a direction-and-length you can picture. Think walking directions — "3 blocks east, 2 blocks north" is both a list and a path.

\[ \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + a_3 b_3 \]

a, b two vectors of the same length.   a₁ the first component of a; b₁ the first of b.   · the dot product: multiply matching slots, add the results. The output is one plain number — a scalar, never a vector.

Read it out loud: "line them up, multiply across, add it all into one number." Big and positive means the two arrows mostly point the same way (strong agreement). Near zero means they're roughly perpendicular — unrelated. This single operation is the core of linear regression (Module 3), logistic regression (Module 5), and every neuron in every network (Module 10). Learn it once, reuse it forever.

DO Predict, then compute — by hand

Let \( \mathbf{a} = [1, 2, 3] \) and \( \mathbf{b} = [4, 5, 6] \). Before you read on: do the three multiplies and the sum on paper. What do you get?

Check my dot product

Multiply matching components, then add:

\[ \mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} = (1)(4) + (2)(5) + (3)(6) = 4 + 10 + 18 = 32 \]

The answer is the single number 32. Notice you never produced a new vector — a dot product always collapses to one scalar. If you got 32, you can do the operation that powers half of ML.

03 · machines that bend space

A matrix grabs the whole plane and tugs

A matrix is a grid of numbers — rows and columns. The shallow way to read it is "a table." The deep way (the 3Blue1Brown way) is a transformation of space: feed it a vector, it hands back a new vector, as if it grabbed the entire plane — a stretchy rubber sheet with a grid on it — and stretched, squished, or rotated it. Every point rides along. Different matrices, different tugs.

To apply a matrix \( M \) to a vector \( \mathbf{v} \), you dot each row of the matrix with the vector. That's the payoff of learning the dot product first:

\[ \begin{bmatrix} m_{11} & m_{12} \\ m_{21} & m_{22} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} m_{11}v_1 + m_{12}v_2 \\ m_{21}v_1 + m_{22}v_2 \end{bmatrix} \]

m₁₁ the entry in row 1, column 1 (and so on).   v₁, v₂ the vector's components.   New top = row 1 dotted with the vector; new bottom = row 2 dotted with the vector. A matrix-vector product is just several dot products stacked up.

Before the lab, lock in a guess about what a transformation can do to space:

A 2×2 matrix turns the unit square into a parallelogram. The "size of the tug" is the determinant — the signed area of that new shape. What does a negative determinant mean geometrically?

B. The sign of the determinant tracks orientation. Picture the two little arrows that ride along the axes — î (one step right) and ĵ (one step up), the unit basis vectors. Positive determinant: space keeps its handedness (î stays counter-clockwise from ĵ, the way they start). Negative: the transformation flipped the plane over, like a mirror — î and ĵ swapped sides. The magnitude is still the area scale; the minus sign is the flip. (C describes determinant zero, which is the next thing you'll hunt down in the lab — and it's a much bigger deal than it looks.)

LAB Tug the plane — 3 missions

Change the four numbers of the matrix (or hit a preset like Rotate or Shear) and watch the grid and the basis vectors î, ĵ glide to their new home. The shaded square is the image of the unit square — its signed area is the determinant. Don't just wiggle sliders. Hunt the targets below.

  • Make the determinant go negative What happened to the grid? It flipped — î and ĵ swapped which side they're on. You mirror-imaged space.
  • Drive the determinant to exactly det = 0 Make the two rows point the same direction (e.g. [1,2] and [2,4]). The whole 2D plane collapses onto a single line.
  • Build a pure shear: keep det = 1 but slant the grid Try [[1,1],[0,1]]. Area is unchanged, yet every point slides sideways in proportion to its height.
Why is det = 0 such a big deal?

When det = 0, the transformation squashes the whole 2D plane down onto a 1D line (or even a single point). Information is destroyed — many different input vectors land on the exact same output, so you can never undo it. That's the math behind "this system has no unique solution" and "this matrix isn't invertible." You'll meet it again the first time a model refuses to train because its features are secretly redundant.

DO Predict, then compute — matrix × vector

Let \( M = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \) and \( \mathbf{v} = [4, 5] \) (read vertically as a column). Where does this matrix move the tip of the arrow \([4,5]\)? Do the two row-dots on paper first.

Check my answer

Row 1 dotted with \( \mathbf{v} \): \( (2)(4) + (0)(5) = 8 + 0 = 8 \).
Row 2 dotted with \( \mathbf{v} \): \( (1)(4) + (3)(5) = 4 + 15 = 19 \).

\[ M\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 8 \\ 19 \end{bmatrix} \]

The matrix took the arrow \([4,5]\) and moved its tip to \([8,19]\) — "transformation of space" in action, done with two little dot products. That's all a matrix-vector product ever is.

04 · which way is better?

A derivative is just "feel the slope under your feet"

Stand on a hill in thick fog. You can't see the valley, but you can feel how steep the ground is right under your feet, and which way it tilts. That feeling — nudge the input a tiny bit, how much and which way does the output change — is a derivative. It's "rise over run": change in output divided by change in input. Positive slope: output rises as input rises. Negative: it falls.

This is the most important idea for learning. A model improves by endlessly asking "which way should I nudge my numbers to shrink my error?" That question is a derivative. It is the entire engine of Module 4.

Quick gut-check before the lab:

You're standing on the curve \(f(x)=x^2\) at \(x = -2\) (left wall of the bowl). You take one tiny step to the right. What does the height do — so what's the sign of the slope there?

A. On the left wall of the bowl, walking right takes you downhill toward the bottom at \(x=0\), so the slope is negative. Plug in the rule \(f'(x)=2x\): at \(x=-2\) that's \(2(-2) = -4\). Negative, just like you felt. The slope is zero only at the very bottom (\(x=0\)), and positive on the right wall. The sign of the derivative literally tells you which way is downhill — which is exactly how a model knows which way to step.

For \( f(x) = x^2 \), the slope at any point turns out to be \( 2x \). We write \( f'(x) = 2x \), where the tick mark means "the derivative of \(f\)." So at \(x=3\), slope \(= f'(3) = 6\). You don't need to know why the rule is \(2x\) yet (that's optional calculus) — and you can find the slope with zero calculus, the way a computer does:

\[ \text{slope} \approx \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} \]

h a tiny step (like 0.001).   f(x+h) − f(x) the "rise" — how much the output moved.   h the "run."   ≈ approximately equal; the smaller \(h\) gets, the closer this lands on the true slope. This is the literal definition of a derivative with the "shrink \(h\) to zero" step left implied.

LAB Secant chases the tangent — 3 missions

Drag the blue point to choose x₀, then shrink the step h (or press "animate h → 0") and watch the orange secant line swing onto the green tangent. The secant slope marches toward the true derivative \(f'(x_0)\).

  • Drag x₀ until the slope readout shows 0 Where is it? The bottom of the bowl, x₀ = 0. Slope zero = flat = you've arrived. Remember this — it's how gradient descent knows to stop.
  • Park x₀ at 3, then shrink h toward zero Watch the secant slope crawl toward 6.00 = the true f′(3). Big h overshoots; tiny h nails it.
  • Move x₀ to the left of zero and read the slope's sign It goes negative — exactly the prediction you just locked in.
DO Predict, then compute — derivative of x² at x=3, two ways

Analytically the rule says \(f'(x)=2x\), so \(f'(3)=6\). Now the numerical route with \(h=0.001\): you need \(f(3.001)\) and \(f(3)\). Predict whether the estimate lands exactly on 6, a hair above, or a hair below — then compute.

Check my numerical estimate

\( f(3.001) = 3.001^2 = 9.006001 \), and \( f(3) = 3^2 = 9 \). Then:

\[ \frac{9.006001 - 9}{0.001} = \frac{0.006001}{0.001} = 6.001 \]

The estimate 6.001 is a hair above the true answer 6 — exactly what we expect for a positive step \(h\) on an upward-curving function, and it gets closer as \(h\) shrinks. Two completely different routes, the same answer. That agreement is the whole point: you can always sanity-check calculus with arithmetic.

One knob at a time: partials and the gradient

Real models have many inputs. A partial derivative asks "how does the output change if I nudge just one input and freeze all the others?" Written \( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \) (the curly \( \partial \) signals "partial"). Same slope idea, one knob at a time. Stack all the partials into a list and you get the gradient — a vector of slopes pointing in the direction of steepest increase. Flip its sign and you're heading downhill. That flip is gradient descent (Module 4). Watch the ideas snap together: partials are slopes, a gradient is a vector of slopes.

The chain rule, the ML way: nudges multiply along a chain

Often one thing depends on another, which depends on another — a chain. A change in \(x\) moves \(u\); a change in \(u\) moves \(y\). How much does \(y\) move when you nudge \(x\)? You multiply the local slopes:

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du}\cdot\frac{du}{dx} \]

dy/du how strongly \(y\) responds to \(u\).   du/dx how strongly \(u\) responds to \(x\).   Multiply them for the total. Think gears: turn the small gear once, it spins the next twice, that spins the last five times — one turn in, ten turns out. You multiply the gear ratios.

If nudging \(x\) by 1 moves \(u\) by 2, and nudging \(u\) by 1 moves \(y\) by 5, then nudging \(x\) by 1 moves \(y\) by \(5 \times 2 = 10\). This is the engine of backpropagation (Modules 10 & 11): a deep network is a long chain, and to learn it sends an error signal backward, multiplying local slopes as it goes. Hold the picture, skip the machinery: nudges multiply along a chain.

05 · spread and chance

Mean is the center, variance is the scatter

Data is noisy, so ML speaks probability — but you need shockingly little to start. A random variable is a quantity whose value isn't fixed (a dice roll, tomorrow's visitors). A distribution says which values are likely. A probability is a number from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain); 0.5 is a coin flip. All the probabilities of the possible outcomes must add to 1 — something has to happen.

Two numbers describe most data. The mean \(\mu\) ("mu") is the center: add everything, divide by how many. The variance \(\sigma^2\) is the spread: for each number take its distance from the mean, square it (so negatives don't cancel and big misses count extra), then average those squares. Its square root \(\sigma\) is the standard deviation — the everyday "typical distance from average," back in the original units.

\[ \mu = \frac{x_1 + \cdots + x_n}{n} \qquad \sigma^2 = \frac{(x_1-\mu)^2 + \cdots + (x_n-\mu)^2}{n} \]

n how many numbers.   μ the mean (center).   σ² the variance (average squared distance from the mean).   σ its square root, the standard deviation. Small σ: tightly clustered. Large σ: widely scattered.

Why do we square the distances in variance instead of just averaging the raw distances from the mean?

C. By the definition of the mean, the distances above it exactly cancel the distances below it — their average is always zero, no matter how scattered the data. Useless as a spread measure. Squaring makes every distance positive (and makes far-out points count extra), so spread finally shows up. This is exactly the "forgetting to square" trap that bites beginners — and the bug you'll hunt in a minute.

DO Predict, then compute — mean & variance of [2, 4, 6]

Eyeball it first: the mean is obviously the middle, 4. Now predict the variance before grinding it out — bigger or smaller than 3? Then compute.

Check my work

Mean: \( \mu = \dfrac{2 + 4 + 6}{3} = \dfrac{12}{3} = 4 \).

Distances from the mean: \( 2-4 = -2 \), \( 4-4 = 0 \), \( 6-4 = 2 \).

Square them: \( (-2)^2 = 4 \), \( 0^2 = 0 \), \( 2^2 = 4 \).

Average the squares: \( \sigma^2 = \dfrac{4 + 0 + 4}{3} = \dfrac{8}{3} \approx 2.667 \).

Standard deviation: \( \sigma = \sqrt{2.667} \approx 1.63 \) — the numbers sit, typically, about 1.63 from the center of 4. Matches intuition: two are exactly 2 away, one is dead-on the mean.

The bell curve, in one breath

The normal distribution is the famous symmetric "bell." It shows up everywhere — heights, measurement errors, exam scores — because sums of many small random effects pile up into this shape. It needs just two numbers: the mean \(\mu\) (where the peak sits) and the standard deviation \(\sigma\) (how wide). Handy rule: about 68% of values land within one \(\sigma\) of the mean, about 95% within two. When ML says "noise," it usually means "normally distributed wiggle around the true value."

06 · make it run

From scratch: predict the output, then run it

Every number this code prints, you computed by hand above. Before you run it, write down what each print should say. If your prediction and the output disagree, one of you is wrong — find out which.

import numpy as np # --- Vectors --- a = np.array([1, 2, 3]) b = np.array([4, 5, 6]) # Dot product BY HAND (multiply matching parts, then add) dot_manual = a[0]*b[0] + a[1]*b[1] + a[2]*b[2] print("dot (by hand):", dot_manual) # predict: ? # --- Matrix times a vector, by hand (two dot products) --- M = np.array([[2, 0], [1, 3]]) v = np.array([4, 5]) row0 = M[0, 0]*v[0] + M[0, 1]*v[1] # 2*4 + 0*5 = ? row1 = M[1, 0]*v[0] + M[1, 1]*v[1] # 1*4 + 3*5 = ? print("M @ v (by hand):", [row0, row1]) # predict: ? # --- Numerical derivative of f(x) = x**2 at x = 3 --- def f(x): return x**2 x = 3.0 h = 0.001 slope = (f(x + h) - f(x)) / h # rise over run print("numerical slope at x=3:", round(slope, 4)) # predict: ? print("true slope (2x):", 2*x) # predict: ?

It prints dot (by hand): 32, M @ v (by hand): [8, 19], numerical slope 6.001, and true slope 6.0 — every worked example above, confirmed. Seeing the by-hand arithmetic and the code agree is how you build trust that you understand the operation, not just the syntax.

The library way: one call each

Once you understand the mechanics, the library does the bookkeeping. Same answers, one line apiece.

import numpy as np a = np.array([1, 2, 3]) b = np.array([4, 5, 6]) print("dot:", a @ b) # 32 (the @ operator = dot/matmul) print("dot:", np.dot(a, b)) # 32 (same thing, named) M = np.array([[2, 0], [1, 3]]) v = np.array([4, 5]) print("M @ v:", M @ v) # [ 8 19] # Mean and variance with one call each data = np.array([2, 4, 6]) print("mean:", data.mean()) # 4.0 print("variance:", data.var()) # 2.6666... (the 8/3 from our worked example) print("std dev:", data.std()) # 1.632... # Numerical derivative across many points at once. # np.gradient estimates the slope of y with respect to x at each point. x = np.linspace(0, 5, 1001) # 1001 evenly spaced x-values from 0 to 5 y = x**2 dydx = np.gradient(y, x) # array of slopes; should look like 2*x i = np.argmin(np.abs(x - 3)) # find the index where x is closest to 3 print("slope near x=3:", round(dydx[i], 3)) # ~6.0

The @ operator gives the dot product (32) and the matrix-vector product ([8, 19]) instantly; .mean() and .var() reproduce our hand-computed 4.0 and 8/3; and np.gradient returns a whole array of slopes tracking \(2x\), reading about 6.0 near \(x=3\). Same answers as the from-scratch version — the payoff of understanding the idea first, then letting the library handle the grind.

07 · find the bug

This variance function looks fine. It lies.

A beginner wrote this to compute variance. It runs, returns a number, and the number is wrong every single time — it reports near-zero spread for data that's clearly scattered. Read it, find the bug before opening the answer, then mentally test it on \([2,4,6]\) (you know the answer is \(8/3\)).

def variance(nums): n = len(nums) mean = sum(nums) / n total = 0 for x in nums: total += x - mean # <-- something is wrong on this line return total / n
Show the bug & why

The line adds the raw distance x - mean instead of its square. The raw distances always cancel to zero (that's the whole reason we square — see the predict card above), so this returns ~0 for any data. Fix it to total += (x - mean) ** 2. Run it on [2, 4, 6]: the broken version returns 0.0; the fixed version returns 2.666... = 8/3, matching your hand calc. One missing ** 2 is the difference between "measures spread" and "always says zero."
