Module 9

Unsupervised Learning

Learning objectives

By the end of this module you'll be able to:

  • Explain what unsupervised learning is and how it differs from the supervised learning of Modules 3–8 (there are no labels to predict).
  • Run the k-means clustering loop in your head — assign every point to its nearest centroid, then move each centroid to the average of its points — and implement it from scratch in NumPy.
  • Choose a sensible number of clusters k with the elbow method using the within-cluster sum of squares (inertia).
  • Describe anomaly detection and decide when it beats a supervised classifier.
  • Explain PCA in plain words — it finds the directions of maximum variance — and read explained_variance_ratio_ from scikit-learn.
  • Connect all of this to embeddings: learned low-dimensional representations you'll meet again in Modules 13–14.

Why should I care?

A music streaming service has 100 million listeners and no idea who they "really" are — nobody labelled them. Yet to build a useful "Discover Weekly" playlist, the company wants to group listeners who behave alike: the late-night lo-fi crowd, the gym EDM crowd, the parents-of-toddlers nursery-rhyme crowd. Nobody handed the algorithm those groups; it has to find them in the raw listening data. That's clustering. The same company also wants to flag a credit card charge that looks nothing like your normal spending — without ever having seen that exact fraud before. That's anomaly detection. And to even store each listener's taste, it compresses thousands of "did they play this song" columns down to a handful of meaningful numbers — a learned embedding. Every one of these tasks works without labels. That is unsupervised learning, and it's how you make sense of the 99% of real-world data that nobody has ever annotated.

Intuition first

Everything you've done so far has been supervised: each training row came with a correct answer — a house price (Module 3), a spam/not-spam label (Module 5). The model's job was to copy that mapping from inputs to known outputs. Unsupervised learning throws away the answer column. You hand the algorithm only the inputs and ask it to find structure on its own: which rows resemble each other, which rows are weird, and which directions in the data actually carry information.

There are three big questions in this module, and each has a classic tool:

  • "Which rows group together?" → clustering (we use k-means).
  • "Which rows are abnormal?" → anomaly detection.
  • "Can I describe each row with fewer numbers?" → dimensionality reduction (we use PCA), which leads to the idea of embeddings.

Clustering: the assign-then-update dance

Imagine a scatter of points on a table and you want to split them into, say, 3 groups. K-means does something almost embarrassingly simple, and it works. It keeps 3 "team flags" (called centroids) and repeats two steps until nothing moves:

  1. Assign. Every point joins the nearest flag's team. (Closest centroid wins.)
  2. Update. Each flag walks to the centre — the average position — of its current team.

Then you re-assign (some points are now closer to a different, moved flag), re-average, and so on. Each round, points and flags settle a little more, until a round changes nothing. That's convergence.

The party analogy. Three friends — Ana, Ben, Cleo — host a party and stand in different corners of a big room. Every guest walks to the nearest host (assign step). Now each host looks at their little crowd and shuffles to stand right in the middle of it (update step). Because a host moved, a few guests near the boundary realise another host is now closer, and they switch crowds. The hosts re-centre again. After a few rounds, everyone has settled into three stable clusters and nobody wants to move. The hosts' final positions are the centroids; the crowds are the clusters. K-means is exactly this dance, done with arithmetic instead of feet.

Each colour is a cluster; the orange star is its centroid — the average location of its members.

Run repeats until a round changes nothing (convergence). Try different k, and hit Reset / new centroids a few times to see how the starting positions can lead to better or worse clusterings.

Anomaly detection: learn "normal", flag the rest

Sometimes you don't want groups — you want the one weird row. Anomaly detection builds a picture of what "normal" looks like (most of your data) and then flags points that don't fit. The crucial reason this is often unsupervised: with fraud, machine faults, or network intrusions, you have mountains of normal examples and almost no examples of the rare bad thing — and tomorrow's fraud won't look like yesterday's. A supervised classifier (Module 5) needs many labelled examples of both classes; anomaly detection only needs to know what normal looks like, so it can catch new kinds of weirdness it has never seen.

Dimensionality reduction: fewer numbers, same story

A dataset with 50 columns lives in 50 dimensions — impossible to picture and slow to compute on. But the columns are usually redundant: height in centimetres and height in inches carry the same information twice. PCA asks: along which direction do the points spread out the most? That direction (the first principal component) captures the most "story". The next direction, at right angles, captures the most of what's left, and so on. Keep the first two or three and you can often plot 50-dimensional data on a flat page while losing very little.

Making it precise

K-means, exactly

You have \( n \) data points \( x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n \) (each \( x_i \) is a point, possibly with several coordinates). You pick \( k \), the number of clusters. K-means keeps \( k \) centroids \( \mu_1, \dots, \mu_k \) (the symbol \( \mu \), "mu", is just a name for a centroid — itself a point in the same space). It also gives every point a label \( c_i \in \{1, \dots, k\} \) saying which cluster point \( i \) belongs to.

Assign step. Each point goes to the nearest centroid:

\[ c_i = \arg\min_{j \in \{1,\dots,k\}} \; \lVert x_i - \mu_j \rVert^2 \]

In words: for point \( i \), try every centroid \( \mu_j \), measure how far the point is from it, and keep the index \( j \) of the closest one. The notation \( \lVert x_i - \mu_j \rVert^2 \) is the squared distance between the point and the centroid — subtract coordinate by coordinate, square, and add up. The \( \arg\min \) means "the argument (here, the cluster index \( j \)) that makes this smallest".

Update step. Each centroid moves to the mean of its assigned points:

\[ \mu_j = \frac{1}{|S_j|} \sum_{i \in S_j} x_i \]

where \( S_j \) is the set of points currently labelled \( j \), and \( |S_j| \) is how many points are in it. So you add up all the points in cluster \( j \) and divide by the count — the ordinary average, done separately for each coordinate.

K-means is secretly minimising one quantity, the within-cluster sum of squares, also called inertia:

\[ J = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \lVert x_i - \mu_{c_i} \rVert^2 \]

This reads: for every point, take the squared distance to its own centroid \( \mu_{c_i} \), and sum over all points. Lower \( J \) means tighter, more compact clusters. The assign step lowers \( J \) (each point picks its closest centroid); the update step lowers \( J \) too (the mean is the single point that minimises summed squared distance). Because both steps only ever decrease \( J \), the algorithm is guaranteed to stop — though possibly at a not-quite-best arrangement, which is why initialization matters.

Choosing k with the elbow

If you increase \( k \), inertia \( J \) always drops (more centroids can hug points more tightly; with \( k = n \), every point is its own centroid and \( J = 0 \)). So you can't just minimise \( J \). Instead you plot \( J \) against \( k \) and look for the elbow — the value of \( k \) after which adding more clusters barely helps. Before the elbow, each new cluster buys a big drop in \( J \); after it, you're just splitting already-tight groups.

PCA, gently

PCA finds the direction \( v \) (a unit-length arrow) along which the data, once centred, has the largest variance — variance being the average squared spread (you saw it in Module 1). Project every point onto \( v \) and you get one number per point; PCA chooses \( v \) so those numbers are as spread out as possible, because spread = information. The first principal component is that best direction; the second is the best direction perpendicular to the first; and so on. Scikit-learn reports explained_variance_ratio_: the fraction of the total variance each component captures. If the first two ratios add to 0.95, two numbers preserve 95% of the spread of the whole dataset. We won't grind through the linear algebra (eigenvectors of the covariance matrix) — the intuition "find the directions of maximum variance" is what you need, and you'll see the algebra properly if you take Module 1's resources further.

Worked example — by hand

Let's run one full k-means iteration on six points along a line (1D, so a point is just a number). The points are:

\[ 1,\quad 2,\quad 4,\quad 7,\quad 8,\quad 12 \]

We pick \( k = 2 \) and start with centroids \( \mu_1 = 2 \) and \( \mu_2 = 8 \). In 1D the distance is just the absolute difference \( |x - \mu| \).

Step 1 — Assign. For each point, compare its distance to each centroid and pick the smaller:

  • Point 1: to \( \mu_1 \) is \( |1-2| = 1 \); to \( \mu_2 \) is \( |1-8| = 7 \). Nearer 2 → cluster 1.
  • Point 2: \( |2-2| = 0 \) vs \( |2-8| = 6 \). → cluster 1.
  • Point 4: \( |4-2| = 2 \) vs \( |4-8| = 4 \). → cluster 1.
  • Point 7: \( |7-2| = 5 \) vs \( |7-8| = 1 \). → cluster 2.
  • Point 8: \( |8-2| = 6 \) vs \( |8-8| = 0 \). → cluster 2.
  • Point 12: \( |12-2| = 10 \) vs \( |12-8| = 4 \). → cluster 2.

So cluster 1 = {1, 2, 4} and cluster 2 = {7, 8, 12}.

Step 2 — Update. Move each centroid to the mean of its members:

\[ \mu_1 = \frac{1 + 2 + 4}{3} = \frac{7}{3} \approx 2.33, \qquad \mu_2 = \frac{7 + 8 + 12}{3} = \frac{27}{3} = 9 \]

The new centroids are \( \mu_1 \approx 2.33 \) and \( \mu_2 = 9 \). That's one complete iteration.

What happens next? Re-assign with the new centroids: every point's nearest centroid is unchanged (1, 2, 4 are still nearer 2.33; 7, 8, 12 still nearer 9), so the means won't move either. Nothing changes — the algorithm has converged after a single update. Let's also note the inertia at the end: \( (1-2.33)^2 + (2-2.33)^2 + (4-2.33)^2 + (7-9)^2 + (8-9)^2 + (12-9)^2 \approx 1.77 + 0.11 + 2.79 + 4 + 1 + 9 = 18.67 \). Smaller centroid moves in future rounds would only lower this further — but here we're already at the minimum for this assignment.

In code: from scratch

Here is k-means in plain NumPy: random initialization, the assign step (distances then argmin), the update step (mean of each cluster), repeated until the centroids stop moving. We test it on three clearly separated blobs.

import numpy as np def kmeans(X, k, n_iters=100, seed=0): rng = np.random.default_rng(seed) # 1) Initialize: pick k distinct data points as starting centroids. centroids = X[rng.choice(len(X), size=k, replace=False)] for _ in range(n_iters): # 2) ASSIGN: squared distance from every point to every centroid. # X[:, None, :] has shape (n, 1, d); centroids[None] has (1, k, d); # broadcasting gives an (n, k) table of distances. dists = np.linalg.norm(X[:, None, :] - centroids[None, :, :], axis=2) labels = dists.argmin(axis=1) # nearest centroid per point # 3) UPDATE: each centroid becomes the mean of its assigned points. new_centroids = np.array([ X[labels == j].mean(axis=0) if np.any(labels == j) else centroids[j] for j in range(k) ]) # 4) STOP if nothing moved (converged). if np.allclose(new_centroids, centroids): break centroids = new_centroids inertia = ((X - centroids[labels]) ** 2).sum() # within-cluster sum of squares return labels, centroids, inertia # Make three round blobs centred at (0,0), (4,5), (5,0). rng = np.random.default_rng(42) X = np.vstack([ rng.normal([0, 0], 0.6, size=(50, 2)), rng.normal([4, 5], 0.6, size=(50, 2)), rng.normal([5, 0], 0.6, size=(50, 2)), ]) labels, centroids, inertia = kmeans(X, k=3, seed=1) print("centroids:\n", np.round(centroids, 2)) print("inertia:", round(inertia, 1)) print("cluster sizes:", np.bincount(labels))

Running this prints centroids very close to the three true centres — [[-0.02 -0.04], [4.01 4.98], [4.98 -0.06]] — an inertia of about 93.0, and cluster sizes: [50 50 50]: a perfect recovery of the three groups. Try changing seed=1 to seed=0 and you'll get a worse inertia of roughly 707 with sizes like [24 26 100] — two starting centroids landed in the same blob and never recovered. That single change is the whole "initialization matters" pitfall, live.

In code: the library way

scikit-learn gives you the same algorithm — but smarter (it uses k-means++ initialization and restarts several times to dodge bad starts), tested, and fast. It also exposes inertia_ so you can run an elbow plot, and PCA for dimensionality reduction. Remember to standardize before PCA (and usually before k-means too).

import numpy as np from sklearn.cluster import KMeans from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler from sklearn.decomposition import PCA rng = np.random.default_rng(42) X = np.vstack([ rng.normal([0, 0], 0.6, size=(50, 2)), rng.normal([4, 5], 0.6, size=(50, 2)), rng.normal([5, 0], 0.6, size=(50, 2)), ]) # --- Clustering with k-means --- km = KMeans(n_clusters=3, n_init=10, random_state=0) labels = km.fit_predict(X) print("inertia_:", round(km.inertia_, 1)) # ~93.0 print("centroids:\n", np.round(km.cluster_centers_, 2)) # --- Elbow method: inertia for k = 1..6 --- for k in range(1, 7): model = KMeans(n_clusters=k, n_init=10, random_state=0).fit(X) print(f"k={k} inertia={model.inertia_:.0f}") # --- PCA (standardize first!) --- Xs = StandardScaler().fit_transform(X) pca = PCA(n_components=2).fit(Xs) print("explained_variance_ratio_:", np.round(pca.explained_variance_ratio_, 3))

The k-means part reports the same inertia_ of about 93.0 we got from scratch — reassuring. The elbow loop prints inertias of roughly 1641, 720, 93, 80, 69, 59 for k = 1 to 6: a huge drop down to k = 3 and then a near-flat tail — a textbook elbow at k = 3, exactly the number of blobs we built. The PCA part prints explained_variance_ratio_: [0.657 0.343], meaning the first principal component already captures about 66% of the spread and the two together capture 100% (this data is genuinely 2D, so nothing is lost).

Embeddings: a peek ahead

PCA hands you a few numbers per row by finding directions of maximum variance — a hand-computed low-dimensional representation. An embedding is the same idea taken further: a low-dimensional vector that a model learns so that "similar things sit close together". A word embedding might place king, queen, and monarch near each other in, say, 300 numbers; a song embedding places similar songs nearby; a face embedding places photos of the same person nearby. The streaming service from our hook stores each listener as a short embedding, then clusters those — clustering on embeddings is a standard combo. The difference from PCA is that embeddings are learned from a task (predict the next word, tell faces apart) rather than computed from variance, so they capture meaning, not just spread. You'll build real learned embeddings in Module 13 — Sequences, NLP, and the Road to Attention and see them power Module 14 — Transformers and LLMs.

Common pitfalls & misconceptions

  • "K-means works on any cluster shape." It assumes round, roughly equal-sized blobs, because it judges everything by distance to a single centre. It mangles long thin shapes, rings, or clusters of very different sizes. For those, look at DBSCAN or Gaussian mixtures.
  • Forgetting to scale. K-means uses raw distance, so a feature measured in the thousands (salary) drowns out one measured in tens (age) — the same fairness problem from Module 7. Standardize features first unless they already share units.
  • Trusting a single run. Random initialization can land two centroids in one blob and converge to a bad answer (you saw seed=0 do exactly this). Always use n_init > 1 (scikit-learn's default uses k-means++ and several restarts) and keep the lowest-inertia result.
  • Picking k by minimum inertia. Inertia always falls as k rises and hits zero when k equals the number of points — so "lowest inertia" wrongly says "use as many clusters as you have rows". Use the elbow, or a silhouette score, and your domain knowledge.
  • Treating PCA components as meaningful features. A principal component is a blend of all your original columns (e.g. "0.3 × height + 0.5 × weight − 0.2 × age"). It maximises variance, not interpretability, so don't expect "PC1 = income". It's great for visualization and speed, not for explaining to a stakeholder.
  • Running PCA on unstandardized data. PCA chases variance, so a column with large raw numbers dominates the components purely because of its units. Standardize before PCA so each feature gets a fair say.
  • Confusing clusters with truth. K-means will always return k clusters, even if the data has none. The groups are a hypothesis to investigate, not a fact.

Exercises

  1. One k-means iteration by hand. Points (1D): 0, 1, 6, 10, 11. Start with centroids \( \mu_1 = 1 \) and \( \mu_2 = 10 \). Do one assign step and one update step. What are the new centroids?
    Show solution

    Assign: point 0 → |0−1|=1 vs |0−10|=10 → cluster 1. Point 1 → 0 vs 9 → cluster 1. Point 6 → |6−1|=5 vs |6−10|=4 → cluster 2. Point 10 → cluster 2. Point 11 → cluster 2. So cluster 1 = {0, 1}, cluster 2 = {6, 10, 11}. Update: \( \mu_1 = (0+1)/2 = 0.5 \); \( \mu_2 = (6+10+11)/3 = 27/3 = 9 \). New centroids: 0.5 and 9.

  2. Why does increasing k never increase inertia? And why does that make "choose k to minimise inertia" a bad rule?
    Show solution

    With more centroids, every point can be at least as close to its nearest centroid as before (an extra centroid can only help or be ignored), so the within-cluster sum of squared distances — inertia — can only stay the same or drop. In the extreme, with k equal to the number of points, each point is its own centroid and inertia is exactly 0. Because inertia is minimised by making k as large as possible, "minimise inertia" always says "use one cluster per point", which is useless. That's why we look for the elbow: the k after which extra clusters stop buying meaningful reductions.

  3. You cluster customers using two raw features: annual spend (range 500–50,000) and number of visits (range 1–40). Without scaling, which feature dominates the clustering, and what should you do?
    Show solution

    K-means measures distance, and spend ranges over tens of thousands while visits range over tens — so a difference of "1000 in spend" swamps a difference of "10 in visits". Distance, and therefore the clusters, are driven almost entirely by spend; visits barely matter. Fix it by standardizing both features (subtract the mean, divide by the standard deviation, as in Module 7) so each contributes on a comparable scale before running k-means.

  4. For a fraud-detection problem you have 2,000,000 normal transactions and only 30 known fraud cases, and fraudsters constantly invent new tricks. Argue whether a supervised classifier or anomaly detection is the better starting point.
    Show solution

    Anomaly detection is the stronger starting point. A supervised classifier needs many examples of both classes; with only 30 fraud cases it has almost nothing to learn the "fraud" pattern from, and it would tend to predict "normal" for everything to score high accuracy (the imbalance trap from Module 6). It would also be blind to genuinely new fraud styles, since it only learns the 30 it saw. Anomaly detection instead models the abundant "normal" behaviour and flags anything that deviates — so it can catch novel fraud it has never seen. Once you accumulate enough confirmed fraud labels, a supervised model (or a hybrid) becomes worth adding.

  5. PCA on a standardized 10-feature dataset gives explained_variance_ratio_ = [0.52, 0.23, 0.11, 0.06, 0.04, 0.02, 0.01, 0.005, 0.003, 0.002]. How many components keep at least 90% of the variance, and what does that tell you about the data?
    Show solution

    Add the ratios until you cross 0.90: 0.52, then 0.75 (two), then 0.86 (three), then 0.92 (four). So 4 components keep about 92% of the variance — just under with 3 (86%), comfortably over with 4. This tells you the 10 original features are highly redundant: four learned directions carry almost all the spread, so you can compress to 4 numbers per row (great for plotting in pairs, speeding up later models, or de-noising) while losing under 10% of the information.
