Module 9

Unsupervised Learning

01 · No labels, no teacher

Here is a pile of dots. Nobody told you what they are.

Every model you've built so far had a teacher. Module 3 had house prices. Module 5 had spam/not-spam. Each training row came with the right answer stapled to it, and the model just learned to copy the mapping. That's supervised learning.

Now I'm taking the answer column away. I hand you 150 points scattered on a table — no colors, no labels, no key — and I ask: are there groups in here? How many? Where? You have no teacher. Just the raw positions.

This is the situation for 99% of the data anyone actually has. Nobody annotated it. So before we name a single algorithm, sit with the real question: can a machine find structure with nobody telling it the answer? Look at a scatter of dots and decide, in your head, how many groups you see and roughly where their centers are. Commit to a number. Then we'll see if a machine agrees.

You have unlabeled customer data and want to "find the natural groups." A teammate says "let's just train a classifier." What's wrong with that?

A classifier is supervised — it learns a mapping from inputs to known answers. No answers, no classifier. When the answer column is missing, you need a method that invents the structure itself. That's unsupervised learning, and clustering is its flagship move.

Three questions show up over and over when you have unlabeled data, and each has a classic tool. Keep these straight — they're the map for the whole module:

which rows group together? → clustering (we use k-means).   which rows are abnormal? → anomaly detection.   can I describe each row with fewer numbers? → dimensionality reduction (we use PCA) → embeddings.

02 · The trick is dumber than you think

K-means is two steps, on repeat, forever.

Here's the whole algorithm before any math. Drop k flags on the table at random — call them centroids. Then loop two steps until nothing moves:

1. Assign. Every point joins the nearest flag's team. Closest centroid wins.

2. Update. Each flag walks to the center — the average position — of its current team.

That's it. After the flags move, some points near a boundary suddenly have a different nearest flag, so they switch teams. Re-average. Re-assign. Each round everything settles a bit more, until a round changes nothing. That last part has a name: convergence.

The party version, because it makes the loop physical: three friends — Ana, Ben, Cleo — stand in random corners of a room. Every guest walks to the nearest host (assign). Each host then shuffles to the middle of their little crowd (update). A host moving means a few guests near the edge realize someone else is now closer, so they switch crowds. Hosts re-center. After a few rounds nobody wants to move. Final host positions are the centroids; the crowds are the clusters. K-means is this dance done with arithmetic instead of feet.

A centroid currently sits to the LEFT of its team's true middle. You run one update step. Where does it move?

Update sets each centroid to the mean of its members — full stop. If it's sitting left of the crowd, the mean is to its right, so it hops right. It doesn't take a cautious half-step; it teleports straight to the average. (No learning rate here — unlike Module 4's gradient descent, k-means moves all the way each time.) You're about to watch this exact move in the lab.

03 · The lab

Run the dance yourself. Then break it on purpose.

Now do it, don't watch it. Step runs one phase at a time — first points recolor to their nearest centroid (✕), then each centroid glides to the mean of its members. Run loops until a round changes nothing. Reset / new centroids reseeds the starting flags. Don't just poke at it — finish the three missions. Each one teaches a different way k-means can surprise you.

LAB K-means, with missions

Predict each outcome before you click. The gap between your guess and what happens is the part that sticks.

  • Count the iterations to convergence Set the right k, then hit Step (assign → update) over and over until a round changes nothing. How many full rounds did it take? On clean blobs it's usually fewer than you'd guess — often under 10.
  • Set k WRONG and watch it shred a real cluster Pick a k bigger than the true number of groups. Run to convergence. Notice how k-means happily slices one genuine blob in half — it will always return exactly k clusters, real or not.
  • Reseed until you get a BAD answer Keep the right k, but hit Reset / new centroids and run, several times. Eventually two flags start inside the same blob and converge to a lopsided, wrong split. That's initialization sensitivity, live.
What you should have seen

Mission 1: convergence is fast and obvious — the centroids stop twitching and the colors freeze. Mission 2: too-big k carves a clean blob into pieces because the algorithm has no concept of "this group is already done"; it just minimizes distance with the budget of centroids you gave it. Mission 3: most reseeds find the good answer, but some don't — the same algorithm on the same data lands in a worse arrangement purely because of where the flags started. That's not a bug. It's why real k-means restarts several times and keeps the best run.

04 · Earn the notation

You already invented it. Here's the math.

You have \( n \) data points \( x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n \) (each \( x_i \) is a point, maybe with several coordinates). You pick \( k \), the number of clusters. K-means keeps \( k \) centroids \( \mu_1, \dots, \mu_k \) (the symbol \( \mu \), "mu", is just a name for a centroid — a point in the same space). Every data point gets a label \( c_i \) saying which cluster it's in. Your two-step dance becomes two formulas.

\[ c_i = \arg\min_{j \in \{1,\dots,k\}} \; \lVert x_i - \mu_j \rVert^2 \]

assign step. For point \( i \), try every centroid \( \mu_j \), measure how far the point is, keep the index \( j \) of the closest. ‖x − μ‖² is the squared distance: subtract coordinate by coordinate, square, add up. arg min means "the argument \( j \) that makes this smallest."

\[ \mu_j = \frac{1}{|S_j|} \sum_{i \in S_j} x_i \]

update step. Each centroid becomes the mean of its members. S_j is the set of points currently labeled \( j \). |S_j| is how many there are. Add up the points in cluster \( j \), divide by the count — the ordinary average, per coordinate.

And here's the quantity k-means is secretly minimizing the whole time — the within-cluster sum of squares, also called inertia:

\[ J = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \lVert x_i - \mu_{c_i} \rVert^2 \]

inertia J. For every point, take the squared distance to its own centroid \( \mu_{c_i} \), and sum over all points. Lower \( J \) means tighter, more compact clusters.

Here's the elegant bit: the assign step lowers \( J \) (each point picks its closest centroid), and the update step lowers \( J \) too (the mean is the single point that minimizes summed squared distance to a group). Both steps only ever decrease \( J \), so the algorithm is guaranteed to stop — though possibly at a not-quite-best arrangement. That's exactly the bad answer you reseeded into in Mission 3.

05 · Do it by hand

One full iteration, just you and arithmetic.

Six points on a line (1D, so each point is just a number): 1, 2, 4, 7, 8, 12. We pick \( k = 2 \) and start with centroids \( \mu_1 = 2 \) and \( \mu_2 = 8 \). In 1D the distance is just \( |x - \mu| \). Before you read the worked solution, do the assign step yourself — for each point, which centroid is closer?

After assigning all six points to the nearer of \( \mu_1 = 2 \) and \( \mu_2 = 8 \), which split do you get?

Point 4 is distance 2 from \( \mu_1=2 \) but distance 4 from \( \mu_2=8 \) → cluster 1. Point 7 is distance 5 from \( \mu_1 \) but only 1 from \( \mu_2 \) → cluster 2. So {1, 2, 4} and {7, 8, 12}.

BY HAND the full iteration

Step 1 — Assign. For each point, compare distance to each centroid and pick the smaller:

  • Point 1: \( |1-2| = 1 \) vs \( |1-8| = 7 \). Nearer 2 → cluster 1.
  • Point 2: \( |2-2| = 0 \) vs \( |2-8| = 6 \) → cluster 1.
  • Point 4: \( |4-2| = 2 \) vs \( |4-8| = 4 \) → cluster 1.
  • Point 7: \( |7-2| = 5 \) vs \( |7-8| = 1 \) → cluster 2.
  • Point 8: \( |8-2| = 6 \) vs \( |8-8| = 0 \) → cluster 2.
  • Point 12: \( |12-2| = 10 \) vs \( |12-8| = 4 \) → cluster 2.

So cluster 1 = {1, 2, 4} and cluster 2 = {7, 8, 12}.

Step 2 — Update. Move each centroid to the mean of its members:

\[ \mu_1 = \frac{1 + 2 + 4}{3} = \frac{7}{3} \approx 2.33, \qquad \mu_2 = \frac{7 + 8 + 12}{3} = \frac{27}{3} = 9 \]

New centroids: \( \mu_1 \approx 2.33 \) and \( \mu_2 = 9 \). That's one complete iteration.

What happens on round two? Re-assign with the new centroids: 1, 2, 4 are still nearest 2.33; 7, 8, 12 are still nearest 9. No point switches, so the means don't move. Converged after a single update. The final inertia: \( (1-2.33)^2 + (2-2.33)^2 + (4-2.33)^2 + (7-9)^2 + (8-9)^2 + (12-9)^2 \approx 1.78 + 0.11 + 2.78 + 4 + 1 + 9 = 18.67 \).

06 · Find the bug

This from-scratch k-means looks right. One line is wrong.

Below is k-means in plain NumPy. It runs without errors — but the clusters come out nonsensical and the reported inertia is garbage. The update logic is correct, the stop logic is correct. Read it, find the broken line before you open the spoiler.

import numpy as np def kmeans(X, k, n_iters=100, seed=0): rng = np.random.default_rng(seed) # 1) Initialize: pick k distinct data points as starting centroids. centroids = X[rng.choice(len(X), size=k, replace=False)] for _ in range(n_iters): # 2) ASSIGN: distance from every point to every centroid. dists = np.linalg.norm(X[:, None, :] - centroids[None, :, :], axis=2) labels = dists.argmax(axis=1) # <-- something is wrong on this line # 3) UPDATE: each centroid becomes the mean of its assigned points. new_centroids = np.array([ X[labels == j].mean(axis=0) if np.any(labels == j) else centroids[j] for j in range(k) ]) # 4) STOP if nothing moved (converged). if np.allclose(new_centroids, centroids): break centroids = new_centroids inertia = ((X - centroids[labels]) ** 2).sum() # within-cluster sum of squares return labels, centroids, inertia
Show the bug & why

labels = dists.argmax(axis=1) assigns every point to its farthest centroid. The assign step is supposed to pick the nearest one, so it must be argmin, not argmax:

labels = dists.argmin(axis=1)

One letter. With argmax, points flee to the opposite side of the room every round, the centroids chase phantom crowds, and inertia (which still measures distance to your own centroid) stays huge. This is the unsupervised cousin of Module 4's flipped sign — a tiny direction error that quietly breaks the whole loop while the code runs perfectly happily.

Here's the corrected version, with the broadcasting trick spelled out. Predict the output first: three blobs centered at (0,0), (4,5), (5,0) — what should the recovered centroids and inertia look like?

import numpy as np def kmeans(X, k, n_iters=100, seed=0): rng = np.random.default_rng(seed) # 1) Initialize: pick k distinct data points as starting centroids. centroids = X[rng.choice(len(X), size=k, replace=False)] for _ in range(n_iters): # 2) ASSIGN: squared distance from every point to every centroid. # X[:, None, :] has shape (n, 1, d); centroids[None] has (1, k, d); # broadcasting gives an (n, k) table of distances. dists = np.linalg.norm(X[:, None, :] - centroids[None, :, :], axis=2) labels = dists.argmin(axis=1) # nearest centroid per point # 3) UPDATE: each centroid becomes the mean of its assigned points. new_centroids = np.array([ X[labels == j].mean(axis=0) if np.any(labels == j) else centroids[j] for j in range(k) ]) # 4) STOP if nothing moved (converged). if np.allclose(new_centroids, centroids): break centroids = new_centroids inertia = ((X - centroids[labels]) ** 2).sum() # within-cluster sum of squares return labels, centroids, inertia # Make three round blobs centred at (0,0), (4,5), (5,0). rng = np.random.default_rng(42) X = np.vstack([ rng.normal([0, 0], 0.6, size=(50, 2)), rng.normal([4, 5], 0.6, size=(50, 2)), rng.normal([5, 0], 0.6, size=(50, 2)), ]) labels, centroids, inertia = kmeans(X, k=3, seed=1) print("centroids:\n", np.round(centroids, 2)) print("inertia:", round(inertia, 1)) print("cluster sizes:", np.bincount(labels))

This prints centroids hugging the three true centers — [[-0.02 -0.04], [4.01 4.98], [4.98 -0.06]] — an inertia of about 93.0, and cluster sizes: [50 50 50]: a perfect recovery. Now change seed=1 to seed=0 and you get a worse inertia of roughly 707 with sizes like [24 26 100] — two starting centroids landed in the same blob and never escaped. That one-character change is the "initialization matters" pitfall from Mission 3, live in code.

07 · The library way + choosing k

scikit-learn does the same thing, but smarter.

scikit-learn runs the same algorithm — but with k-means++ initialization and several restarts to dodge bad starts (so you stop losing to the seed lottery), tested and fast. It exposes inertia_ so you can build an elbow plot, and ships PCA for dimensionality reduction. Standardize before PCA, and usually before k-means too. Predict the elbow output before you read on: the data is genuinely 3 blobs — at which k should inertia stop dropping sharply?

import numpy as np from sklearn.cluster import KMeans from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler from sklearn.decomposition import PCA rng = np.random.default_rng(42) X = np.vstack([ rng.normal([0, 0], 0.6, size=(50, 2)), rng.normal([4, 5], 0.6, size=(50, 2)), rng.normal([5, 0], 0.6, size=(50, 2)), ]) # --- Clustering with k-means --- km = KMeans(n_clusters=3, n_init=10, random_state=0) labels = km.fit_predict(X) print("inertia_:", round(km.inertia_, 1)) # ~93.0 print("centroids:\n", np.round(km.cluster_centers_, 2)) # --- Elbow method: inertia for k = 1..6 --- for k in range(1, 7): model = KMeans(n_clusters=k, n_init=10, random_state=0).fit(X) print(f"k={k} inertia={model.inertia_:.0f}") # --- PCA (standardize first!) --- Xs = StandardScaler().fit_transform(X) pca = PCA(n_components=2).fit(Xs) print("explained_variance_ratio_:", np.round(pca.explained_variance_ratio_, 3))

The k-means part reports the same inertia_ of about 93.0 we got from scratch — reassuring. The elbow loop prints inertias of roughly 1641, 720, 93, 80, 69, 59 for k = 1 to 6: a huge drop down to k = 3, then a near-flat tail. That sudden bend is the elbow — the textbook signal that k = 3, exactly the number of blobs we built. The PCA part prints explained_variance_ratio_: [0.657 0.343]: the first principal component captures about 66% of the spread, and the two together capture 100% (this data is genuinely 2D, so nothing is lost).

why the elbow, not the minimum? Inertia always falls as k rises — with k equal to the number of points, every point is its own centroid and \( J = 0 \). So "pick the k with lowest inertia" is a trap that screams "use one cluster per row." Plot \( J \) vs \( k \) and find the bend: before it, each new cluster buys a big drop; after it, you're just splitting already-tight groups for nothing.

08 · The other two questions

Anomalies and PCA, in plain words.

Anomaly detection — learn "normal," flag the rest. Sometimes you don't want groups, you want the one weird row. The method: build a picture of what "normal" looks like from your mountain of ordinary data, then flag whatever doesn't fit. Why unsupervised? With fraud, machine faults, or network intrusions you have tons of normal examples and almost no examples of the rare bad thing — and tomorrow's fraud won't look like yesterday's. A supervised classifier (Module 5) needs many labeled examples of both classes. Anomaly detection only needs to know what normal looks like, so it can catch new kinds of weird it has never seen.

PCA — fewer numbers, same story. A dataset with 50 columns lives in 50 dimensions: impossible to picture, slow to compute on, and usually redundant (height in cm and height in inches carry the same information twice). PCA asks: along which direction do the points spread out the most? That direction — the first principal component — captures the most "story." Project every point onto it and you get one number per point, chosen so those numbers are as spread out as possible, because spread = information. The second component is the best direction perpendicular to the first, and so on. Keep the first two or three and you can plot 50-dimensional data on a flat page while losing very little.

scikit-learn reports explained_variance_ratio_: the fraction of total variance each component captures. If the first two ratios add to 0.95, two numbers preserve 95% of the spread of the whole dataset. The intuition — "find the directions of maximum variance" — is all you need; the underlying machinery (eigenvectors of the covariance matrix) is a Module 1 deep-dive.

You run PCA on raw data where one column is salary (range 0–200,000) and another is age (range 0–100), without standardizing. What dominates the first principal component?

PCA chases variance, and variance is measured in the raw units. A column ranging over 200,000 swamps one ranging over 100 for no real reason — it's an artifact of scale, not meaning. Standardize first (subtract the mean, divide by the standard deviation, as in Module 7) so each feature gets a fair say. Same scaling trap bites k-means too, since it judges everything by distance.

09 · Where this goes next

From PCA to embeddings — the bridge to the rest of the course.

PCA hands you a few numbers per row by finding directions of maximum variance — a computed low-dimensional representation. An embedding is the same idea taken further: a low-dimensional vector that a model learns so that "similar things sit close together." A word embedding might place king, queen, and monarch near each other in, say, 300 numbers; a song embedding places similar songs nearby; a face embedding places photos of the same person nearby. A streaming service stores each listener as a short embedding, then clusters those — clustering on embeddings is a standard combo. The difference from PCA: embeddings are learned from a task (predict the next word, tell faces apart), so they capture meaning, not just spread.

Lock this in. The "similar things sit close together in a low-dimensional space" idea you just met in PCA is the seed of embeddings, which you'll build for real in Module 13 (Sequences, NLP, and the Road to Attention) and see power Module 14 (Transformers and LLMs). And the k-means trap "feature scale distorts distance" is the exact fairness issue from Module 7 — standardize, every time.
10 · The traps that actually bite

Read these once now; you'll hit every one.

None of these are exotic. They are the mistakes everyone makes on their first real clustering job.

  • "K-means works on any cluster shape." It assumes round, roughly equal-sized blobs, because it judges everything by distance to a single center. It mangles long thin shapes, rings, and clusters of very different sizes. For those, reach for DBSCAN or Gaussian mixtures.
  • Forgetting to scale. Raw distance means a feature in the thousands (salary) drowns one in the tens (age). Standardize first unless features already share units.
  • Trusting a single run. Random init can land two centroids in one blob (you saw seed=0 do exactly this — inertia 707 vs 93). Always use n_init > 1 and keep the lowest-inertia result.
  • Picking k by minimum inertia. Inertia hits zero when k equals the number of points, so "lowest inertia" says "one cluster per row." Use the elbow, a silhouette score, and domain knowledge.
  • Treating PCA components as features. A component is a blend of all your columns (e.g. "0.3 × height + 0.5 × weight − 0.2 × age"). It maximizes variance, not interpretability — don't expect "PC1 = income."
  • Confusing clusters with truth. K-means always returns k clusters, even on data with none. The groups are a hypothesis to investigate, not a fact.
11 · Cold recall — no scrolling back

Pull it out of your own head.

Re-reading feels like learning but mostly isn't. Retrieving is. Answer each one in your head first, then flip. Can't? That's your signal to scroll up — but try first.

What are the two repeating steps of k-means, and which quantity does each lower?
tap to flip
Assign each point to its nearest centroid, then update each centroid to the mean of its members. Both steps only ever lower inertia (within-cluster sum of squares), so it always converges.
Why can't you choose k by picking the lowest inertia?
tap to flip
Inertia always falls as k rises and hits 0 when k = number of points. "Lowest inertia" means "one cluster per row." Use the elbow instead — the k after which extra clusters barely help.
Same data, same k, two different answers. What's the cause and the fix?
tap to flip
Initialization sensitivity. Where the centroids start changes where they converge. Fix: k-means++ init plus several restarts (n_init > 1), keep the lowest-inertia run.
One sentence: what does PCA do, and why standardize first?
tap to flip
PCA finds the directions of maximum variance to describe each row with fewer numbers. Standardize first so a column doesn't dominate just because its raw units are bigger.
When does anomaly detection beat a supervised classifier?
tap to flip
When you have lots of "normal" data, very few labeled bad examples, and the bad thing keeps changing (fraud, faults, intrusions). Model normal, flag deviations — catches novel weirdness a classifier never saw.

Stretch exercises

  1. One k-means iteration by hand. Points (1D): 0, 1, 6, 10, 11. Start with centroids \( \mu_1 = 1 \) and \( \mu_2 = 10 \). Do one assign step and one update step. What are the new centroids?
    Show solution

    Assign: 0 → |0−1|=1 vs |0−10|=10 → cluster 1. 1 → 0 vs 9 → cluster 1. 6 → |6−1|=5 vs |6−10|=4 → cluster 2. 10 → cluster 2. 11 → cluster 2. So cluster 1 = {0, 1}, cluster 2 = {6, 10, 11}. Update: \( \mu_1 = (0+1)/2 = 0.5 \); \( \mu_2 = (6+10+11)/3 = 27/3 = 9 \). New centroids: 0.5 and 9.

  2. You cluster customers using two raw features: annual spend (range 500–50,000) and number of visits (range 1–40). Without scaling, which feature dominates, and what do you do?
    Show solution

    Spend ranges over tens of thousands while visits range over tens, so a difference of "1000 in spend" swamps "10 in visits." Distance — and therefore the clusters — are driven almost entirely by spend; visits barely matter. Fix it by standardizing both features (subtract the mean, divide by the standard deviation) so each contributes on a comparable scale before running k-means.

  3. For fraud detection you have 2,000,000 normal transactions and only 30 known fraud cases, and fraudsters keep inventing new tricks. Supervised classifier or anomaly detection?
    Show solution

    Anomaly detection is the stronger start. A classifier needs many examples of both classes; with 30 fraud cases it has almost nothing to learn "fraud" from, and it would predict "normal" for everything to score high accuracy (the imbalance trap from Module 6). It's also blind to genuinely new fraud styles. Anomaly detection models the abundant normal behavior and flags deviations, so it can catch novel fraud it has never seen. Once you accumulate enough confirmed fraud labels, add a supervised model (or a hybrid).

  4. PCA on a standardized 10-feature dataset gives explained_variance_ratio_ = [0.52, 0.23, 0.11, 0.06, 0.04, 0.02, 0.01, 0.005, 0.003, 0.002]. How many components keep at least 90% of the variance, and what does that tell you?
    Show solution

    Add until you cross 0.90: 0.52, then 0.75 (two), then 0.86 (three), then 0.92 (four). So 4 components keep about 92% — just under with 3 (86%), comfortably over with 4. The 10 original features are highly redundant: four learned directions carry almost all the spread, so you can compress to 4 numbers per row (great for plotting in pairs, speeding up later models, or de-noising) while losing under 10% of the information.
