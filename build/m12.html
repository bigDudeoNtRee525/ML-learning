Module 12

Convolutional Neural Networks for Vision

01 · do the math that breaks the obvious idea

One dense layer. Count the weights. Then panic.

You already know how to build a neural network (Module 10) and train it (Module 11). So here's the obvious move: take a photo, flatten it into a list of pixels, feed it to a dense layer. Done. Computer vision solved.

Before you scroll, do the arithmetic yourself. A normal colour photo is 224 pixels wide, 224 tall, 3 colour channels (red, green, blue). Multiply: that's the length of the flattened input. Now imagine the first dense layer has just 1,000 neurons, each connected to every input pixel. How many weights is that one layer?

Write down your guess. Thousands? Millions? Get a number in your head before you tap.

A 224×224×3 image into a 1,000-neuron dense first layer. How many weights in that single layer?

224 × 224 × 3 = 150,528 input numbers per image. Each of the 1,000 neurons connects to all of them: 150,528 × 1,000 ≈ 150 million weights. One layer. A real net has dozens. That is too much to store comfortably and way too much to learn from a normal dataset without overfitting catastrophically (remember overfitting from Module 6). Dense layers on raw images are a dead end.

And the parameter explosion isn't even the worst part.

02 · the second, sneakier problem

Flattening murders the picture.

To feed an image into a dense layer you have to flatten it into one long line of numbers. The instant you do that, the network forgets that pixel #225 was sitting directly below pixel #1. Neighbouring pixels — the ones that together make edges, corners, textures — get scattered into unrelated slots.

Worse: a dense net has to learn a separate detector for a cat's ear in the top-left, another for the top-right, another for the middle. Move the cat two pixels and it's a brand-new pattern as far as the network is concerned. Nothing is shared. You're paying 150 million weights and still re-learning the same thing in every location.

So what's the fix? Here's the whole idea, and you should sit with it for a second before the formula shows up.

The rubber stamp. Picture a giant photo and a tiny transparent stamp with one little pattern printed on it — say, a short vertical edge. Press the stamp onto the top-left of the photo and score how well the photo underneath matches the stamp. Slide it one step right, score again. Keep going across and down until you've stamped everywhere, writing each score into a grid. That grid is a map of where this pattern appears. The stamp is the filter (or kernel); it's maybe 3×3 = 9 numbers, reused everywhere. The grid of scores is a feature map. One small reusable detector, slid across the whole image — that single idea is the entire heart of a convolution.

This fixes both problems at once. The stamp is tiny, so few weights. And because you slide the same stamp everywhere, the pattern is found no matter where it sits — learn it once, apply it everywhere. (Fancy name for that: translation equivariance. Move the cat, its activations move with it.) Now go watch it actually happen.

Show what you should have seen

Vertical edge: the map is near-zero (mid-gray) across flat regions and lights up in the column straddling the dark→bright boundary. The detector found exactly one vertical edge and ignored everything flat. That's a feature map doing its job.

Identity: the feature map is just a 7×7 crop of the original image — it copies the centre pixel of every window and throws the neighbours away. A kernel can literally do nothing. Good baseline for your intuition.

Box blur: the sharp edge spreads into a soft gradient. Averaging smears neighbouring pixels together, so high-frequency detail (sharp edges) gets washed out. Edges = the thing detectors want; blur = the thing that destroys them.

04 · predict the shape before you trust the formula

How big is the output?

You just watched a 3×3 filter sweep a 9×9 image and fill a 7×7 map. Why 7? Don't take the widget's word for it — reason it out. The filter's top-left corner can sit at column 0, 1, 2, … but once its right edge would hang off the image, you have to stop. Count the valid starting positions in one row.

A 3×3 filter does a valid (no padding) convolution, stride 1, over a 9×9 image. What's the output side length?

A 3×3 window's top-left can start at columns 0 through 6 — that's 7 positions (at column 7 the window's right edge would hang off the 9-wide image). Same for rows. So the output is 7×7. The general rule: \( 9 - 3 + 1 = 7 \). "Valid" convolution always shrinks by \( F - 1 \) per dimension. Now let's earn that formula in full.

05 · now the notation, because you've earned it

The math you already invented.

Call the input grid \( X \) (pixel brightnesses) and the filter \( K \) (the small grid of numbers — these are the weights the network learns). Lay the filter on a patch of \( X \), multiply each filter number by the input number underneath, and add all the products into one number. That number is one cell of the output \( Y \). Move the filter, repeat.

\[ Y_{i,j} \;=\; \sum_{a=0}^{F-1}\;\sum_{b=0}^{F-1}\; X_{\,i+a,\;j+b}\;\cdot\;K_{a,b} \]

Y the output feature map. X the input image. K the filter/kernel. F the filter side length (e.g. 3 for a 3×3). The two sums just walk over every cell of the filter; \( (i,j) \) is where you placed its top-left corner.

Read it out loud: "line the filter up at position \( (i,j) \), multiply each overlapping pair, sum them into one output number." That's it. (Pedant's note: this is technically cross-correlation; real textbook convolution flips the filter first. Deep-learning libraries skip the flip and call it convolution anyway. Since the filter is learned, the flip makes no practical difference — don't lose sleep over it.)

Real networks bolt on three more knobs. You met two of them already in the lab as intuition; here are the names:

\[ O \;=\; \left\lfloor \frac{W - F + 2P}{S} \right\rfloor + 1 \]

O output side length. W input side length. F filter size. P padding (rings of zeros added to each side). S stride (how many pixels you slide each step). \( \lfloor \cdot \rfloor \) means round down.

  • Stride \( S \) — pixels you jump per step. \( S=1 \): slide one pixel, big overlapping output. \( S=2 \): jump two, roughly halving the output.
  • Padding \( P \) — rings of zeros around the border. \( P=0 \) is valid convolution (output shrinks every layer). Padding chosen so output equals input is same convolution. The \( 2P \) in the formula is there because you pad both sides.
  • Pooling — a separate shrinking step. Max pooling with a 2×2 window keeps only the largest value in each 2×2 block, halving width and height. Smaller, faster, and a bit robust to tiny shifts.

Sanity-check the formula against the lab: \( W=9, F=3, P=0, S=1 \) gives \( O = \lfloor(9-3+0)/1\rfloor + 1 = 6 + 1 = 7 \). The widget was right. Memorize this formula — getting a shape wrong is the #1 beginner bug, and you'll use it constantly.

Two more quick checks. A 28×28 image, 3×3 filter, \( P=0, S=1 \): \( O = \lfloor(28-3+0)/1\rfloor + 1 = 26 \), so 26×26. Add \( P=1 \): \( O = \lfloor(28-3+2)/1\rfloor + 1 = 28 \) — same size as the input, which is exactly why \( P=1 \) with a 3×3 filter is called "same" padding.

Channels. A colour image is 3 stacked grids (R, G, B). A filter then has depth 3 too — its own 3×3 for each input channel — and it sums across all channels into one output map. A conv layer holds many such filters (say 32), making 32 output maps stacked into a new 32-channel "image" for the next layer. So depth grows as spatial size shrinks: an image travels from 3×224×224 toward something like 512×7×7 before a final dense layer decides.

PyTorch stores image batches in NCHW order: shape (N, C, H, W) = (batch size, channels, height, width). Feeding (H, W) or NHWC is the second most common beginner bug. Tape NCHW to your monitor.

06 · do it by hand

Convolve a 3×3 with a 2×2. No computer.

Get a pen. We'll convolve a 3×3 input with a 2×2 filter, valid padding, stride 1. First, the shape: \( O = \lfloor(3-2+0)/1\rfloor + 1 = 2 \), so a 2×2 output — four windows.

\[ X = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \qquad K = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \]

This filter computes "(top-left) minus (bottom-right)" — a crude diagonal-difference detector. Before you read on, compute Window 1 yourself (rows 0–1, cols 0–1). Then check.

Window 1 — top-left, \( \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \): \( 1{\cdot}1 + 2{\cdot}0 + 0{\cdot}0 + 1{\cdot}(-1) = 1 + 0 + 0 - 1 = \mathbf{0} \).

Window 2 — top-right, \( \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \): \( 2{\cdot}1 + 3{\cdot}0 + 1{\cdot}0 + 2{\cdot}(-1) = 2 - 2 = \mathbf{0} \).

Window 3 — bottom-left, \( \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 3 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \): \( 0{\cdot}1 + 1{\cdot}0 + 3{\cdot}0 + 1{\cdot}(-1) = -1 = \mathbf{-1} \).

Window 4 — bottom-right, \( \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \): \( 1{\cdot}1 + 2{\cdot}0 + 1{\cdot}0 + 0{\cdot}(-1) = 1 = \mathbf{1} \).

Assemble in grid order:

\[ Y = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ -1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \]

9 input numbers and 4 filter numbers produced a 2×2 summary — and the same four weights were reused in all four windows. That reuse is exactly what saves you from the 150-million-weight explosion.

07 · predict the output, then run it

From scratch in NumPy.

Here's the by-hand procedure as code: slide the kernel, compute each windowed sum. Before you run it: the first print reproduces the worked example above. What 2×2 grid should it print? (You computed it 30 seconds ago.) Predict, then read.

import numpy as np def conv2d(X, K, stride=1, padding=0): """Valid/padded 2D cross-correlation of a single-channel image X with kernel K.""" if padding > 0: X = np.pad(X, padding, mode="constant", constant_values=0) Hin, Win = X.shape F = K.shape[0] # assume square filter F x F Hout = (Hin - F) // stride + 1 # the output-size formula Wout = (Win - F) // stride + 1 Y = np.zeros((Hout, Wout)) for i in range(Hout): for j in range(Wout): r, c = i * stride, j * stride window = X[r:r+F, c:c+F] # the patch under the filter Y[i, j] = np.sum(window * K) # multiply overlapping pairs, sum them return Y # Reproduce the worked example X = np.array([[1, 2, 3], [0, 1, 2], [3, 1, 0]]) K = np.array([[1, 0], [0, -1]]) print("Worked example output:\n", conv2d(X, K)) # A vertical-edge detector on a tiny image: dark on the left, bright on the right img = np.array([[0, 0, 9, 9], [0, 0, 9, 9], [0, 0, 9, 9], [0, 0, 9, 9]], dtype=float) vedge = np.array([[ 1, -1], [ 1, -1]]) print("Vertical-edge feature map:\n", conv2d(img, vedge))

First print: [[0, 0], [-1, 1]] — exactly your hand calculation. The second filter [[1,-1],[1,-1]] fires only where brightness changes left-to-right. The image is flat 0 on the left, flat 9 on the right, one vertical boundary in the middle — so the feature map is near zero everywhere except the column straddling that boundary, where it reads about −18. A clear "vertical edge here" signal, from just four weights. (Same behaviour you saw in the lab, now in numbers.)

08 · find the bug

This shape math is wrong. Where?

A beginner wrote this SmallCNN for MNIST (28×28 grayscale, 10 classes). It defines fine but crashes at runtime with a shape-mismatch error the moment the first batch hits fc1. Trace the spatial size through both conv+pool stages using the output-size formula, and find the wrong number before you open the answer.

import torch import torch.nn as nn import torch.nn.functional as F class SmallCNN(nn.Module): def __init__(self): super().__init__() self.conv1 = nn.Conv2d(1, 32, kernel_size=3, padding=1) # 28x28 stays 28x28 self.conv2 = nn.Conv2d(32, 64, kernel_size=3, padding=1) # stays same size self.pool = nn.MaxPool2d(2) # halves H and W self.fc1 = nn.Linear(64 * 14 * 14, 128) # <-- something is wrong on this line self.fc2 = nn.Linear(128, 10) def forward(self, x): # x: (N,1,28,28) x = self.pool(F.relu(self.conv1(x))) # -> (N,32,14,14) x = self.pool(F.relu(self.conv2(x))) # -> (N,64,7,7) x = torch.flatten(x, 1) x = F.relu(self.fc1(x)) return self.fc2(x)
Show the bug & the fix

The padding-1, 3×3 convs keep the spatial size (28→28, then 14→14 — that's why "same" padding exists). But there are two poolings: 28 → 14 → 7. So the tensor entering flatten is (N, 64, 7, 7), and flatten gives 64·7·7 = 3,136 features — not 64·14·14 = 12,544. The author counted only one pooling. Fix:

self.fc1 = nn.Linear(64 * 7 * 7, 128)

This is the #1 beginner bug in the flesh: a shape miscount. The forward comments even spell out (N,64,7,7) — when in doubt, print(x.shape) before every layer and let the formula, not vibes, tell you the number.

09 · the library way, for real

A working CNN that reads handwriting.

Here's the corrected, complete CNN. Many filters, ReLU nonlinearities (Module 10), pooling, and gradient descent (Module 4) to learn the filter numbers instead of hand-picking them. Watch the shapes flow in the comments — that's the whole skill.

import torch import torch.nn as nn import torch.nn.functional as F from torch.utils.data import DataLoader from torchvision import datasets, transforms # 1) Data. ToTensor() puts pixels in [0,1] and shapes them as (C,H,W)=(1,28,28). # Normalize subtracts the dataset mean and divides by its std (see Module 7). tfm = transforms.Compose([ transforms.ToTensor(), transforms.Normalize((0.1307,), (0.3081,)), # MNIST mean/std ]) train_ds = datasets.MNIST(root="data", train=True, download=True, transform=tfm) test_ds = datasets.MNIST(root="data", train=False, download=True, transform=tfm) train_dl = DataLoader(train_ds, batch_size=64, shuffle=True) test_dl = DataLoader(test_ds, batch_size=1000) # 2) A small CNN. Track shapes with the output-size formula in comments. class SmallCNN(nn.Module): def __init__(self): super().__init__() self.conv1 = nn.Conv2d(1, 32, kernel_size=3, padding=1) # 1->32 ch, 28x28 stays 28x28 self.conv2 = nn.Conv2d(32, 64, kernel_size=3, padding=1) # 32->64 ch, 14x14 stays 14x14 self.pool = nn.MaxPool2d(2) # halves H and W self.fc1 = nn.Linear(64 * 7 * 7, 128) # flatten -> 128 self.fc2 = nn.Linear(128, 10) # 128 -> 10 classes def forward(self, x): # x: (N,1,28,28) x = self.pool(F.relu(self.conv1(x))) # -> (N,32,14,14) x = self.pool(F.relu(self.conv2(x))) # -> (N,64,7,7) x = torch.flatten(x, 1) # -> (N, 64*7*7) = (N, 3136) x = F.relu(self.fc1(x)) # -> (N,128) return self.fc2(x) # -> (N,10) raw scores (logits) device = "cuda" if torch.cuda.is_available() else "cpu" model = SmallCNN().to(device) opt = torch.optim.Adam(model.parameters(), lr=1e-3) loss_fn = nn.CrossEntropyLoss() # combines softmax + log-loss (Module 5) # 3) Train for one epoch (one pass over the data) to keep it quick. model.train() for images, labels in train_dl: images, labels = images.to(device), labels.to(device) opt.zero_grad() logits = model(images) loss = loss_fn(logits, labels) loss.backward() # backprop computes gradients (Module 11) opt.step() # gradient-descent update # 4) Evaluate accuracy on the held-out test set. model.eval() correct = total = 0 with torch.no_grad(): for images, labels in test_dl: images, labels = images.to(device), labels.to(device) pred = model(images).argmax(dim=1) correct += (pred == labels).sum().item() total += labels.size(0) print(f"Test accuracy: {correct/total:.4f}")

After a single epoch this typically hits about 98% test accuracy — out of 10,000 digits it misses only a couple hundred. A few more epochs and it passes 99%. Notice the flow: 1 channel becomes 32 then 64 (more detectors deeper in) while spatial size shrinks 28 → 14 → 7 via the two poolings. Flatten turns the final 64×7×7 = 3,136 numbers into a vector two dense layers map to 10 scores. The convolutions did the seeing; the dense layers just decide.

10 · steal a giant's brain

Transfer learning: ~1,000 trainable params, 90%+ accuracy.

MNIST is easy — 60,000 training images. But say you have 200 photos of two flower species. Train a big CNN from scratch on 200 images and it overfits instantly (Module 6). The fix is transfer learning: grab a network already trained on millions of images, so its early filters already detect edges, textures, and shapes useful for any vision task. Freeze it, replace just the final classification layer with one sized for your classes, train only that.

Before you run it: the last line prints how many parameters are trainable. ResNet18 has ~11 million total. After we freeze everything and bolt on a 512→2 layer, how many are trainable? Guess the order of magnitude.

import torch import torch.nn as nn from torchvision import models # Load resnet18 with weights pretrained on ImageNet (1000 classes, millions of images). weights = models.ResNet18_Weights.DEFAULT net = models.resnet18(weights=weights) # Freeze every existing parameter so training won't disturb the learned filters. for p in net.parameters(): p.requires_grad = False # Replace the final fully-connected layer (1000 outputs) with our own (2 classes). num_features = net.fc.in_features # resnet18 -> 512 net.fc = nn.Linear(num_features, 2) # new layer: its params DO require grad # Only the new layer's parameters are trainable, so the optimizer sees just those. opt = torch.optim.Adam(net.fc.parameters(), lr=1e-3) loss_fn = nn.CrossEntropyLoss() # IMPORTANT: preprocess YOUR images exactly the way the pretrained net expects. preprocess = weights.transforms() # resize to 224, center-crop, normalize with ImageNet stats # Then a normal training loop over your small dataset, e.g.: # for images, labels in your_loader: # opt.zero_grad() # loss = loss_fn(net(images), labels) # loss.backward(); opt.step() print("Trainable params:", sum(p.numel() for p in net.parameters() if p.requires_grad))

It prints about 1,026 (512 weights + 1 bias per output, times 2 outputs) instead of 11 million — yet on a small flower or pet dataset this routinely hits 90%+ in minutes. The pretrained filters supply visual "common sense"; you only teach the net how to name your classes. The one non-negotiable: preprocess your images with the same resize and normalization the network was trained with (here weights.transforms() does it). Feed it raw, differently-scaled pixels and accuracy collapses.

Why the residual trick exists (you'll meet it again). When researchers stacked very deep nets (50, 100+ layers), accuracy got mysteriously worse — the gradient signal degraded over so many layers (vanishing gradients, Module 11). ResNet's fix is the residual connection: instead of a block computing a brand-new output \( H(x) \), it computes only a correction \( F(x) \) and adds the input back, outputting \( F(x)+x \). If the best move is to do nothing, the block learns \( F(x)=0 \) and passes \( x \) through untouched. And that "+x" gives gradients a clean shortcut straight back, so even 100+ layers train. This one trick is everywhere today — including inside the Transformers of Module 14.

Lineage worth knowing: LeNet-5 (1998) read cheque digits — basically our SmallCNN. AlexNet (2012) was a deeper LeNet on GPUs that crushed ImageNet and started the deep-learning era (popularized ReLU + dropout). ResNet (2015) added the residual connection and unlocked very deep nets.
