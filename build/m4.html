Module 4

Gradient Descent: How Models Learn

In Module 3 we said "find the \( w \) and \( b \) that make the cost small" — and then quietly skipped the only interesting part: how. This is the how. It's one line of math, run a few billion times, and it trains everything from a straight line to ChatGPT. You're not going to read about it. You're going to be it, break it, debug it, and get quizzed until it's boring.

01 · the problem, before the lesson

You're a robot, lost in fog

You wake up somewhere on a hillside. The fog is so thick you can't see your own hands, let alone the valley below. You have to get to the lowest point. You have exactly one sense: you can feel the slope of the ground under your feet — which way it tilts, and how steeply.

That's the whole game. No map. No view of the bottom. Just the tilt beneath you, here, now. What's your move? Don't scroll — actually decide your strategy in one sentence before you read on.

Got it? Here's the thing: you almost certainly just invented gradient descent. The instinct is "feel which way the ground falls, step that way, repeat." That instinct, written down carefully, trains every model in this course. The rest of this module is making that instinct precise enough to give to a computer — and finding the two stupid-simple ways it blows up.

One trap, flagged early. If you only ever walk downhill, you can get stuck in a shallow dip that isn't the deepest valley — a local minimum. For the bowl-shaped costs in this module there's only one valley, so it never bites. It comes back to haunt us in Module 10 (neural nets), where the landscape is full of dips.

02 · lock in a guess

Predict before you peek

You figured out the rule: step in the direction the ground goes down — opposite the slope. Steeper ground, bigger step you can afford. Flat ground, you're basically there. Fine. Now commit to this before we go further, because the answer is the single most common way training explodes.

You make each step proportional to the slope (steep = big step). On a very steep wall, you get greedy and crank that step size way up. What happens?

A big step on a steep slope flings you past the bottom and up the opposite wall — where it's also steep, so the next big step flings you back, even further. The size of the step has a name: the learning rate. Getting it wrong is most beginners' first real bug, and you'll watch it happen live in the lab two beats from now.

Opposite problem. You're terrified of overshooting, so you make every step tiny — like 0.001 of the slope. Direction is always correct. What's the catch?

Tiny steps are correct and useless. Move 0.006 toward a target three units away and do the math on how long that takes. The learning rate has a Goldilocks zone: too big diverges, too small crawls, and the sweet spot is narrower than beginners expect. Finding it is a real engineering decision, not a footnote.

03 · now (and only now) the math

You already invented it — here's the notation

The "height of the hill" is the cost \( J(w) \): one number that says how wrong the model is, as a function of a knob \( w \) we can turn. The "slope you feel" is the derivative \( \frac{dJ}{dw} \): how fast the cost changes when you nudge \( w \). Read that slope as the answer to one question:

"If I nudge \( w \) up a hair, does the cost go up or down, and how fast?" Positive slope → cost rises as \( w \) grows → you should decrease \( w \). Negative slope → you should increase \( w \). Near zero → flat ground, probably the bottom.

Your whole foggy-robot strategy is exactly one line:

\[ w \;\leftarrow\; w \;-\; \alpha\,\frac{dJ}{dw} \]

w the knob you're tuning. dJ/dw the slope — which way is uphill and how steep. α (alpha) the learning rate, your step size, a small positive number you choose. − the magic minus sign: subtracting the slope walks you downhill.

Read it out loud: "new knob = old knob, minus a little bit of the slope." That little bit is \( \alpha \). The minus sign is the entire trick — if the slope is positive (cost climbing as \( w \) grows) you subtract a positive number and \( w \) shrinks; if the slope is negative you subtract a negative and \( w \) grows. Either way, you move toward lower cost. \( \alpha \) is a hyperparameter: a dial you set by hand, not something the algorithm learns.

The whole of training — linear regression, logistic regression, the network behind ChatGPT — is this line, run millions of times. That is not a metaphor. It is literally this line.

When there are two knobs (\( w \) and \( b \)), apply the same rule to each, using each one's own slope (now called a partial derivative, \( \frac{\partial J}{\partial w} \) — "partial" just means "slope in one knob's direction, holding the other fixed"):

\[ w \leftarrow w - \alpha\,\frac{\partial J}{\partial w}, \qquad b \leftarrow b - \alpha\,\frac{\partial J}{\partial b} \]

Compute both slopes first from the current \( (w,b) \), then update both. Never update \( w \) and recompute \( b \)'s slope from the changed \( w \) — they take one synchronized step together. The full list of partials is called the gradient, written \( \nabla J \). It points the fastest way uphill; we go the opposite way. That's the entire algorithm.

04 · be the algorithm (by hand)

Run it in your own head first

Forget data for a second. Take the cleanest possible hill: \( J(w) = (w-3)^2 \). It's a bowl with its lowest point obviously at \( w = 3 \) (cost 0 there). Pretend you don't know that and let gradient descent find it. The slope is \( \frac{dJ}{dw} = 2(w-3) \) (chain rule: power comes down, times the derivative of the inside, which is 1). Start at \( w = 0 \), learning rate \( \alpha = 0.1 \), so the rule is \( w \leftarrow w - 0.1\cdot 2(w-3) \).

Do Step 1 yourself before you open the answer. At \( w = 0 \): what's the slope, and where does the step land you?

Check Step 1

Slope \( = 2(0-3) = -6 \). Update: \( w = 0 - 0.1\times(-6) = 0 + 0.6 = \mathbf{0.6} \). The slope was negative (cost falls as \( w \) rises), so you moved right, toward 3. Correct direction.

Now the rest of the march — verify each line:

  • Step 1. \( w=0 \): slope \( 2(0-3)=-6 \). \( w = 0 - 0.1(-6) = \mathbf{0.6} \).
  • Step 2. \( w=0.6 \): slope \( 2(0.6-3)=-4.8 \). \( w = 0.6 - 0.1(-4.8) = \mathbf{1.08} \).
  • Step 3. \( w=1.08 \): slope \( 2(1.08-3)=-3.84 \). \( w = 1.08 + 0.384 = \mathbf{1.464} \).
  • Step 4. \( w=1.464 \): slope \( 2(1.464-3)=-3.072 \). \( w = 1.464 + 0.3072 = \mathbf{1.7712} \).

The values march \( 0 \to 0.6 \to 1.08 \to 1.464 \to 1.7712 \to \dots \), each step smaller than the last — because the slope keeps shrinking as you near the bottom. Gradient descent is self-braking: steep ground = big steps, flat ground = tiny steps, so it glides into the minimum instead of blowing past it (as long as \( \alpha \) isn't too big — hold that thought).

There's a tidy pattern hiding here. Each step, the distance to the target multiplies by \( (1 - 2\alpha) = 0.8 \). Check it: distance starts at 3; after step 1 it's \( 3 - 0.6 = 2.4 = 0.8\times 3 \); after step 2 it's \( 3 - 1.08 = 1.92 = 0.8\times 2.4 \). So \( w \) never quite reaches 3 — it gets exponentially close. That's why we run "enough" iterations instead of expecting a perfect landing. Remember this multiplier; it's about to tell us exactly when things explode.

05 · the lab — three missions

Automate it, then break it on purpose

Now the computer applies \( w \leftarrow w - \alpha\cdot 2(w-3) \) for you, one step per click (or hit Run). This is the same bowl you just walked by hand. Your job isn't to "play with it" — it's to complete three missions. Drag the learning-rate slider and watch the slope line flatten as the ball nears the bottom.

LAB gradient descent, with stakes

Start at \( w = 0 \). The ball sits at the current \( w \); each Step does \( w \leftarrow w - \alpha\cdot 2(w-3) \). Watch the cost readout — is it shrinking or growing?

  • Reach the bottom in 8 steps or fewer Get the cost under 0.05 (w ≈ 3). Small α crawls — go bigger, but not too big. Try around α = 0.3.
  • Make it diverge on purpose Crank α until the cost shoots UP instead of down. This is the overshoot you predicted in Beat 02.
  • Find the edge of stability Get it to bounce side to side without diverging — a steady oscillation that never settles and never explodes. It lives right around α = 1.0.

When you nailed Mission 3 you found something deep: there's a razor-sharp threshold where the same algorithm flips from "converges" to "explodes." That threshold is what makes choosing \( \alpha \) a real decision.

Before you compute it: with the distance-multiplier rule \( (1 - 2\alpha) \), what's the exact \( \alpha \) where the bowl tips from converging to diverging?

The multiplier is \( (1-2\alpha) \). Convergence needs its magnitude below 1. At \( \alpha = 1.0 \) it's exactly \( -1 \): from \( w=0 \) you bounce \( 0 \to 6 \to 0 \to 6 \to\dots \), mirror-imaged across 3, never closer, never farther. Above 1.0 the magnitude exceeds 1 and the distance grows every step — divergence. That's the edge you felt in Mission 3.

06 · fix the broken loop

This code looks right. It isn't.

A beginner wrote this to descend our bowl. It runs without errors — and the cost grows every single step instead of shrinking. Read it. Find the bug before you open the answer. There is exactly one wrong character.

def train(w=0.0, lr=0.1, steps=20): for i in range(steps): grad = 2 * (w - 3) # slope of J(w) = (w-3)**2 w = w + lr * grad # take a step # <-- something is wrong on this line print(i, round(w, 3), round((w - 3) ** 2, 3)) return w
Show the bug & why

w = w + lr * grad walks uphill. The gradient points toward higher cost, so to go down you must subtract it: w = w - lr * grad. That one flipped sign is the difference between a model that learns and a model that screams off to infinity. Note this is a different failure from the lab: there, divergence came from too-big a step in the right direction; here, every step is the right size but the wrong way. Two ways to break the same one-line rule.

07 · from one knob to real data

The same line, now with x's and y's

The bowl was a warm-up. Real linear regression has two knobs and actual data. The prediction for example \( i \) is \( \hat{y}_i = w x_i + b \), and with \( n \) examples the mean squared error cost is:

\[ J(w,b) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(\hat{y}_i - y_i\right)^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(w x_i + b - y_i\right)^2 \]

x_i the input for example \( i \). ŷ_i the model's guess. y_i the truth. ŷ_i − y_i the residual (how far off you are). Squaring makes every error positive and punishes big misses harder. Σ "add over all n examples."

Differentiate that (chain rule; the factor of 2 falls out of the square) and you get the two slopes you'll actually code:

\[ \frac{\partial J}{\partial w} = \frac{2}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(\hat{y}_i - y_i\right)x_i, \qquad \frac{\partial J}{\partial b} = \frac{2}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(\hat{y}_i - y_i\right) \]

Read them in words. The \( b \)-slope is twice the average residual: predicting too low everywhere → residuals negative → slope negative → update raises \( b \). The \( w \)-slope is the average residual weighted by \( x_i \): an example with a big \( x \) pulls harder, because changing \( w \) moves its prediction more.

Now do one real step by hand. Two points: \( (x_1,y_1)=(1,2) \) and \( (x_2,y_2)=(2,3) \). Start at \( w=0, b=0 \), \( \alpha=0.1 \), \( n=2 \). Try to get all the way to the updated \( w \) and \( b \) before opening the answer.

Check your one full GD step

Predict. \( \hat{y}_1 = 0\cdot 1 + 0 = 0 \), \( \hat{y}_2 = 0\cdot 2 + 0 = 0 \).

Residuals \( (\hat{y}_i - y_i) \): \( 0-2 = -2 \) and \( 0-3 = -3 \). Both negative — predicting too low, as expected from an all-zero model.

Slope for w: \( \frac{2}{2}\big[(-2)(1) + (-3)(2)\big] = 1\times(-2-6) = -8 \).

Slope for b: \( \frac{2}{2}\big[(-2)+(-3)\big] = 1\times(-5) = -5 \).

Update both at once. \( w = 0 - 0.1\times(-8) = \mathbf{0.8} \); \( b = 0 - 0.1\times(-5) = \mathbf{0.5} \).

Did cost actually drop? Before: \( J = \frac{1}{2}\big[(-2)^2+(-3)^2\big] = \frac{1}{2}(4+9) = 6.5 \). After: \( \hat{y}_1 = 0.8(1)+0.5 = 1.3 \), \( \hat{y}_2 = 0.8(2)+0.5 = 2.1 \); residuals \( (-0.7,-0.9) \); \( J = \frac{1}{2}(0.49+0.81) = 0.65 \). Cost fell from 6.5 to 0.65 in a single step. That's gradient descent earning its keep.

08 · how much data per step?

Batch, mini-batch, stochastic

Every gradient formula above sums over all \( n \) examples. With a few hundred rows, fine. With millions — or the trillions of tokens an LLM trains on — computing the full sum before every step is absurdly slow. The three flavours differ in exactly one thing: how much data you look at before taking each step.

  • Batch GD. Use all examples per step. Gradient is exact, path is smooth and stable, each step is expensive. This is what we'll code from scratch — it's the clearest.
  • Stochastic GD (SGD). Use one random example per step. Dirt cheap, so you take many steps fast, and the noise can even jiggle you out of shallow dips. But each gradient is a rough estimate, so the path zig-zags.
  • Mini-batch GD. Use a small group — typically 32 to 256 examples — per step. The sweet spot everyone actually uses: far cheaper than batch, far less noisy than pure SGD, and it maps beautifully onto GPUs. One full pass over all the mini-batches is an epoch.

Mental model: batch GD takes a few confident strides; SGD takes many tipsy little steps; mini-batch takes brisk, mostly-sober steps. All three head downhill — they just trade accuracy-per-step against steps-per-second. You'll lean on mini-batch SGD constantly once we hit neural nets in Module 10 and Module 11.

09 · from scratch, then the library

Predict the output, then run it

Here's the whole loop in NumPy — the exact MSE gradients you just derived, fitting a line to data that secretly follows \( y \approx 2x + 1 \) plus noise. Before you run it, predict: after 1000 epochs, will \( w \) land closer to 2 or to 0? Will the cost keep dropping forever, or hit a floor?

import numpy as np # --- make some data that roughly follows y = 2x + 1 --- rng = np.random.default_rng(0) # fixed seed -> reproducible x = np.linspace(0, 5, 50) # 50 inputs from 0 to 5 y = 2.0 * x + 1.0 + rng.normal(0, 0.5, size=x.shape) # true line + noise n = len(x) # --- start the knobs at zero --- w, b = 0.0, 0.0 alpha = 0.01 # learning rate epochs = 1000 # how many full passes (batch GD) def cost(w, b): yhat = w * x + b return np.mean((yhat - y) ** 2) # mean squared error for epoch in range(epochs): yhat = w * x + b # predictions for ALL points error = yhat - y # residuals (y-hat minus y) # exact MSE gradients: dw = (2 / n) * np.sum(error * x) # dJ/dw db = (2 / n) * np.sum(error) # dJ/db # one synchronized step downhill: w = w - alpha * dw b = b - alpha * db if epoch % 200 == 0: print(f"epoch {epoch:4d} cost {cost(w, b):.4f} w {w:.3f} b {b:.3f}") print(f"\nlearned: w = {w:.3f}, b = {b:.3f} (true was w=2, b=1)")

Now run it. The cost drops every line and the parameters climb toward the truth. Roughly:

epoch 0 cost 31.1602 w 0.395 b 0.121 epoch 200 cost 0.1789 w 2.130 b 0.729 epoch 400 cost 0.1784 w 2.121 b 0.757 epoch 600 cost 0.1784 w 2.118 b 0.768 epoch 800 cost 0.1783 w 2.117 b 0.772 learned: w = 2.116, b = 0.773 (true was w=2, b=1)

Read the story in those numbers. The cost crashes from over 30 down to about 0.18 and then stops. That floor isn't failure — it's the leftover noise we sprinkled in (you can't fit randomness). With noise standard deviation 0.5 you'd expect a floor near \( 0.5^2 = 0.25 \); we land a touch below it (about 0.18) because the fitted line soaks up a little of the random wiggle in these particular 50 points. Learned \( w \approx 2.12 \) sits right next to the true 2; \( b \approx 0.77 \) lands near the true 1, missing slightly because noise in a small sample tugs the best-fit line off the underlying truth. Two things you'd flag as broken: a cost that rises (α too high) or one still falling fast at the last epoch (run more epochs, or raise α). A different seed gives slightly different numbers — expected and fine.

Once you've felt the loop, scikit-learn does it for you. SGDRegressor is linear regression trained by (mini-)stochastic gradient descent — the library version of everything above. It forces one real-world habit on you: scale your features first (a skinny, lopsided cost bowl makes GD zig-zag painfully). We chain a scaler and the regressor in a Pipeline. Predict the printed prediction at x = 2.5 before running.

import numpy as np from sklearn.linear_model import SGDRegressor from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler from sklearn.pipeline import make_pipeline rng = np.random.default_rng(0) x = np.linspace(0, 5, 50) y = 2.0 * x + 1.0 + rng.normal(0, 0.5, size=x.shape) X = x.reshape(-1, 1) # sklearn wants a 2D array of features model = make_pipeline( StandardScaler(), # center & scale x -> faster, stabler GD SGDRegressor(loss="squared_error", # plain MSE (the cost we used) learning_rate="constant", eta0=0.05, # eta0 is alpha max_iter=1000, random_state=0) ) model.fit(X, y) pred = model.predict([[2.5]]) # predict y when x = 2.5 (truth ~ 6.0) print(f"prediction at x=2.5: {pred[0]:.3f}") print(f"R^2 on training data: {model.score(X, y):.3f}")

It prints a prediction near \( 6.0 \) for \( x = 2.5 \) (since \( 2\times 2.5 + 1 = 6 \)) and an \( R^2 \) close to \( 1.0 \) — the line explains almost all the variation in \( y \). Note eta0 is scikit-learn's name for \( \alpha \). For a closed-form, no-learning-rate fit you'd reach for LinearRegression (Module 3); SGDRegressor is the one that scales to huge data and shares its DNA with neural-network training.

10 · the traps that actually get people

Where beginners lose hours

  • "Bigger learning rate = faster learning." Up to a point — then it flips. Too big and the cost grows every step until it overflows to nan. If cost ever rises, first fix is shrink \( \alpha \) by 10x.
  • Forgetting to scale features. One feature ranges 0–1, another 0–100000 → the cost bowl becomes a long skinny ravine, and GD zig-zags across the narrow direction forever. Standardize (subtract mean, divide by std). Deep dive in Module 7.
  • Updating one parameter with the other's already-updated value. Compute all gradients from the current parameters first, then apply every update. Interleaving breaks the math.
  • Thinking GD always finds the best possible answer. For convex (bowl-shaped) costs like linear and logistic regression, it does. For neural nets the landscape has many valleys; GD finds a good minimum, not provably the global one. Good-enough is what we ship.
  • Confusing learning rate with epochs. \( \alpha \) sets how far each step goes; epochs set how many. A too-small \( \alpha \) can be partly rescued by more epochs; a too-big \( \alpha \) cannot be fixed by any number of epochs — it diverges.
  • Dropping the factor of 2 in the gradient. Using \( \frac{1}{n} \) instead of \( \frac{2}{n} \) nothing breaks catastrophically — it's just like halving your learning rate. But know which convention you're on so \( \alpha \) means what you think.
