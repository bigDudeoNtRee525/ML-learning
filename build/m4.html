Module 4

Gradient Descent: How Models Learn

Learning objectives

By the end of this module you'll be able to:

  • Picture a model's cost as a landscape and explain why "learning" means walking downhill on it.
  • Say in plain words what a gradient is, and why we step in the negative gradient direction.
  • Apply the update rule \( w := w - \alpha \frac{\partial J}{\partial w} \) by hand, and explain what each piece means.
  • Choose a sensible learning rate \( \alpha \) and predict what happens when it is too big or too small.
  • Distinguish batch, mini-batch, and stochastic gradient descent, and say when you'd use each.
  • Write a correct gradient-descent loop from scratch in NumPy that fits a line, and run the same fit with scikit-learn.

Why should I care?

In Module 3 — Linear Regression you met a model \( \hat{y} = wx + b \) and a cost (mean squared error) that scores how wrong it is. But we cheated: we said "find the \( w \) and \( b \) that make the cost small" without saying how. Gradient descent is the how. It is the single algorithm that trains almost everything you'll ever build — linear regression, logistic regression (Module 5), and every neural network and large language model in Modules 10–14. When ChatGPT was trained, the same idea you'll code in 15 lines today was run billions of times over trillions of words. Learn it once, deeply, and you understand the engine under the entire field. Concretely: imagine you run a coffee cart and want to predict daily sales from the temperature. You have a hunch sales rise with heat, but by how much? Gradient descent is the procedure that nudges your guess for "extra cups per degree" up and down until it best matches what actually happened.

Intuition first

A model has knobs (we call them parameters: the weight \( w \) and the bias \( b \)). For every setting of the knobs there is a single number that says "how badly are we doing?" — the cost. If you imagine the knobs as a position and the cost as height, you get a landscape: hills where the model is bad, valleys where it is good. Training is just finding the bottom of a valley.

The foggy mountain. You're standing somewhere on a hillside in thick fog. You can't see the bottom of the valley, but you can feel the slope under your feet. A perfectly reasonable plan: feel which way is steepest downhill, take a small step that way, and repeat. Step by step you descend, even though you never saw the whole mountain. Gradient descent is exactly this — the "slope under your feet" is the gradient, and your step size is the learning rate. The catch (which we'll return to): if you only ever walk downhill, you might settle in a small dip that isn't the deepest valley. That's the local minimum problem.

A second picture that beginners find sticky: a ball rolling in a bowl. Drop a marble into a smooth bowl and it rolls toward the lowest point, slowing as it nears the bottom. For the simple cost shapes in this module the landscape really is a bowl (a single lowest point), so the ball-in-a-bowl image is not just cute — it's accurate.

Each green dot is one update. The steeper the bowl, the bigger the step; near the bottom the slope flattens and steps shrink, so we glide gently into the minimum.

Notice something the picture already tells us: where the bowl is steep, the slope is large, so we move a lot; where it flattens out near the bottom, the slope is near zero, so we barely move. Gradient descent is self-braking. That's why it doesn't fly past the minimum (as long as the steps aren't too big — more on that under learning rate).

Play with it: press Step to take one update on the bowl J(w) = (w − 3)², or Run to auto-descend. Drag the learning-rate slider: around 0.1–0.3 the ball glides to w = 3, a tiny α crawls, and α ≥ 1.0 makes it overshoot and diverge. Watch the red slope line (the gradient) flatten as it nears the bottom.

Making it precise

Let's name things. We want to minimize a cost function \( J \) — a number measuring total error — by choosing a parameter \( w \) (a single adjustable number, the "knob"). The key quantity is the derivative of the cost with respect to the knob, written \( \frac{\partial J}{\partial w} \) and read "the partial derivative of \( J \) with respect to \( w \)." In plain words it answers one question:

"If I nudge \( w \) up by a tiny amount, does the cost go up or down, and how fast?" A positive value means "increasing \( w \) increases the cost" (so we should decrease \( w \)); a negative value means "increasing \( w \) decreases the cost" (so we should increase \( w \)); a value near zero means "we're on flat ground — possibly at the bottom." The word partial just signals that when there are several knobs (like \( w \) and \( b \)), we measure the slope in one knob's direction at a time, holding the others fixed.

The collection of all the partial derivatives, one per parameter, is the gradient, written \( \nabla J \) (the symbol is "nabla"). The gradient is a list of slopes that, taken together, points in the direction of steepest ascent — the fastest way uphill. We want to go down, so we step in the opposite direction: the negative gradient. That single fact is the whole algorithm.

Here is the update rule. Read \( := \) as "becomes" (assignment, like in code):

\[ w := w - \alpha \, \frac{\partial J}{\partial w} \]

In words: "the new value of \( w \) is the old value, minus the learning rate times the slope at the current point." Each symbol:

  • \( w \) — the parameter we're tuning (a number).
  • \( \alpha \) — the learning rate (read "alpha"), a small positive number you choose, like \( 0.1 \) or \( 0.01 \). It controls how big a step we take. It is a hyperparameter: a setting you pick, not something the algorithm learns.
  • \( \frac{\partial J}{\partial w} \) — the slope of the cost at the current \( w \): how cost changes when \( w \) changes.
  • The minus sign — this is what makes us go downhill. If the slope is positive (cost rising as \( w \) grows), we subtract a positive number and \( w \) shrinks; if the slope is negative, subtracting a negative grows \( w \). Either way we move toward lower cost.

When there are two knobs, we just apply the same rule to each, using each one's own partial derivative:

\[ w := w - \alpha \, \frac{\partial J}{\partial w}, \qquad b := b - \alpha \, \frac{\partial J}{\partial b} \]

We compute both slopes first from the current \( (w, b) \), then update both — never update \( w \) and then recompute \( b \)'s slope from the changed \( w \). They move together, one synchronized step.

The exact gradients for linear regression (MSE)

For linear regression the prediction is \( \hat{y}_i = w x_i + b \) for example \( i \), where \( x_i \) is the input and \( \hat{y}_i \) (read "y-hat") is the model's guess. With \( n \) examples and true labels \( y_i \), the mean squared error cost is:

\[ J(w, b) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left( \hat{y}_i - y_i \right)^2 = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left( w x_i + b - y_i \right)^2 \]

where \( \sum_{i=1}^{n} \) means "add up over all \( n \) examples," and \( (\hat{y}_i - y_i) \) is the residual (how far off prediction \( i \) is). Squaring makes every error positive and punishes big misses more. The slopes work out (using the chain rule from Module 1 — Just-Enough Math Intuition; the factor of 2 falls out of differentiating the square) to:

\[ \frac{\partial J}{\partial w} = \frac{2}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left( \hat{y}_i - y_i \right) x_i, \qquad \frac{\partial J}{\partial b} = \frac{2}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left( \hat{y}_i - y_i \right) \]

You don't need to derive these by hand right now — but read them in words, because the words are intuitive. The slope for the bias \( b \) is just (twice) the average residual: if we're predicting too low on average, the residuals are negative, the slope is negative, and the update raises \( b \). The slope for \( w \) is (twice) the average residual weighted by the input \( x_i \): an example with a large \( x \) pulls harder on the slope, because changing \( w \) affects its prediction more. Keep these two formulas nearby — you'll code them in a minute.

Worked example — by hand

Part A: a one-knob bowl. Forget data for a moment and just minimize the clean cost \( J(w) = (w - 3)^2 \). This is a bowl whose lowest point is obviously at \( w = 3 \) (where the cost is 0). Let's pretend we don't know that and let gradient descent find it. The derivative of \( (w-3)^2 \) is \( \frac{dJ}{dw} = 2(w - 3) \) (chain rule: bring the power down, multiply by the inside's derivative, which is 1). We start at \( w = 0 \) with learning rate \( \alpha = 0.1 \), and apply \( w := w - 0.1 \cdot 2(w-3) \).

  • Step 1. At \( w = 0 \): slope \( = 2(0 - 3) = -6 \). Update: \( w = 0 - 0.1 \times (-6) = 0 + 0.6 = \mathbf{0.6} \). The slope was negative (cost falls as \( w \) rises), so we moved right, toward 3. Good.
  • Step 2. At \( w = 0.6 \): slope \( = 2(0.6 - 3) = -4.8 \). Update: \( w = 0.6 - 0.1 \times (-4.8) = 0.6 + 0.48 = \mathbf{1.08} \).
  • Step 3. At \( w = 1.08 \): slope \( = 2(1.08 - 3) = -3.84 \). Update: \( w = 1.08 + 0.384 = \mathbf{1.464} \).
  • Step 4. At \( w = 1.464 \): slope \( = 2(1.464 - 3) = -3.072 \). Update: \( w = 1.464 + 0.3072 = \mathbf{1.7712} \).

The values march \( 0 \to 0.6 \to 1.08 \to 1.464 \to 1.7712 \to \dots \), each step smaller than the last (because the slope keeps shrinking as we near the bottom), homing in on \( w = 3 \). In fact there's a tidy pattern here: each step the distance to the target multiplies by \( (1 - 2\alpha) = 0.8 \). Check: distance starts at 3; after step 1 it's \( 3 - 0.6 = 2.4 = 0.8 \times 3 \); after step 2 it's \( 3 - 1.08 = 1.92 = 0.8 \times 2.4 \). So \( w \) never quite reaches 3, but gets exponentially close — that's why we run "enough" iterations rather than expecting a perfect landing.

Part B: one real GD step on tiny data. Now a real (if minuscule) linear-regression fit. Two points: \( (x_1, y_1) = (1, 2) \) and \( (x_2, y_2) = (2, 3) \). Start at \( w = 0 \), \( b = 0 \), learning rate \( \alpha = 0.1 \), with \( n = 2 \).

  1. Predict. \( \hat{y}_1 = 0 \cdot 1 + 0 = 0 \), \( \hat{y}_2 = 0 \cdot 2 + 0 = 0 \).
  2. Residuals \( (\hat{y}_i - y_i) \): \( 0 - 2 = -2 \) and \( 0 - 3 = -3 \). Both negative — we're predicting too low everywhere, as expected from an all-zero model.
  3. Slope for \( w \): \( \frac{\partial J}{\partial w} = \frac{2}{2}\big[(-2)(1) + (-3)(2)\big] = 1 \times (-2 - 6) = -8 \).
  4. Slope for \( b \): \( \frac{\partial J}{\partial b} = \frac{2}{2}\big[(-2) + (-3)\big] = 1 \times (-5) = -5 \).
  5. Update both at once. \( w = 0 - 0.1 \times (-8) = \mathbf{0.8} \); \( b = 0 - 0.1 \times (-5) = \mathbf{0.5} \).

Did cost actually drop? Before: residuals \( (-2, -3) \), so \( J = \frac{1}{2}\big[(-2)^2 + (-3)^2\big] = \frac{1}{2}(4 + 9) = 6.5 \). After: new predictions \( \hat{y}_1 = 0.8(1) + 0.5 = 1.3 \), \( \hat{y}_2 = 0.8(2) + 0.5 = 2.1 \); residuals \( (1.3 - 2,\; 2.1 - 3) = (-0.7, -0.9) \); \( J = \frac{1}{2}\big[(-0.7)^2 + (-0.9)^2\big] = \frac{1}{2}(0.49 + 0.81) = 0.65 \). The cost fell from \( 6.5 \) to \( 0.65 \) in a single step. That is gradient descent earning its keep.

The learning rate: the one dial you must respect

The learning rate \( \alpha \) decides how far you step. It is the most common thing beginners get wrong, so let's make it concrete using the same bowl \( J(w) = (w-3)^2 \) starting at \( w = 0 \).

  • Just right (\( \alpha = 0.1 \)). As we saw: \( 0 \to 0.6 \to 1.08 \to 1.464 \to \dots \to 3 \). Smooth, steady descent.
  • Too small (\( \alpha = 0.001 \)). Step 1: \( w = 0 - 0.001 \times (-6) = 0.006 \). After step 1 you've moved \( 0.006 \) toward a target \( 3 \) units away. It will get there — but it might take thousands of iterations. Correct direction, painfully slow. You waste compute.
  • Too big (\( \alpha = 1.1 \)). Step 1: slope \( = -6 \), \( w = 0 - 1.1 \times (-6) = 6.6 \). You overshot the minimum (3) and landed further away on the other side (distance 3.6, was 3). Step 2: slope \( = 2(6.6 - 3) = 7.2 \), \( w = 6.6 - 1.1 \times 7.2 = 6.6 - 7.92 = -1.32 \). Now you've overshot back past 3 to the other side again, even further out. The values bounce \( 0 \to 6.6 \to -1.32 \to \dots \) with the distance growing every step. This is divergence — the cost explodes to infinity. Recall the rule from Part A: the distance to the target multiplies by \( (1 - 2\alpha) \) each step. With \( \alpha = 1.1 \) that factor is \( 1 - 2.2 = -1.2 \), and a factor bigger than 1 in size means runaway growth.

The practical lesson: when training and your cost is going up or printing nan (not-a-number, from overflow), your first suspect is "learning rate too high." Cut it by 10x and try again. When the cost barely moves, your learning rate may be too small (or your features need scaling — see pitfalls). A good habit is to try \( \alpha \in \{0.001, 0.01, 0.1\} \) and watch the cost curve.

Left: tiny steps crawl down. Middle: well-sized steps converge cleanly. Right: oversized steps bounce up the walls and fly apart (divergence).

Batch, mini-batch, and stochastic gradient descent

Every gradient formula above sums over all \( n \) examples. With a few hundred rows that's fine. With millions (or the trillions of tokens an LLM trains on), computing the full sum for every single step is wastefully slow. The three flavours differ only in how much data you look at before taking each step:

  • Batch gradient descent. Use all examples to compute the gradient for each step. Pros: the gradient is exact, so the path is smooth and stable. Cons: each step is expensive; one step needs a full pass over the data. This is what we'll code from scratch — it's the clearest.
  • Stochastic gradient descent (SGD). Use one randomly-chosen example per step. Pros: dirt cheap per step, so you take many steps fast; the randomness can even jiggle you out of shallow dips. Cons: each gradient is a noisy estimate of the true one, so the path zig-zags toward the minimum instead of gliding.
  • Mini-batch gradient descent. Use a small group — a mini-batch, typically 32 to 256 examples — per step. This is the practical sweet spot everyone actually uses: far cheaper than batch, far less noisy than pure SGD, and it maps beautifully onto GPU hardware. One full pass through all the mini-batches is called an epoch.

A useful mental model: batch GD takes a few confident strides; SGD takes many tipsy little steps; mini-batch takes brisk, mostly-sober steps. All three head downhill; they just trade accuracy-per-step against steps-per-second. You'll lean on mini-batch SGD constantly once we reach neural networks in Module 10 and Module 11 — Training Deep Networks That Actually Work.

In code: from scratch

Let's fit a line \( \hat{y} = wx + b \) to data that secretly follows \( y \approx 2x + 1 \) (plus a little noise), using nothing but NumPy and the exact MSE gradients above. We'll print the cost every so often so you can watch it fall.

import numpy as np # --- make some data that roughly follows y = 2x + 1 --- rng = np.random.default_rng(0) # fixed seed -> reproducible x = np.linspace(0, 5, 50) # 50 inputs from 0 to 5 y = 2.0 * x + 1.0 + rng.normal(0, 0.5, size=x.shape) # true line + noise n = len(x) # --- start the knobs at zero --- w, b = 0.0, 0.0 alpha = 0.01 # learning rate epochs = 1000 # how many full passes (batch GD) def cost(w, b): yhat = w * x + b return np.mean((yhat - y) ** 2) # mean squared error for epoch in range(epochs): yhat = w * x + b # predictions for ALL points error = yhat - y # residuals (y-hat minus y) # exact MSE gradients: dw = (2 / n) * np.sum(error * x) # dJ/dw db = (2 / n) * np.sum(error) # dJ/db # one synchronized step downhill: w = w - alpha * dw b = b - alpha * db if epoch % 200 == 0: print(f"epoch {epoch:4d} cost {cost(w, b):.4f} w {w:.3f} b {b:.3f}") print(f"\nlearned: w = {w:.3f}, b = {b:.3f} (true was w=2, b=1)")

Running this prints a cost that drops every line and parameters that climb toward the truth, ending near \( w \approx 2 \), \( b \approx 1 \). Your exact decimals depend on the random noise, but the shape of the output is always the same — something like:

epoch 0 cost 31.1602 w 0.395 b 0.121 epoch 200 cost 0.1789 w 2.130 b 0.729 epoch 400 cost 0.1784 w 2.121 b 0.757 epoch 600 cost 0.1784 w 2.118 b 0.768 epoch 800 cost 0.1783 w 2.117 b 0.772 learned: w = 2.116, b = 0.773 (true was w=2, b=1)

Read the story in those numbers: the cost crashes from over 30 down to roughly 0.18 and then stops improving — that floor isn't a failure, it's the leftover noise we sprinkled in (we can't fit randomness). With noise standard deviation 0.5 you'd expect a floor near \( 0.5^2 = 0.25 \); we land a touch below it (about 0.18) because the fitted line also soaks up a little of the random wiggle in these 50 particular points. The learned \( w \approx 2.12 \) sits right next to the true \( 2 \), and \( b \approx 0.77 \) lands in the neighbourhood of the true \( 1 \) — it doesn't hit it exactly because the noise in this small sample pulls the best-fit line slightly off the underlying truth. The two lines you'd flag if something broke: a cost that rises (learning rate too high) or a cost that's still falling fast at the last epoch (run more epochs, or raise the rate). If you set a different seed in default_rng you'll get slightly different numbers — that's expected and fine.

In code: the library way

Once you understand the loop, scikit-learn does it for you. SGDRegressor is linear regression trained by (mini-)stochastic gradient descent — the library version of everything above. One important real-world detail it forces on you: scale your features first (see pitfalls for why). We chain a scaler and the regressor with a Pipeline so the scaling is handled automatically.

import numpy as np from sklearn.linear_model import SGDRegressor from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler from sklearn.pipeline import make_pipeline rng = np.random.default_rng(0) x = np.linspace(0, 5, 50) y = 2.0 * x + 1.0 + rng.normal(0, 0.5, size=x.shape) X = x.reshape(-1, 1) # sklearn wants a 2D array of features model = make_pipeline( StandardScaler(), # center & scale x -> faster, stabler GD SGDRegressor(loss="squared_error", # plain MSE (the cost we used) learning_rate="constant", eta0=0.05, # eta0 is alpha max_iter=1000, random_state=0) ) model.fit(X, y) pred = model.predict([[2.5]]) # predict y when x = 2.5 (truth ~ 6.0) print(f"prediction at x=2.5: {pred[0]:.3f}") print(f"R^2 on training data: {model.score(X, y):.3f}")

This prints a prediction near \( 6.0 \) for \( x = 2.5 \) (since \( 2 \times 2.5 + 1 = 6 \)) and an \( R^2 \) close to \( 1.0 \), meaning the line explains almost all the variation in \( y \). Note eta0 is scikit-learn's name for the learning rate \( \alpha \). For a closed-form, no-learning-rate fit you'd reach for LinearRegression (Module 3) — but SGDRegressor is the one that scales to huge data and shares its DNA with neural-network training.

Common pitfalls & misconceptions

  • "Bigger learning rate = faster learning." Up to a point, yes — then it flips. Too big and you overshoot the minimum and the cost grows every step until it overflows to nan. If cost ever rises, your first fix is to shrink \( \alpha \) by 10x.
  • Forgetting to scale features. If one feature ranges 0–1 and another ranges 0–100000, the cost landscape becomes a long, skinny ravine instead of a round bowl. Gradient descent then zig-zags painfully slowly across the narrow direction. Standardizing features (subtract the mean, divide by the standard deviation) rounds out the bowl and is essential for GD-based methods. We'll go deep on this in Module 7 — Feature Engineering and Preprocessing.
  • Updating one parameter using the other's already-updated value. Compute all gradients from the current parameters first, then apply every update. Interleaving them breaks the math.
  • Thinking gradient descent always finds the best possible answer. For the bowl-shaped (convex) costs of linear and logistic regression it does — there's one valley. For neural networks the landscape has many valleys (local minima) and flat plateaus; GD finds a good minimum, not provably the global one. In practice good-enough is what we ship.
  • Confusing the learning rate with the number of epochs. \( \alpha \) sets how far each step goes; epochs set how many steps. A too-small \( \alpha \) can be partly rescued by more epochs, but a too-big \( \alpha \) cannot be fixed by any number of epochs — it diverges.
  • Dropping or mismatching the factor of 2 in the gradient. The MSE gradient has a \( \frac{2}{n} \). If you accidentally use \( \frac{1}{n} \), nothing breaks catastrophically — it's mathematically equivalent to halving your learning rate. But know which convention you're using so your \( \alpha \) means what you think.

Exercises

  1. Using \( J(w) = (w-3)^2 \) with derivative \( 2(w-3) \), start at \( w = 5 \) with \( \alpha = 0.1 \) and compute the first two updates by hand. Is the cost decreasing?
    Show solution

    Step 1: slope \( = 2(5-3) = 4 \); \( w = 5 - 0.1 \times 4 = 4.6 \). Step 2: slope \( = 2(4.6-3) = 3.2 \); \( w = 4.6 - 0.1 \times 3.2 = 4.28 \). The values \( 5 \to 4.6 \to 4.28 \) move down toward 3 (this time from the right, so \( w \) decreases). Cost before: \( (5-3)^2 = 4 \); after step 1: \( (4.6-3)^2 = 2.56 \); after step 2: \( (4.28-3)^2 \approx 1.64 \). Decreasing, as expected.

  2. For the same bowl starting at \( w = 0 \), what does \( \alpha = 1.0 \) do? Compute two steps and describe the behaviour.
    Show solution

    Step 1: slope \( = 2(0-3) = -6 \); \( w = 0 - 1.0 \times (-6) = 6 \). Step 2: slope \( = 2(6-3) = 6 \); \( w = 6 - 1.0 \times 6 = 0 \). You bounce \( 0 \to 6 \to 0 \to 6 \to \dots \) forever, mirror-imaged across the minimum at 3, never getting closer. The distance-multiplier is \( 1 - 2\alpha = 1 - 2 = -1 \): magnitude exactly 1, so it neither converges nor diverges — it oscillates. Any \( \alpha \) larger than 1 would diverge; the borderline is at \( \alpha = 1 \).

  3. Take the 2-point dataset \( (1,2), (2,3) \) again, but start from the result of one step we computed earlier: \( w = 0.8 \), \( b = 0.5 \), with \( \alpha = 0.1 \), \( n = 2 \). Do one more full GD step (both parameters) and confirm cost drops below 0.65.
    Show solution

    Predict: \( \hat{y}_1 = 0.8(1)+0.5 = 1.3 \), \( \hat{y}_2 = 0.8(2)+0.5 = 2.1 \). Residuals: \( 1.3-2 = -0.7 \), \( 2.1-3 = -0.9 \). Slopes: \( \frac{\partial J}{\partial w} = \frac{2}{2}[(-0.7)(1) + (-0.9)(2)] = -0.7 - 1.8 = -2.5 \); \( \frac{\partial J}{\partial b} = \frac{2}{2}[(-0.7)+(-0.9)] = -1.6 \). Update: \( w = 0.8 - 0.1(-2.5) = 1.05 \); \( b = 0.5 - 0.1(-1.6) = 0.66 \). New predictions: \( \hat{y}_1 = 1.05+0.66 = 1.71 \), \( \hat{y}_2 = 2.10+0.66 = 2.76 \); residuals \( -0.29, -0.24 \); cost \( = \frac{1}{2}(0.0841 + 0.0576) = \frac{1}{2}(0.1417) \approx 0.071 \). It dropped from 0.65 to about 0.071 — still falling toward the true line \( y = x + 1 \) (where \( w=1, b=1 \)).

  4. In the from-scratch NumPy code, why do we compute dw and db from yhat before updating w and b, rather than updating w and then recomputing yhat for db? What could go wrong otherwise?
    Show solution

    The gradient is defined at a single point \( (w, b) \) — both partial derivatives describe the slope of the landscape at the current location. If you update w first and then recompute error for db, you'd be measuring \( b \)'s slope at a different point than \( w \)'s, so the two updates no longer form one coherent downhill step. The result is a subtly wrong trajectory that can slow convergence or, in nastier landscapes, destabilize it. Always snapshot the predictions/residuals once, derive every gradient from that snapshot, then apply all updates together.

  5. You train a model and the printed cost goes \( 12 \to 40 \to 380 \to \text{nan} \). Name the most likely cause and give two concrete things to try.
    Show solution

    The cost is exploding, the classic signature of a learning rate that is too high — steps overshoot the minimum and grow each time until the numbers overflow to nan. Two fixes: (1) reduce \( \alpha \) by a factor of 10 (e.g. 0.05 → 0.005) and rerun; (2) standardize your features (subtract mean, divide by standard deviation) so the cost bowl is rounder and tolerant of larger steps. If both fail, double-check you didn't drop the \( \frac{1}{n} \) normalization in the gradient, which inflates the effective step size on large datasets.
