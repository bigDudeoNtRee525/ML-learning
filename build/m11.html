Module 11

Training Deep Networks That Actually Work

01 · the part nobody tells you

Your network trains. Terribly. Welcome.

You did everything right. You stacked the layers, you picked a loss, you hit run. And the loss sits there at 0.69 like a corpse — or worse, it sprints to nan in four steps and dies screaming.

Here is the thing they don't put on the brochure: getting a deep net to learn at all is almost never about a clever architecture. It's about a dozen unglamorous knobs — the optimizer, the learning rate, normalization, dropout, and remembering one specific line that everyone ships broken at least once. A team can have a brilliant model that outputs garbage because one knob is wrong.

This module is the field manual for those knobs. By the end, "my loss is nan and I don't know why" goes from a mystery to a 5-minute fix. But first, a gut check.

A beginner's training loop runs with zero errors, but the loss bounces around and never settles. No crash, no traceback. What's the single most likely culprit?

The famous one-line bug is a missing optimizer.zero_grad(). PyTorch accumulates gradients on purpose — each backward() adds to .grad instead of replacing it. Forget to wipe them and step 2 moves by the sum of step 1 + step 2's gradients, step 3 by the sum of three, and so on. Your effective step size silently balloons and training thrashes. It runs clean. It just doesn't learn. You'll watch this exact pile-up happen with real numbers in a minute.

02 · the one trick that makes it possible

The receipt that remembers itself

Back in Module 10 you hand-derived backpropagation for a tiny network to prove there's no magic inside. That was the right way to learn it. It is also nobody's idea of fun when the network has a million parameters. Real practitioners never hand-derive gradients. They describe the network, hand over the data, and a framework computes every gradient automatically. For most of the field that framework is PyTorch, and its superpower has a name: autograd.

The mental model: imagine every arithmetic operation your code does gets written on a long receipt, in order — "multiplied a by b, then added c, then squared it." When you finally ask "how does the final number depend on the first input?", you don't re-derive anything. You read the receipt backwards, applying the chain rule one line at a time, and the gradient falls out. PyTorch keeps this receipt automatically as your code runs (it calls it the computation graph), and .backward() is the act of reading it backwards. You write the forward math once, in ordinary Python, and the gradient comes free.

The second gift is organization. A real network is a stack of layers, each with its own weights. nn.Module is a container that holds layers, knows all their learnable parameters, and runs them in order when you call it. You describe what the network computes; PyTorch tracks which numbers are learnable and how to update them.

Lock this in. Autograd is not a new idea — it's the chain rule from Module 1, executed mechanically, and the same downhill step from Module 4. You're not learning new math here. You're learning who does it for you.
03 · prove it to yourself, by hand

Don't trust the robot until you've checked it

Before you lean on autograd for a million parameters, verify it on one you can do on paper. Take the simplest function with one input:

\[ y = (x - 2)^2 \]

x the single input we'll differentiate with respect to. y the output — a parabola with its lowest point at \(x=2\).

We want the slope \( \frac{dy}{dx} \) at \( x = 5 \). Chain rule: bring the power down, multiply by the derivative of the inside (which is \(1\)):

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = 2\,(x - 2)\cdot 1 = 2(x-2) \]

Now commit to a number before you scroll. Plug in \(x=5\). What's the slope?

\( \left.\frac{dy}{dx}\right|_{x=5} = 2(5-2) = \;? \)

\( 2(5-2) = 2\times 3 = 6 \). And read what that means: at \(x=5\), nudging \(x\) up a hair makes \(y\) climb about 6× as fast — steep, because the parabola bottoms out at \(x=2\) and we're well to the right of it. (C is the trap: the slope is only 0 at the minimum, \(x=2\), not here.)

Now ask PyTorch the same question. Predict the printed value before you run it — you already know it's 6:

import torch x = torch.tensor(5.0, requires_grad=True) # x = 5, and "watch its gradient" y = (x - 2) ** 2 # build the receipt: subtract 2, then square y.backward() # read the receipt backwards (chain rule) print(x.grad) # the slope dy/dx at x = 5

It prints tensor(6.) — identical to your hand answer. Behind the scenes PyTorch recorded two operations (a subtraction and a squaring); backward() multiplied their local derivatives: squaring contributes \(2(x-2)=6\), subtraction contributes \(1\), giving \(6\times 1 = 6\). One tensor, one method call, and the chain rule you'd grind through by hand for a deep net is done for you. That equivalence — autograd is the chain rule, automated — is the foundation of everything below.

04 · the ritual you'll run for the rest of your life

Five steps, always this order

With autograd and nn.Module in hand, training collapses into a short fixed ritual. Memorize the order — it never changes:

forward  →  loss  →  backward  →  step  →  zero-grad  ↻

forward compute predictions. loss score how wrong they are. backward read the receipt → gradients. step nudge every parameter downhill. zero-grad wipe the gradients before the next batch.

Skip that last box and you're back to the gradient pile-up from beat 01 — the single most common beginner bug. The optimizer does the step and the wipe. Plain stochastic gradient descent applies the exact rule from Module 4, per parameter \(\theta\):

\[ \theta := \theta - \alpha \, \frac{\partial L}{\partial \theta} \]

\(\theta\) (theta) any single weight or bias. \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \theta}\) how much the loss \(L\) changes when you nudge \(\theta\) (its gradient). \(\alpha\) (alpha) the learning rate — your step size. := "becomes."

That is all optimizer.step() does for plain SGD. The fancier optimizers below change only the right-hand side: how the raw gradient becomes a step.

05 · pick your stepper

SGD vs momentum vs Adam

Plain SGD steps straight along the current gradient. The catch: if the loss surface is a long narrow valley — steep across, gently sloped along — the raw gradient mostly points across, so SGD zig-zags off the walls and crawls along the floor.

SGD with momentum keeps a running average of recent gradients — a velocity — and steps with that. Let \(v\) be the velocity, \(\beta\) (beta, usually \(0.9\)) the fraction of old velocity kept:

\[ v := \beta\, v + \frac{\partial L}{\partial \theta}, \qquad \theta := \theta - \alpha\, v \]

\(v\) velocity, a memory of where you've been heading. \(\beta\) how much of that memory you keep each step. The across-valley wobbles flip sign and cancel; the along-valley push accumulates — a heavy ball that ignores small bumps and builds speed downhill.

Adam ("adaptive moment estimation") goes further: it gives each parameter its own learning rate, auto-scaled by how big that parameter's recent gradients were. Tiny-gradient parameters get relatively bigger steps; wild-swinging ones get smaller, steadier steps.

You throw an untuned model at a new dataset and just want it to learn something fast, with minimal fiddling. Plain SGD with a hand-picked learning rate, or Adam at its default? What usually happens?

Adam usually just works with its default \(lr=0.001\) because it adapts the per-parameter rate for you — which is why it's the default for almost everything, and what you should reach for first. The honest footnote: plain SGD-with-momentum, carefully tuned, sometimes generalizes a touch better, which is why research papers still use it. But "carefully tuned" is the load-bearing phrase. As a beginner: Adam.

06 · stop it from cheating

Dropout, weight decay, and the test-time gotcha

Deep nets have so many parameters they can memorize the training set — fitting the noise, not the pattern. That's the overfitting from Module 6. Two cheap, standard cures:

Dropout: during training, randomly switch off a fraction \(p\) of neurons (zero their outputs) every forward pass. The network can't lean on any one neuron — it might vanish next batch — so it spreads the work around and learns redundant, robust features.

Here's the trap that bites everyone once:

You trained with nn.Dropout(0.5). Now you're evaluating on the test set. What does dropout do at test time?

Dropout is active only during training. At evaluation, every neuron is used (the layer rescales to keep the math consistent). This is exactly why model.train() and model.eval() exist and why forgetting to flip the mode gives you "my test accuracy is mysteriously unstable." If you forget model.eval(), you really do get answer B's chaos — randomly worse numbers every run. Flip the mode, always.

Weight decay: gently shrink every weight toward zero each step by penalizing large weights. It's the deep-learning name for L2 regularization from Module 6 — it discourages any one weight from growing huge, keeping the function smooth. One argument: weight_decay=1e-4 in the optimizer.

07 · keep the signal alive

Normalization, initialization, and schedules

As data flows through many layers, the scale of the numbers drifts — exploding huge or shrinking to nothing — which makes training slow and brittle. Normalization layers re-center and re-scale activations partway through (roughly mean \(0\), spread \(1\)), then let the network rescale them back via two learnable knobs. Batch norm computes stats across the examples in the current mini-batch (great for vision, Module 12). Layer norm computes them across the features of each single example (the Transformer standard, Module 14, because it doesn't depend on batch size). You don't need the formulas to use them — just know that a normalization layer between linear layers usually trains faster and tolerates a higher learning rate.

Initialization. Every weight needs a starting value. Set them all to zero and every neuron in a layer computes the same thing and gets the same gradient forever — they never differentiate (the "symmetry" problem). So we start with small random weights — but the scale matters enormously. A bit too large and the signal multiplies up layer after layer until it explodes to nan; a bit too small and it shrinks until the early layers get no gradient and stop learning — the vanishing gradient. The fix is principled init (Kaiming/He for ReLU, Xavier/Glorot for tanh) that keeps the signal's spread constant across layers. PyTorch's built-in layers already use sane defaults, so this mostly matters for custom layers — but recognizing the failure mode is the real win.

Learning-rate schedules. A single fixed rate is a compromise: big enough for fast early progress, then too big to settle gently. A schedule changes the rate over time — usually starting moderate and decaying toward zero. Common: step decay (×\(0.1\) every few epochs), cosine annealing (smooth cosine descent), and a brief warmup (start tiny, ramp up over the first few hundred steps — standard for Transformers). Take bold strides while far from the valley floor, then tiptoe as you arrive.

08 · watch the gradient pile up

From scratch: why zero_grad() isn't optional

Before the framework hides it, see autograd do the chain rule on a longer computation — and prove it by hand first. Compute \( z = (3w+1)^2 \) at \( w = 2 \). By hand: \( \frac{dz}{dw} = 2(3w+1)\cdot 3 = 6(3w+1) \); at \(w=2\) that's \(6\times 7 = 42\). Predict the prints, then run:

import torch w = torch.tensor(2.0, requires_grad=True) u = 3 * w + 1 # intermediate value u = 7 z = u ** 2 # z = 49 z.backward() print("z =", z.item()) # 49.0 print("dz/dw =", w.grad.item()) # 42.0 -> matches 6*(3*2+1) = 42 # A subtle, important point: gradients ACCUMULATE. # If we backprop again WITHOUT zeroing, grads add up. w2 = torch.tensor(2.0, requires_grad=True) for step in range(3): out = (3 * w2 + 1) ** 2 out.backward() print(f"after backward #{step+1}, w2.grad = {w2.grad.item()}") # prints 42, then 84, then 126 -- each call ADDS 42, it does not replace.

The first block prints z = 49.0 and dz/dw = 42.0, matching the hand calc exactly. The second block is the whole point of this beat: each backward() adds to .grad rather than overwriting it, so you get \(42, 84, 126\). PyTorch accumulates on purpose (occasionally useful), but in a normal loop you must wipe each iteration — otherwise step two uses the sum of step one's and step two's gradients, and training quietly corrupts itself. That's beat 01's bug, with receipts.

Now a complete from-scratch loop that uses autograd for gradients but does the optimizer step by hand, so nothing is hidden. We fit \( y \approx 2x + 1 \), updating weight and bias manually inside torch.no_grad() ("don't track these update ops — they're not part of the model's computation"):

import torch torch.manual_seed(0) x = torch.linspace(0, 5, 50).reshape(-1, 1) # 50 inputs, shape (50, 1) y = 2.0 * x + 1.0 + 0.3 * torch.randn(x.shape) # true line + noise w = torch.zeros(1, 1, requires_grad=True) # weight, tracked b = torch.zeros(1, requires_grad=True) # bias, tracked lr = 0.05 for epoch in range(1000): yhat = x @ w + b # forward: matrix-multiply, shape (50, 1) loss = ((yhat - y) ** 2).mean() # mean squared error loss.backward() # autograd fills w.grad and b.grad with torch.no_grad(): # update WITHOUT recording on the receipt w -= lr * w.grad b -= lr * b.grad w.grad.zero_() # wipe gradients for the next epoch b.grad.zero_() if epoch % 200 == 0: print(f"epoch {epoch:4d} loss {loss.item():.4f} " f"w {w.item():.3f} b {b.item():.3f}") print(f"\nlearned w = {w.item():.3f}, b = {b.item():.3f} (true 2 and 1)")

The loss falls steadily and the parameters climb toward truth, ending near \( w \approx 2 \), \( b \approx 1 \) (the floor the loss settles at is just the noise we added, which no model can fit). This is the canonical loop, naked: forward, backward, step, zero-grad. The library version below changes exactly one thing — an optimizer object does the step-and-zero for you. The four phases are identical.

09 · find the bug

This loop runs clean. It still won't learn.

A teammate wrote the training loop below. It throws no errors. The accuracy never improves and the loss refuses to settle. Read it. Find the bug before you open the answer — you already have everything you need.

for epoch in range(20): model.train() # dropout ON for xb, yb in train_dl: xb, yb = xb.to(device), yb.to(device) logits = model(xb) # forward loss = loss_fn(logits, yb) # loss loss.backward() # backward (autograd) optimizer.step() # update every parameter # something is missing on the line right after this one <-- bug 1 correct = total = 0 # a mode is never set before eval <-- bug 2 with torch.no_grad(): for xb, yb in test_dl: xb, yb = xb.to(device), yb.to(device) preds = model(xb).argmax(dim=1) correct += (preds == yb).sum().item() total += yb.size(0) print(f"epoch {epoch:2d} test accuracy {correct/total:.3f}")
Show the bugs & the fix

Bug 1 — the famous one. There's no optimizer.zero_grad(). Gradients accumulate every batch (42 → 84 → 126, remember?), so each step moves by the growing sum of all prior gradients — the effective step size balloons and training thrashes. Add optimizer.zero_grad() right after optimizer.step() (or at the top of the inner loop; the only rule is it sits between successive backward() calls).

Bug 2 — the sneaky one. Before the evaluation loop there's no model.eval(), so the model is still in train mode from model.train() above — dropout stays ON during testing. Your test accuracy comes out noisy and randomly worse every run. Add model.eval() before the with torch.no_grad() block. (And model.train() back at the top of each epoch, which this loop already does.)

Two missing lines. Zero error messages. This is what "training that actually works" really means.

10 · the real thing

The library way — a production-shaped loop

Here's the complete, runnable PyTorch loop with everything a real project has: a Dataset and DataLoader for mini-batching, an nn.Module with dropout, Adam with weight decay, the canonical loop, train/eval switching, device handling, and a saved checkpoint. Small synthetic data so it runs in seconds — but the structure is identical to MNIST. Swap the dataset and you're training on real images.

import torch from torch import nn from torch.utils.data import TensorDataset, DataLoader torch.manual_seed(0) device = "cuda" if torch.cuda.is_available() else "cpu" # use GPU if present # --- 1. Data: a nonlinear 2-feature, 2-class problem --- n = 1000 X = torch.randn(n, 2) # label 1 if inside a tilted region, else 0 (a curved boundary) y = ((X[:, 0] * 1.5 - X[:, 1] + 0.4 * X[:, 0] ** 2) > 0).long() split = 800 train_ds = TensorDataset(X[:split], y[:split]) test_ds = TensorDataset(X[split:], y[split:]) train_dl = DataLoader(train_ds, batch_size=32, shuffle=True) # mini-batches test_dl = DataLoader(test_ds, batch_size=32) # --- 2. Model: a small MLP with a hidden layer, ReLU, and dropout --- class Net(nn.Module): def __init__(self): super().__init__() self.net = nn.Sequential( nn.Linear(2, 32), # 2 inputs -> 32 hidden units nn.ReLU(), nn.Dropout(0.2), # active only in train mode nn.Linear(32, 2), # 32 -> 2 class scores (logits) ) def forward(self, x): return self.net(x) model = Net().to(device) # model on the chosen device loss_fn = nn.CrossEntropyLoss() # correct loss for classification optimizer = torch.optim.Adam(model.parameters(), lr=1e-3, weight_decay=1e-4) # Adam + weight decay # --- 3. The canonical training loop --- for epoch in range(20): model.train() # turn dropout ON for xb, yb in train_dl: xb, yb = xb.to(device), yb.to(device) # data on the SAME device as model logits = model(xb) # forward loss = loss_fn(logits, yb) # loss loss.backward() # backward (autograd) optimizer.step() # update every parameter optimizer.zero_grad() # wipe grads for next batch # --- evaluate each epoch --- model.eval() # turn dropout OFF correct = total = 0 with torch.no_grad(): # no receipt -> faster, less memory for xb, yb in test_dl: xb, yb = xb.to(device), yb.to(device) preds = model(xb).argmax(dim=1) # pick the higher-scoring class correct += (preds == yb).sum().item() total += yb.size(0) if epoch % 5 == 0 or epoch == 19: print(f"epoch {epoch:2d} test accuracy {correct/total:.3f}") # --- 4. Save and reload the trained weights --- torch.save(model.state_dict(), "net.pt") # save just the weights (best practice) fresh = Net().to(device) fresh.load_state_dict(torch.load("net.pt", map_location=device)) fresh.eval() print("reloaded model ready")

This trains in a second or two and prints a test accuracy climbing into the low-to-mid \(0.9\)s as the net learns the curved boundary. Walk the four phases inside the loop and you'll see they are exactly the from-scratch loop, delegated: model(xb) is forward, loss_fn(...) is the loss, loss.backward() is autograd, optimizer.step() is the update, optimizer.zero_grad() is the wipe. Five things lift it from toy to production: (1) DataLoader serves shuffled mini-batches; (2) model.train()/model.eval() flip dropout; (3) torch.no_grad() during eval saves time and memory; (4) every tensor moves to the same device; (5) state_dict() saves the weights so you can ship it. You'll reuse this exact skeleton in Module 12 (CNNs) and Module 13 (Sequences & NLP) with only the model and dataset swapped.

11 · the traps, named

Symptoms → fixes

Every one of these has burned a real engineer this week. Learn the symptom and the symptom tells you the fix.

  • Forgot optimizer.zero_grad(). Loss won't settle or diverges, no error. Gradients accumulated (42 → 84 → 126). Fix: one line per batch.
  • Wrong loss. Use nn.CrossEntropyLoss for classification, nn.MSELoss for regression. Sharp edge: CrossEntropyLoss wants raw logits (it applies softmax internally) — do not add a softmax in your final layer, and targets must be integer class indices, not one-hot.
  • Learning rate off. Loss → nan means too high — divide by 10. Loss barely moves means too low (or you forgot to scale your inputs). Start at Adam lr=1e-3.
  • Wrong mode. model.train() keeps dropout on and batch-norm updating; model.eval() turns dropout off and freezes those stats. Eval in train mode → noisy worse numbers; train in eval mode → regularization disabled. Set the mode explicitly before every phase.
  • Model and data on different devices. Expected all tensors to be on the same device. Move both the model (once, at creation) and every batch (inside the loop) to the same device.
  • Vanishing/exploding gradients. Loss stuck flat (early layers frozen) or blowing to nan is usually init/normalization, not a loop bug. Reach for normalization layers, ReLU over sigmoid in hidden layers, and gradient clipping (torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_) if needed.
  • Logging a tensor with its graph attached. Use loss.item() when logging; accumulate metrics inside torch.no_grad(). Summing raw loss tensors across an epoch keeps the whole graph alive and silently eats your memory.

13 · prove it

A few you should be able to crack cold

  1. By hand, find \( \frac{dy}{dx} \) for \( y = (2x-1)^2 \) at \( x = 3 \). Then write the three lines of PyTorch that confirm it and say what x.grad prints.
    Show solution

    Chain rule: \( \frac{dy}{dx} = 2(2x-1)\cdot 2 = 4(2x-1) \). At \(x=3\): \(4(2\cdot3-1)=4\times 5 = 20\). Code: x = torch.tensor(3.0, requires_grad=True), then y = (2*x - 1)**2, then y.backward(). Afterward x.grad prints tensor(20.).

  2. Unscramble the loop: optimizer.step(), loss = loss_fn(pred, y), optimizer.zero_grad(), pred = model(x), loss.backward(). Then say what breaks without zero_grad().
    Show solution

    (1) pred = model(x) — forward; (2) loss = loss_fn(pred, y) — loss; (3) loss.backward() — gradients; (4) optimizer.step() — update; (5) optimizer.zero_grad() — clear. (Putting zero_grad() at the top of the next iteration is equally valid — it just must sit between successive backward() calls.) Without it, gradients accumulate, so each step moves by the sum of all gradients so far; the effective step grows without bound and training destabilizes.

  3. A friend's 3-class model ends with nn.Linear(64, 3) then nn.Softmax(dim=1), and uses nn.CrossEntropyLoss. What's wrong, how do you fix it?
    Show solution

    nn.CrossEntropyLoss already applies (log-)softmax internally and expects raw logits. The extra nn.Softmax applies softmax twice, flattening the scores into a near-uniform range that gives weak, badly-scaled gradients — trains slowly and badly. Fix: delete the nn.Softmax; let nn.Linear(64, 3) feed raw logits straight into the loss. Need probabilities at inference? Apply softmax only then, outside the loss.

  4. "RuntimeError: Expected all tensors to be on the same device, but found at least two devices, cuda:0 and cpu." The model was made with Net().to("cuda"). Diagnose and give the one-line fix.
    Show solution

    Model is on GPU, the input batch is still on CPU, so weights (cuda) and data (cpu) can't multiply. Fix inside the loop: xb, yb = xb.to(device), yb.to(device) right after unpacking the batch. Model once at creation, every batch every iteration — same device.

  5. Your deep net's loss goes 0.7 → 0.69 → 0.69 → 0.69 … forever, and you've confirmed the learning rate is fine. List three structural fixes with reasoning.
    Show solution

    Flat-from-the-start loss usually means almost no gradient reaches the early layers — a vanishing-gradient / init symptom. (1) Add normalization (e.g. nn.BatchNorm1d between linear layers) to keep activations and gradients at a healthy scale across depth. (2) ReLU instead of sigmoid/tanh in hidden layers — sigmoids saturate (slope → 0 for large inputs), choking the gradient; ReLU passes it through for positive inputs. (3) Fix initialization — for custom layers, apply Kaiming (He) init so the signal's spread stays constant across layers. (Opposite failure, loss → nan? Add gradient clipping.) Same root cause throughout: keep a usable gradient flowing from the loss all the way back to layer one.
