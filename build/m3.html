02 · commit before you reveal

One dot goes rogue. Which way does the line tip?

You've got a tidy fitted line through the cloud. Now imagine the rightmost dot jumps straight up — way up, off into the corner — and you re-fit. Before you read on, lock in a guess:

You yank the rightmost data point way up. After re-fitting, what happens to the best line?

The line tilts to chase it. Here's the deep reason: the line is fit to make the squared gaps small, and squaring punishes big misses far harder than small ones. A point 10 away from the line contributes 100 to the cost; a point 1 away contributes just 1. So one wild dot screams louder than ten tame ones, and the line lurches toward it — getting slightly worse for everyone else. That's why a single outlier can wreck a fit, and it falls straight out of the ruler we're about to define.

03 · now you've earned the math

The line is two knobs. The ruler is one number.

You already used both knobs in the lab. Time to name them. A model is just a rule that turns an input into a prediction. The input is the feature (temperature). The thing you predict is the target (drinks). For one feature, the rule is a straight line:

\[ \hat{y} = w\,x + b \]

x the feature — one input number (a temperature like 28). ŷ ("y-hat") the model's prediction — the hat means "estimated," to keep it apart from the true \(y\). w the weight, a.k.a. slope — how many more drinks per extra degree (the tilt knob). b the bias, a.k.a. intercept — the height knob; the prediction when \(x=0\).

That equation is the model. "Learning" just means picking good \(w\) and \(b\). But picking needs a score — a way to say "this line is worse than that one." That's the cost function, and it's the ruler you wished you had in Beat 1. The standard one for regression is the mean squared error: for each dot, take the gap between prediction and truth, square it, then average.

\[ J(w, b) = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^{m} \left( \hat{y}^{(i)} - y^{(i)} \right)^2 \]

m the number of examples (rows). (i) a superscript meaning "the \(i\)-th example" — an index, not a power, so \(x^{(2)}\) is the 2nd example's feature, not \(x\) squared. ŷ⁽ⁱ⁾ − y⁽ⁱ⁾ the residual — how far off you were on dot \(i\) (the red gap in the lab). J(w,b) the cost — one number for total wrongness. Lower is better; the best line makes it smallest.

Why square the gaps? Two jobs in one move: squaring makes over- and under-shooting both count as positive (so they can't cancel out), and it punishes big misses extra — the exact outlier behaviour you predicted in Beat 2. You may see \( \tfrac{1}{2m} \) elsewhere; the \( \tfrac12 \) just makes a derivative cleaner in Module 4 and changes nothing about which line wins. We use \( \tfrac1m \).

04 · do the ruler by hand

Score a line yourself — predict the MSE first.

Three days of data, one feature. Let \( x \) be a tiny temperature index and \( y \) the drinks sold:

\( (x,y) = (1, 2),\ (2, 2.5),\ (3, 3.5) \), so \( m = 3 \).

Guess the line \( w = 1 \), \( b = 1 \), i.e. \( \hat{y} = x + 1 \). The predictions land at \( 2, 3, 4 \). The residuals (prediction minus truth) are \( 0,\ 0.5,\ 0.5 \). Before you average the squares — guess the MSE.

Residuals are \(0,\ 0.5,\ 0.5\). What's the MSE (mean of the squared residuals)?

Square first, then average. Squares are \(0,\ 0.25,\ 0.25\). Sum is \(0.5\). Divide by \(m=3\): \( J = 0.5/3 \approx 0.167 \). Option B is the classic trap — averaging the raw residuals (which nearly cancel) before squaring. The squaring is the entire personality of MSE; skip it and you've computed something else.

BY HAND the full computation

Here's the worked version of what you just predicted, step by step.

Step 1 — predictions \( \hat{y} = x + 1 \): \(x{=}1\to2\), \(x{=}2\to3\), \(x{=}3\to4\).

Step 2 — residuals \( \hat{y} - y \): \(2-2=0\), \(3-2.5=0.5\), \(4-3.5=0.5\).

Step 3 — squared residuals: \(0^2=0\), \(0.5^2=0.25\), \(0.5^2=0.25\).

Step 4 — average them (the MSE):

\[ J = \frac{0 + 0.25 + 0.25}{3} = \frac{0.5}{3} \approx 0.1667 \]

So our guessed line scores \( J \approx 0.167 \). Is that good? Nothing to compare it to — so let's find the best possible line by hand. For linear regression there's an exact shortcut, no iteration: the normal equation.

\[ \mathbf{w} = \left( \mathbf{X}^{\top} \mathbf{X} \right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}^{\top} \mathbf{y} \]

X the design matrix — one row per example, a leading column of \(1\)s (for the bias) plus a column per feature. Xᵀ X transposed (rows/cols swapped). ⁻¹ matrix inverse. The output \(\mathbf{w}\) holds the bias and slope that minimise \(J\) — the same answer minimising the cost would give, in one shot. Great for small problems; the inverse gets pricey on big ones, which is why Module 4 brings the iterative version.

BY HAND solve for the best line

Same tiny dataset. We'll grind the normal equation by hand and confirm it beats the guess.

Step 5 — design matrix (column of ones for the bias, then \(x\)):

\[ \mathbf{X} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{y} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 2.5 \\ 3.5 \end{bmatrix} \]

Step 6 — \( \mathbf{X}^{\top}\mathbf{X} \): with \( \sum x = 6 \) and \( \sum x^2 = 14 \):

\[ \mathbf{X}^{\top}\mathbf{X} = \begin{bmatrix} m & \sum x \\ \sum x & \sum x^2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 6 \\ 6 & 14 \end{bmatrix} \]

Step 7 — \( \mathbf{X}^{\top}\mathbf{y} \): with \( \sum y = 8 \) and \( \sum xy = 2+5+10.5 = 17.5 \):

\[ \mathbf{X}^{\top}\mathbf{y} = \begin{bmatrix} \sum y \\ \sum x y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 8 \\ 17.5 \end{bmatrix} \]

Step 8 — invert the 2×2. For \( \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} \), the inverse is \( \frac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{bmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{bmatrix} \). Here \( ad-bc = (3)(14)-(6)(6) = 42-36 = 6 \).

\[ (\mathbf{X}^{\top}\mathbf{X})^{-1} = \frac{1}{6}\begin{bmatrix} 14 & -6 \\ -6 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \]

Step 9 — multiply for the weights:

\[ \mathbf{w} = \frac{1}{6}\begin{bmatrix} 14 & -6 \\ -6 & 3 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 8 \\ 17.5 \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{6}\begin{bmatrix} 112 - 105 \\ -48 + 52.5 \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{6}\begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ 4.5 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1.1667 \\ 0.75 \end{bmatrix} \]

So the best line is \( b \approx 1.1667 \) (intercept) and \( w = 0.75 \) (slope): \( \hat{y} = 0.75x + 1.1667 \).

Step 10 — sanity-check its cost. Predictions: \( 1.9167,\ 2.6667,\ 3.4167 \). Residuals: \( -0.0833,\ 0.1667,\ -0.0833 \). Squared: \( 0.00694,\ 0.02778,\ 0.00694 \). MSE \( \approx 0.01389 \) — far below the guess's \(0.167\). The best line is about 12× better. That gap is exactly what your eye left on the table in Beat 1.

05 · the same math, in NumPy

Predict the printout, then run it.

This code does exactly what you just did by hand: score the guess, then solve the normal equation. Before you run it — what four numbers does it print?

import numpy as np # Tiny dataset from the worked example x = np.array([1.0, 2.0, 3.0]) y = np.array([2.0, 2.5, 3.5]) m = len(x) # --- Score a guessed line w=1, b=1 --- w_guess, b_guess = 1.0, 1.0 y_hat = w_guess * x + b_guess # predictions: [2, 3, 4] residuals = y_hat - y # [0, 0.5, 0.5] mse_guess = np.mean(residuals ** 2) # average of squared residuals print("Guessed predictions:", y_hat) print("Guessed MSE:", round(mse_guess, 4)) # --- Solve for the BEST line with the normal equation --- # Build the design matrix X: a column of ones (for b) and the x column X = np.column_stack([np.ones(m), x]) # shape (3, 2) # w_best = (X^T X)^(-1) X^T y w_best = np.linalg.inv(X.T @ X) @ X.T @ y b_opt, w_opt = w_best[0], w_best[1] # first entry is bias, second is slope print("Best intercept b:", round(b_opt, 4)) print("Best slope w:", round(w_opt, 4)) # Cost of the best line y_hat_best = X @ w_best mse_best = np.mean((y_hat_best - y) ** 2) print("Best MSE:", round(mse_best, 5))
Reveal the output

It prints Guessed MSE: 0.1667, then Best intercept b: 1.1667, Best slope w: 0.75, and Best MSE: 0.01389 — exactly your hand calculations. X.T @ X uses @ for matrix multiply and X.T for the transpose. In real code prefer np.linalg.solve(X.T @ X, X.T @ y) over an explicit inverse — more accurate and faster — but the inverse makes the formula obvious here.

06 · find the bug

This MSE function is wrong. Spot it cold.

A beginner wrote this to compute the mean squared error. It runs without an error and returns a number — a plausible-looking number. But it's not the MSE. Find the bug before you open the fix.

import numpy as np def mse(x, y, w, b): y_hat = w * x + b residuals = y_hat - y total = np.sum(residuals) # <-- something is wrong on this line return total / len(x) x = np.array([1.0, 2.0, 3.0]) y = np.array([2.0, 2.5, 3.5]) print(mse(x, y, 1.0, 1.0)) # prints 0.333..., not 0.1667
Show the bug & why

It sums the raw residuals instead of the squared ones. On our data the residuals are \(0, 0.5, 0.5\); summed and divided by 3 that's \(0.333\), the wrong answer (and the exact Beat-4 trap). Worse: on real data, positive and negative residuals would cancel, so a terrible line could score near zero. Fix — square before summing:

total = np.sum(residuals ** 2) — now it returns \(0.1667\), the true MSE. The squaring isn't decoration; it's what makes the number mean "wrongness" at all.

07 · scaling up

From one feature to many.

Real problems have several features (temperature, day-of-week, whether it rained). Give each its own weight and add them up:

\[ \hat{y} = w_1 x_1 + w_2 x_2 + \dots + w_n x_n + b \]

where \( n \) is the number of features and \( w_j \) is the weight for feature \( j \). Writing a sum for every example gets noisy fast, so we package numbers into vectors and matrices — vectorization — which makes code shorter and dramatically faster. The bias-absorbing trick from the worked example (a constant \(1\) feature whose weight is \(b\)) lets us write the whole dataset's predictions as one multiply:

\[ \hat{\mathbf{y}} = \mathbf{X}\,\mathbf{w} \]

X the design matrix, shape \(m \times (n{+}1)\) — one row per example, a leading column of ones plus one column per feature. w the weight vector of length \(n{+}1\) (its first entry is the bias). ŷ the length-\(m\) vector of all predictions at once. One matrix multiply replaces every loop. (Shaky on matrices? Revisit Module 1.)

08 · the three-line version

scikit-learn: same answer, less typing.

scikit-learn wraps all of that behind fit/predict. You hand it features and targets; it finds the best \(w\) and \(b\). One catch on shape: it expects features as a 2D array (rows = examples, columns = features), so we reshape our single feature into a column. Predict what coef_ and intercept_ will be before you run it — you already know the answer from Beat 4.

import numpy as np from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression x = np.array([1.0, 2.0, 3.0]).reshape(-1, 1) # shape (3, 1): one column = one feature y = np.array([2.0, 2.5, 3.5]) model = LinearRegression() # by default it learns an intercept (bias) too model.fit(x, y) # this is where the learning happens print("slope (coef_):", model.coef_) # the learned weight(s) print("intercept (b):", model.intercept_) # the learned bias # Predict for new inputs, e.g. x = 4 and x = 5 new_x = np.array([[4.0], [5.0]]) print("predictions:", model.predict(new_x)) # A built-in goodness-of-fit score (R^2): 1.0 is perfect print("R^2 on training data:", round(model.score(x, y), 4))
Reveal the output

slope (coef_): [0.75] and intercept (b): 1.1666... — the same answer the normal equation gave, because under the hood it solves essentially the same least-squares problem. Predictions for \(x=4,5\) are about \(4.17\) and \(4.92\). The R^2 (about \(0.96\)) is the fraction of the target's variation the line explains — you'll meet it properly in Module 6. Three lines, a working model, and you know exactly what each one does.

09 · the traps that bite beginners

Five ways this lies to you.

  • "The slope proves the feature causes the target." No — regression measures association, not causation. Ice-cream sales and drowning deaths both rise in summer; a regression linking them is real, but hot weather drives both. A model can fit beautifully and still encode a confounder.
  • Forgetting residuals are squared, not absolute. One big miss hurts far more than several small ones — exactly why one outlier yanks the whole line (Beat 2).
  • Ignoring scale with multiple features. A weight of \(0.001\) on "area in m²" and \(5\) on "number of bedrooms" does not mean bedrooms matter more — the features live on different scales. Standardize before comparing importances (Module 7). The normal equation doesn't need scaling, but gradient descent in Module 4 strongly does.
  • Extrapolating far outside the data. A line fit on 10–30°C may predict negative sales at −40°C. The straight-line assumption is only trustworthy inside the range you observed.
  • Reading \( x^{(i)} \) as "x to the power i." The parenthesized superscript is an index naming the example, not an exponent. \( x^{(2)} \) is the 2nd example's feature; \( x^2 \) is x squared.
10 · pull it from your own head

Cold recall — answer first, then flip.

Re-reading feels like learning and mostly isn't. Retrieving is. Answer each in your head, then tap.

In \( \hat{y} = wx + b \), what do \(w\) and \(b\) each control?
tap to flip
w is the slope — tilt of the line, "drinks per extra degree." b is the intercept — height of the line, the prediction at \(x=0\).
How do you compute MSE on three points?
tap to flip
For each point: prediction minus truth = residual. Square each residual. Average the squares. Square before you average — never after.
Why does one outlier wreck a least-squares line?
tap to flip
Squaring makes a gap of 10 cost 100, but a gap of 1 cost only 1. The big miss dominates the total, so the line lurches to chase the outlier.
What does the normal equation give you, and what's the catch?
tap to flip
\( \mathbf{w} = (\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{y} \) — the exact best weights in one shot, no iteration. Catch: the inverse is expensive on huge datasets, so big problems use gradient descent instead.
Why add a column of ones to the design matrix?
tap to flip
It absorbs the bias \(b\) into the weight vector: the ones-column's weight is \(b\). Then \( \hat{\mathbf{y}} = \mathbf{X}\mathbf{w} \) handles slope and intercept in one multiply.

12 · sharpen it

A few to grind (solutions hidden).

  1. Using \( \hat{y} = 2x + 1 \), compute predictions for \( x = 0, 3, 10 \) and state the slope and intercept in plain words.
    Show solution

    \( \hat{y}(0)=1 \), \( \hat{y}(3)=7 \), \( \hat{y}(10)=21 \). Slope 2: the prediction rises by 2 for every +1 in \(x\). Intercept 1: the prediction is 1 when \(x=0\).

  2. For \( (1,1),(2,3),(3,4) \) with guessed \( w=1, b=0 \), compute each residual, each square, and the MSE.
    Show solution

    Predictions \( \hat{y}=x \): \(1,2,3\). Residuals: \(0, -1, -1\). Squares: \(0,1,1\). MSE \(=(0+1+1)/3=2/3 \approx 0.667\).

  3. You fit \( \hat{y} = 0.8\,x_1 + 25\,x_2 + 40 \) for an electricity bill, with \(x_1\) = floor area (m²) and \(x_2\) = number of AC units. Interpret each weight and the intercept.
    Show solution

    The \(0.8\) on \(x_1\): each extra m² adds about \$0.80 to the predicted bill, holding AC count fixed. The \(25\) on \(x_2\): each extra AC adds about \$25, holding area fixed. The intercept \(40\): a (hypothetical) home with zero area and zero AC is predicted \$40 — usually not physically meaningful, just the baseline. "Holding the other fixed" matters: each weight is its feature's effect with the others held constant.

  4. Set up the normal equation for \( (0,1),(1,3),(2,5) \) and find \(w\) and \(b\). Verify by predicting at the three points.
    Show solution

    \( \mathbf{X}=\begin{bmatrix}1&0\\1&1\\1&2\end{bmatrix} \), \( \mathbf{y}=\begin{bmatrix}1\\3\\5\end{bmatrix} \). Then \( \mathbf{X}^{\top}\mathbf{X}=\begin{bmatrix}3&3\\3&5\end{bmatrix} \), \( \mathbf{X}^{\top}\mathbf{y}=\begin{bmatrix}9\\13\end{bmatrix} \). Determinant \(=3(5)-3(3)=6\), inverse \(=\tfrac16\begin{bmatrix}5&-3\\-3&3\end{bmatrix}\). Weights \(=\tfrac16\begin{bmatrix}5(9)-3(13)\\-3(9)+3(13)\end{bmatrix}=\tfrac16\begin{bmatrix}6\\12\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix}\). So \(b=1, w=2\), i.e. \( \hat{y}=2x+1 \). Check: \(x=0\to1\), \(x=1\to3\), \(x=2\to5\) — exact fit (MSE \(=0\)), since the points are perfectly collinear.

  5. A friend regresses temperature on ice-cream sales, then sales on temperature, and is shocked the two slopes aren't reciprocals. What do you tell them?
    Show solution

    Neither is wrong — they minimise different residuals. Regressing \(y\) on \(x\) shrinks vertical gaps in \(y\); the reverse shrinks horizontal gaps in \(x\). Only with a perfect (\(R^2=1\)) fit do the slopes become exact reciprocals; with any scatter they differ. The lesson echoes the causation trap: regression is directional and descriptive, not a symmetric physical law. Pick the direction that matches what you actually want to predict.

Lock this in. The cost \( J(w,b) = \frac{1}{m}\sum(\hat{y}-y)^2 \) you just built by hand is exactly the hill that Module 4 — Gradient Descent rolls downhill to find the best \(w\) and \(b\). The normal equation jumps to the bottom in one leap; gradient descent walks there step by step — and is the only option once the dataset is too big to invert. Same cost, same minimum, two ways down the same hill. And this whole \( \hat{\mathbf{y}} = \mathbf{X}\mathbf{w} \) primitive returns as the literal last layer of the neural networks in Module 10.
